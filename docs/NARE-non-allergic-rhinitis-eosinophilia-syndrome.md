## 1. Definition and Distinction from Allergic Rhinitis

### 1.1 What NARE/NARES Is

Non-Allergic Rhinitis with Eosinophilia Syndrome (NARES, also abbreviated NARE) is a clinical syndrome characterized by **chronic eosinophilic inflammation of the nasal mucosa in the complete absence of IgE-mediated allergic sensitization**. First described by Jacobs et al. in 1981, it represents a distinct immunopathological entity:

- **Negative skin prick tests** (SPT) to all standard aeroallergen panels
- **Negative serum-specific IgE** (sIgE) to relevant allergens
- **Normal total serum IgE** (in most cases)
- **No evidence of systemic atopy**
- Yet **nasal cytology reveals prominent eosinophilic infiltration** (>5-25% eosinophils, depending on diagnostic threshold used)

The defining paradox: the nasal mucosa is flooded with eosinophils -- cells classically associated with allergic/Th2 responses -- yet every conventional allergy test returns negative. This creates a diagnostic blind spot.

### 1.2 Prevalence

NARES accounts for a substantial fraction of chronic rhinitis:

| Population | NARES Prevalence |
|---|---|
| All chronic rhinitis cases | **15-33%** (various estimates) |
| Non-allergic chronic rhinitis specifically | **~26%** (21/81 patients in one nasal cytology study) |
| General population with rhinitis symptoms | **2-14%** |

These numbers are almost certainly underestimates. Nasal cytology -- the only definitive diagnostic tool -- is rarely performed in primary care or even general allergy practice. Most NARES patients are simply labeled as having "non-allergic rhinitis" without further characterization, or are told their "allergies resolved" and symptoms are unexplained.

### 1.3 Why NARES Is Misdiagnosed

NARES is frequently misdiagnosed or missed entirely for several reasons:

1. **Clinical mimicry of allergic rhinitis**: The symptom profile -- sneezing, profuse watery rhinorrhea, nasal congestion, and notably **anosmia/hyposmia** (loss of smell, a particularly prominent feature of NARES) -- overlaps extensively with perennial allergic rhinitis.

2. **"Post-allergy" misattribution**: Patients with prior confirmed allergy who develop NARES may be told their symptoms are "residual" or "post-allergic," when in reality a new, independent eosinophilic process has emerged.

3. **The allergy test paradox**: When skin prick tests and specific IgE come back negative, clinicians often conclude "it's not allergy, so there's no eosinophilic inflammation." This reasoning is fundamentally flawed. **Eosinophilic inflammation does not require IgE.** The innate immune system has IgE-independent pathways that produce identical tissue eosinophilia.

4. **Nasal cytology is underutilized**: The definitive diagnostic test is simple, inexpensive, and non-invasive (a nasal swab or scraping), yet it is not part of routine workup in most clinical settings.

### 1.4 Diagnostic Criteria

The diagnosis of NARES requires the conjunction of:

| Criterion | Details |
|---|---|
| **Symptoms** | Chronic rhinitis symptoms: sneezing paroxysms, watery rhinorrhea, nasal congestion, nasal/palatal pruritus. Anosmia/hyposmia is highly characteristic. |
| **Negative allergy testing** | Negative SPT and/or negative serum-specific IgE to relevant aeroallergens |
| **Nasal cytology** | Eosinophils comprising **>5-25%** of cells on nasal smear (threshold varies by center; the original Jacobs criteria used >20%; some centers use >5% or >10%) |
| **Exclusion** | No evidence of infection, structural abnormality, aspirin sensitivity (at time of diagnosis), nasal polyps (at time of diagnosis), or medication-induced rhinitis |

**Nasal cytology technique**: A sample is obtained by gentle scraping of the inferior turbinate mucosa with a plastic curette (Rhino-probe), a cytobrush, or nasal lavage. The sample is fixed, stained (May-Grunwald-Giemsa or Wright stain), and cells are counted under light microscopy. A differential cell count of at least 50-100 cells is performed, and the percentage of eosinophils is calculated.

## 2. Pathophysiology -- The Biochemistry

### 2.1 The Central Question: What Drives Eosinophilia Without IgE?

In classical allergic rhinitis, the pathway is well-characterized:

**Allergen --> DC/APC --> Th2 cells --> IL-4/IL-13 --> B cell IgE class switching --> IgE --> FcepsilonRI on mast cells --> crosslinking --> degranulation --> histamine, PGD2, LTC4 --> IL-5 from Th2 cells --> eosinophil recruitment**

In NARES, this entire adaptive immune arm is absent. No allergen-specific Th2 cells. No class-switched B cells. No specific IgE. Yet eosinophils accumulate. The answer lies in **IgE-independent, innate immune pathways** -- specifically the **epithelial alarmin/ILC2 axis**.

### 2.2 Distinguishing NARES from Local Allergic Rhinitis (Entopy)

Before discussing the innate pathway, an important distinction must be made. **Local allergic rhinitis (LAR)** -- sometimes called "entopy" -- describes a condition where:

- Systemic allergy tests (SPT, serum sIgE) are negative
- But **allergen-specific IgE is produced locally within the nasal mucosa** by tissue-resident B cells
- Nasal provocation tests with specific allergens are positive
- Local IgE can be detected in nasal lavage fluid

LAR is fundamentally an **IgE-mediated** process confined to the nasal mucosa. It is NOT the same as NARES. Research by Rondon et al. has shown that up to 40% of patients previously labeled "non-allergic rhinitis" may have LAR/entopy.

**NARES is distinct from LAR**: Studies have specifically demonstrated that NARES **is not associated with local production of specific IgE in the nasal mucosa** (Huggins & Brostoff, 2015). The eosinophilia in NARES is driven by IgE-independent mechanisms. This distinction is clinically significant because LAR may respond to allergen avoidance and immunotherapy, while NARES will not.

However, distinguishing the two requires nasal provocation testing and/or measurement of nasal-specific IgE -- tests not widely available. In practice, many "NARES" patients may have unrecognized LAR, and vice versa. The overlap zone remains an area of active research.

### 2.3 The Epithelial Alarmin / ILC2 Pathway: "Innate Th2" Immunity

The emerging model for NARES pathogenesis centers on **epithelial-derived cytokines (alarmins)** activating **group 2 innate lymphoid cells (ILC2s)**, producing eosinophil-recruiting cytokines without any involvement of adaptive immunity or IgE.

#### 2.3.1 Alarmins: TSLP, IL-25, and IL-33

The nasal epithelium acts as both a physical barrier and a sentinel immune organ. When damaged or stressed by environmental irritants (pollution, temperature changes, cigarette smoke, viral infections, ozone, particulate matter), epithelial cells release three key "alarmin" cytokines:

**Thymic Stromal Lymphopoietin (TSLP)**:
- A four-helix-bundle cytokine produced by epithelial cells, encoded by the TSLP gene on chromosome 5q22.1
- Signals through a heterodimeric receptor: **TSLPR** (TSLP receptor, also called CRLF2) and **IL-7Ralpha** (CD127)
- Downstream signaling: TSLP binding activates **JAK1** and **JAK2**, which phosphorylate **STAT3** and **STAT5**
- TSLP directly activates ILC2 cells, dendritic cells, mast cells, and basophils
- In dendritic cells, TSLP promotes Th2-polarizing antigen presentation (OX40L upregulation)
- In NARES, TSLP may be the primary driver, activating ILC2s independently of any allergen or adaptive immune signal

**IL-33**:
- A member of the IL-1 cytokine family, constitutively expressed in the nucleus of epithelial cells (stored as a preformed "nuclear alarmin")
- Released upon **cellular damage or necrosis** -- unlike conventional cytokines, IL-33 is not actively secreted but is liberated when epithelial cells are injured (passive release from necrotic cells, or active release via non-classical secretion)
- Cleaved and inactivated by caspase-1 (unlike IL-1beta, which is activated by caspase-1 -- a key regulatory difference)
- Full-length IL-33 is bioactive; proteolytic processing by neutrophil and mast cell proteases (cathepsin G, elastase, chymase) generates **more potent** truncated forms
- Signals through the receptor complex **ST2** (IL1RL1, also called IL-33R) and co-receptor **IL-1RAcP**
- ST2 is highly expressed on ILC2 cells, Th2 cells, mast cells, basophils, and eosinophils
- Downstream signaling: ST2/IL-1RAcP recruits **MyD88** adaptor --> **IRAK1/IRAK4** --> **TRAF6** --> **NF-kappaB** and **MAPK (p38, JNK, ERK)** activation
- In ILC2 cells, IL-33 is the most potent activating signal, driving rapid production of IL-5 and IL-13

**IL-25 (IL-17E)**:
- A member of the IL-17 cytokine family (the outlier -- an anti-inflammatory/Th2-promoting member of an otherwise pro-inflammatory family)
- Produced by epithelial cells, mast cells, eosinophils, basophils, and macrophages
- Signals through the receptor complex **IL-17RA/IL-17RB**
- Downstream signaling: **Act1** adaptor --> **TRAF6** --> **NF-kappaB** activation
- Activates ILC2 cells and promotes Th2 cytokine production
- Synergizes with IL-33 and TSLP in ILC2 activation

#### 2.3.2 ILC2 Cells: The Innate Th2 Effectors

**Group 2 innate lymphoid cells (ILC2s)** are the critical effector cells bridging epithelial damage to eosinophilic inflammation in NARES:

- **Lineage-negative** lymphocytes: they lack T cell receptors, B cell receptors, and classical lineage markers (CD3-, CD4-, CD8-, CD19-, CD20-)
- **Identity markers**: Lin- CD127+ CRTH2+ CD161+ (in humans); express the transcription factor **GATA-3** (the same master Th2 transcription factor, but in an innate cell)
- **Resident in mucosal tissues**: ILC2s are enriched at barrier surfaces (nasal mucosa, lungs, gut, skin) -- positioned to respond rapidly to epithelial damage
- **No antigen specificity**: Unlike Th2 cells, ILC2s have no antigen receptor. They respond to alarmins (TSLP, IL-33, IL-25) regardless of what caused the epithelial damage. This is why NARES has no specific allergic trigger.

**ILC2 activation cascade**:

1. Epithelial damage/stress releases TSLP, IL-33, IL-25
2. These alarmins bind their respective receptors on tissue-resident ILC2s
3. ILC2s rapidly proliferate and produce large quantities of:
   - **IL-5**: the master eosinophil survival, differentiation, and recruitment cytokine
   - **IL-13**: drives goblet cell metaplasia, mucus hypersecretion, epithelial barrier dysfunction
   - **IL-9**: promotes mast cell expansion (amplifying the inflammatory milieu)
   - **IL-4** (in smaller amounts): can promote local Th2 polarization if adaptive immunity is engaged
   - **Amphiregulin**: an EGFR ligand involved in tissue repair (but can promote fibrosis with chronic activation)
4. This entire process occurs **without T cells, without B cells, without IgE** -- a purely innate type 2 immune response

**The self-perpetuating loop in NARES**:
Eosinophil tissue infiltration (driven by ILC2-derived IL-5) causes epithelial damage via granule protein release --> more alarmin release --> more ILC2 activation --> more IL-5 --> more eosinophils --> more damage. This positive feedback loop explains the chronic, self-sustaining nature of NARES even in the absence of any external allergic trigger.

### 2.4 IL-5 Signaling: The Master Eosinophil Factor

IL-5 is the single most important cytokine for eosinophil biology. Its signaling pathway is central to NARES pathogenesis.

#### 2.4.1 IL-5 Receptor Structure

The IL-5 receptor is a heterodimer:
- **IL-5Ralpha** (CD125): The ligand-specific alpha chain, expressed almost exclusively on eosinophils and basophils. Encoded by IL5RA on chromosome 3p26.2.
- **betac** (CD131, common beta chain): Shared with IL-3 and GM-CSF receptors. Provides the signaling machinery. Encoded by CSF2RB on chromosome 22q12.3.

IL-5 binds IL-5Ralpha with low-moderate affinity (Kd ~100 pM); heterodimerization with betac converts this to high-affinity binding (Kd ~300 pM) and initiates signal transduction.

#### 2.4.2 Downstream Signaling

Upon IL-5 binding and IL-5Ralpha/betac heterodimerization:

1. **JAK activation**: **JAK2** (constitutively associated with IL-5Ralpha) and **JAK1** (constitutively associated with betac) are brought into proximity, undergo transphosphorylation and activation
2. **STAT phosphorylation**: Activated JAKs phosphorylate tyrosine residues on the betac intracellular domain, creating docking sites for STAT proteins:
   - **STAT5a/b**: Phosphorylated at Tyr694/699, dimerize, translocate to nucleus -- the primary transcriptional effectors. Target genes include:
     - **BCL-XL** and **MCL-1**: Anti-apoptotic Bcl-2 family members -- the molecular basis of IL-5-mediated eosinophil survival
     - **pim-1**: Serine/threonine kinase promoting cell survival
     - **CIS** and **SOCS3**: Negative feedback regulators of JAK-STAT signaling
   - **STAT3**: Phosphorylated at Tyr705, contributes to survival and activation gene programs
   - **STAT1**: Phosphorylated to a lesser extent
3. **Ras-MAPK pathway**: Shc/Grb2/SOS --> Ras --> Raf --> MEK --> **ERK1/2** --> transcription factor activation (Elk-1, c-Fos) -- promotes eosinophil activation, degranulation, and leukotriene synthesis
4. **PI3K-Akt pathway**: betac activates PI3K --> PIP3 --> Akt --> promotes survival (phosphorylates and inactivates pro-apoptotic Bad and caspase-9)

**Net effects of IL-5 on eosinophils**:
- **Eosinophilopoiesis**: Drives differentiation of eosinophil precursors (CD34+ IL-5Ralpha+) in bone marrow
- **Bone marrow release**: Stimulates release of mature eosinophils into circulation
- **Survival**: Extends eosinophil lifespan from ~3-5 days to weeks by upregulating BCL-XL and MCL-1
- **Activation/priming**: Lowers the threshold for degranulation in response to other stimuli
- **Chemotaxis enhancement**: Upregulates adhesion molecules (beta2-integrins, VLA-4) and chemokine receptor expression

### 2.5 Eotaxin/CCR3 Axis: Eosinophil Tissue Homing

While IL-5 provides the systemic eosinophil expansion and survival signal, the **eotaxin/CCR3 axis** directs eosinophils specifically to the nasal mucosa.

**The Eotaxin Family**:
- **Eotaxin-1 (CCL11)**: The prototypic eosinophil chemoattractant. Produced by epithelial cells, fibroblasts, smooth muscle cells, and endothelial cells in response to IL-4, IL-13, and TNF-alpha. Encoded on chromosome 17q21.1.
- **Eotaxin-2 (CCL24)**: Similar function, different gene (chromosome 7q11.23). Produced by epithelial cells and macrophages in response to IL-4/IL-13.
- **Eotaxin-3 (CCL26)**: Induced by IL-4 and IL-13 in epithelial cells. The most potent CCR3 agonist. Chromosome 7q11.23.

**CCR3 (CD193)**:
- The primary chemokine receptor on eosinophils, also expressed on basophils, mast cells, and Th2 cells
- A seven-transmembrane G-protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) coupled to **Galphai**
- Signaling: Eotaxin binding --> Galphai activation --> inhibition of adenylyl cyclase + activation of PLC-beta --> IP3/DAG --> Ca2+ mobilization --> cytoskeletal rearrangement --> chemotaxis
- Also activates: PI3Kgamma --> Akt --> survival; Ras-MAPK cascade --> activation
- Eotaxin/CCR3 signaling also functions as an **eosinophil survival factor** independently of IL-5, further prolonging eosinophil tissue persistence

**In NARES**: IL-13 produced by ILC2s drives epithelial and stromal cell production of eotaxins, creating a chemotactic gradient that draws circulating eosinophils into the nasal mucosa. The combination of IL-5 (survival, expansion) and eotaxins (tissue homing) explains the selective nasal eosinophil accumulation.

### 2.6 Eosinophil Tissue Damage: Granule Protein Biochemistry

Once recruited to the nasal mucosa, eosinophils release their granule contents via degranulation (exocytosis, piecemeal degranulation, or cytolysis). The four principal cationic granule proteins constitute ~90% of granule protein content and are directly responsible for tissue damage.

#### 2.6.1 Granule Architecture

The eosinophil-specific granule has a unique bipartite structure:
- **Electron-dense crystalloid core**: Contains crystallized **MBP-1** (and MBP-2)
- **Surrounding electron-lucent matrix**: Contains **ECP**, **EDN**, and **EPO**

This structural organization allows differential release: piecemeal degranulation can selectively secrete matrix proteins while retaining the core (producing "coreless" granules), or release core contents independently.

#### 2.6.2 Major Basic Protein (MBP-1/PRG2)

- **Gene**: PRG2 on chromosome 11q12
- **Molecular weight**: ~14 kDa (mature form), synthesized as a 25 kDa precursor (pro-MBP)
- **Isoelectric point**: pI ~10.9 (extremely basic, hence the name)
- **Structure**: Single polypeptide, 117 amino acids in mature form, rich in arginine (accounts for much of the basicity). The pro-form (acidic) serves as a chaperone that neutralizes the charge and prevents premature cytotoxicity during biosynthesis and transport.
- **Concentration**: ~5 ug per 10^6 eosinophils; the most abundant granule protein

**Mechanisms of tissue damage by MBP**:

1. **Direct cytotoxicity**: MBP's extreme positive charge allows electrostatic interaction with negatively charged cell membranes (phospholipid headgroups, glycosaminoglycans). At concentrations of 10-100 ug/mL (achieved in tissue deposits), MBP **disrupts lipid bilayer integrity** by inserting into the membrane, creating ion-permeable pores. This leads to calcium influx, mitochondrial dysfunction, and necrotic cell death.

2. **Epithelial desquamation**: In the nasal mucosa, MBP causes detachment of ciliated epithelial cells from the basement membrane, creating denuded epithelial surfaces -- a hallmark histological finding in NARES.

3. **M2 muscarinic receptor antagonism**: MBP acts as an **allosteric antagonist of M2 muscarinic acetylcholine receptors** on parasympathetic nerve endings and smooth muscle. M2 receptors are autoinhibitory -- they normally limit acetylcholine (ACh) release via negative feedback. MBP blockade of M2 receptors removes this brake:
   - **Result**: Unopposed parasympathetic activity --> excessive ACh release --> stimulation of M3 muscarinic receptors on submucosal glands and blood vessels --> **profuse watery rhinorrhea** and **vasodilation/congestion**
   - This is a major contributor to the hyperreactivity seen in NARES

4. **Mast cell and basophil activation**: MBP triggers histamine release from mast cells and basophils in an **IgE-independent** manner (direct membrane perturbation), amplifying the inflammatory response even without allergen-specific IgE.

5. **Complement activation**: MBP activates the classical and alternative complement pathways, generating C3a and C5a anaphylatoxins.

#### 2.6.3 Eosinophil Cationic Protein (ECP/RNASE3)

- **Gene**: RNASE3 on chromosome 14q11.2 (a member of the RNase A superfamily)
- **Molecular weight**: 16-21 kDa (glycosylation-dependent heterogeneity)
- **Isoelectric point**: pI ~10.8
- **Enzymatic activity**: RNase activity (but ~100-fold less potent than EDN); the cytotoxic function is largely independent of RNase activity

**Mechanisms of tissue damage by ECP**:

1. **Membrane pore formation**: ECP creates voltage-insensitive, non-selective ion channels in lipid bilayers. The mechanism involves:
   - Electrostatic binding to membrane surface via cationic residues
   - Insertion of hydrophobic domains into the lipid bilayer
   - Oligomerization to form transmembrane pores (similar in concept to perforin or complement MAC)
   - This renders membranes **permeable to ions and small molecules**, leading to osmotic lysis

2. **Cytotoxicity breadth**: ECP is toxic to epithelial cells, neurons, cardiomyocytes, and airway smooth muscle cells. In the nasal mucosa, it contributes to epithelial barrier disruption.

3. **Neurotoxicity**: ECP (like EDN) causes the **Gordon phenomenon** when injected intrathecally in rabbits (cerebellar dysfunction). This neurotoxic activity may contribute to sensory nerve dysfunction in NARES.

4. **Antibacterial activity**: ECP can penetrate bacterial membranes and aggregate lipopolysaccharides. This innate defense function may be part of the evolutionary rationale for eosinophil granule proteins.

5. **Clinical utility as a biomarker**: Serum ECP levels correlate with eosinophil activation status (not just eosinophil count) and can be used to monitor NARES disease activity.

#### 2.6.4 Eosinophil Peroxidase (EPO/EPX)

- **Gene**: EPX on chromosome 17q23.1
- **Molecular weight**: ~71 kDa heterodimer (heavy chain ~50 kDa + light chain ~15 kDa)
- **Prosthetic group**: Heme (iron protoporphyrin IX), covalently bound -- distinguishes it from myeloperoxidase (MPO) of neutrophils
- **Substrate**: H2O2 + halide ions (Br-, I-, SCN-; preferentially Br- in physiological conditions, unlike MPO which prefers Cl-)

**Mechanisms of tissue damage by EPO**:

1. **Oxidative halogenation**: EPO catalyzes:
   - H2O2 + Br- --> **HOBr** (hypobromous acid)
   - H2O2 + SCN- --> **HOSCN** (hypothiocyanous acid)
   - These hypohalous acids are potent oxidants that:
     - Oxidize thiol groups on proteins (disrupting enzyme function)
     - Chlorinate/brominate tyrosine residues on proteins (3-bromotyrosine is a specific biomarker of eosinophil-mediated oxidative damage)
     - Oxidize lipids (lipid peroxidation --> membrane damage)
     - Damage DNA (8-bromoadenine, 8-bromoguanine formation)

2. **Reactive nitrogen species**: EPO can oxidize nitrite (NO2-) to form nitrogen dioxide radical (NO2*), and can generate **tyrosyl radicals** that nitrate proteins (3-nitrotyrosine formation). This links eosinophil activation to nitrosative stress.

3. **Tissue oxidative stress amplification**: The EPO/H2O2/halide system generates sustained oxidative stress in the nasal mucosa, consuming local antioxidant reserves (glutathione, ascorbate, alpha-tocopherol) and contributing to the nutrient depletion discussed in Section 4.

4. **3-Bromotyrosine as a biomarker**: Because eosinophil peroxidase preferentially brominates (vs. neutrophil MPO which chlorinates), tissue or fluid levels of **3-bromotyrosine** serve as a specific marker of eosinophil-mediated oxidative damage, distinguishing eosinophilic from neutrophilic inflammation.

#### 2.6.5 Eosinophil-Derived Neurotoxin (EDN/RNASE2)

- **Gene**: RNASE2 on chromosome 14q11.2 (paralog of ECP, ~67% sequence identity)
- **Molecular weight**: ~18.6 kDa
- **Isoelectric point**: pI ~8.9 (less basic than MBP or ECP)
- **Enzymatic activity**: Potent RNase (~100-fold more active than ECP); this is its primary functional modality

**Mechanisms of action**:

1. **Antiviral defense**: EDN's RNase activity degrades single-stranded RNA, providing innate defense against respiratory RNA viruses (RSV, influenza). This is likely the evolutionary origin of eosinophil tissue residency in respiratory mucosa.

2. **Neurotoxicity**: EDN causes the Gordon phenomenon (cerebellar Purkinje cell degeneration). In the nasal mucosa, EDN may contribute to sensory nerve irritation and dysfunction.

3. **TLR2 activation**: EDN has been shown to activate dendritic cells via **TLR2** signaling, promoting Th2 polarization. This creates a potential link between innate eosinophilic inflammation and eventual adaptive Th2 immunity -- possibly explaining why some NARES patients may eventually develop allergic sensitization.

4. **Relatively less cytotoxic**: Compared to MBP and ECP, EDN has less direct membrane-damaging cytotoxicity, consistent with its lower pI and reduced membrane-binding capacity.

### 2.7 Neurogenic Inflammation Component

A distinctive feature of NARES is the prominent **neurogenic inflammation** driven by eosinophil-mediated nerve sensitization. This explains the disproportionate rhinorrhea and sneezing often reported by NARES patients.

#### 2.7.1 Eosinophil-Nerve Interactions

The nasal mucosa is densely innervated by **C-fiber sensory neurons** (unmyelinated polymodal nociceptors) expressing:
- **TRPV1** (transient receptor potential vanilloid 1): the capsaicin/heat receptor
- **TRPA1** (transient receptor potential ankyrin 1): irritant receptor
- Neuropeptide content: **Substance P** (SP), **Calcitonin Gene-Related Peptide** (CGRP), **Neurokinin A** (NKA)

Eosinophil granule proteins and mediators sensitize these sensory nerves through multiple mechanisms:

1. **TRPV1 upregulation**: MBP and EPO-derived oxidants upregulate TRPV1 expression and lower its activation threshold on sensory C-fibers. This means normally sub-threshold stimuli (temperature changes, mild irritants, perfumes) now trigger nerve firing -- explaining the **nasal hyperreactivity** characteristic of NARES.

2. **Nerve growth factor (NGF)**: Eosinophils produce NGF, which binds **TrkA** receptors on sensory neurons, promoting:
   - TRPV1 phosphorylation (via PI3K and PKC pathways) --> reduced activation threshold
   - Increased neuropeptide synthesis (upregulated preprotachykinin and CGRP gene transcription)
   - Nerve fiber sprouting (neuronal plasticity) --> increased sensory innervation density

3. **Direct nerve membrane damage**: MBP's membrane-disrupting properties can directly depolarize nerve endings, triggering neuropeptide release.

#### 2.7.2 The Neurogenic Inflammatory Cascade

Once sensory C-fibers are activated (by eosinophil products, irritants, or temperature):

1. **Orthodromic signaling** (afferent): Action potentials travel centrally to the brainstem --> triggers sneeze reflex and parasympathetic activation
2. **Axon reflex / antidromic signaling** (local): Action potentials spread antidromically along collateral nerve branches, causing local neuropeptide release from nerve terminals:

**Substance P (SP)**:
- Binds **NK1R** (neurokinin-1 receptor) on:
  - Endothelial cells --> vasodilation, plasma extravasation (via NO release and gap junction opening)
  - Submucosal glands --> mucus secretion
  - Mast cells --> IgE-independent degranulation (amplification)
  - Immune cells --> pro-inflammatory cytokine production

**Calcitonin Gene-Related Peptide (CGRP)**:
- Binds **CRLR/RAMP1** receptor complex on:
  - Arteriolar smooth muscle --> potent vasodilation (via cAMP-mediated relaxation)
  - Immune cells --> modulation of cytokine production

**Neurokinin A (NKA)**:
- Binds **NK2R** on:
  - Submucosal glands --> mucus hypersecretion
  - Blood vessels --> vasodilation

3. **Parasympathetic hyperreactivity**: MBP-mediated M2 receptor blockade (Section 2.6.2) combines with centrally-mediated parasympathetic reflex activation to produce **unopposed cholinergic drive**:
   - Excessive ACh release at parasympathetic nerve terminals
   - Stimulation of **M3 receptors** on submucosal glands --> profuse watery rhinorrhea
   - Stimulation of **M3 receptors** on blood vessels --> vasodilation and congestion

This neurogenic component explains why NARES patients often describe symptoms triggered by non-specific irritants (strong odors, cold air, changes in humidity, spicy foods) -- the sensitized nerves respond to stimuli that would not affect a normal nasal mucosa.

### 2.8 Prostaglandin and Leukotriene Involvement

Eosinophils are significant producers of lipid mediators that amplify and perpetuate nasal inflammation.

#### 2.8.1 Prostaglandin D2 (PGD2) Positive Feedback Loop

Eosinophils express COX-2 and produce **PGD2** from arachidonic acid. PGD2 acts on two receptors:

- **DP1** (PTGDR): Coupled to Gs --> cAMP elevation. Primarily causes vasodilation and has some anti-inflammatory effects.
- **DP2/CRTH2** (GPR44, prostaglandin D2 receptor 2): **The critical receptor for eosinophil biology.** Coupled to Gi --> cAMP reduction and calcium mobilization.

**CRTH2 signaling on eosinophils**:
- Chemotaxis: PGD2/CRTH2 is a potent eosinophil chemoattractant (comparable to eotaxin)
- Activation: Promotes eosinophil degranulation and superoxide generation
- Survival: Inhibits eosinophil apoptosis
- Adhesion: Upregulates CD11b (Mac-1) integrin expression

**The positive feedback loop**:
Eosinophils produce PGD2 --> PGD2 activates CRTH2 on other eosinophils --> more eosinophil recruitment and activation --> more PGD2 production --> amplification

CRTH2 is also expressed on:
- **ILC2 cells**: PGD2 is a potent ILC2 activator, synergizing with IL-33 to drive IL-5/IL-13 production
- **Th2 cells**: Promoting type 2 cytokine secretion
- **Basophils**: Enhancing histamine release

This PGD2/CRTH2 axis creates a self-amplifying circuit: ILC2 activation --> eosinophil recruitment --> PGD2 production --> more ILC2 activation.

#### 2.8.2 Cysteinyl Leukotrienes (CysLTs)

Eosinophils are major producers of **cysteinyl leukotrienes** via the 5-lipoxygenase (5-LOX) pathway:

**Synthesis pathway**:
1. **cPLA2** releases arachidonic acid from membrane phospholipids
2. **5-LOX** (ALOX5), assisted by **FLAP** (5-lipoxygenase-activating protein, an integral ER membrane protein), converts AA to **5-HPETE** then to **LTA4** (leukotriene A4, an unstable epoxide)
3. **LTC4 synthase** (a glutathione S-transferase) conjugates LTA4 with glutathione to form **LTC4**
4. LTC4 is exported by MRP1 (ABCC1) transporter
5. **gamma-glutamyl transferase** cleaves the glutamate residue: LTC4 --> **LTD4**
6. **Dipeptidase** cleaves the glycine residue: LTD4 --> **LTE4**
7. LTC4, LTD4, and LTE4 collectively = cysteinyl leukotrienes

**CysLT receptors and effects in nasal mucosa**:

| Receptor | Ligand Preference | Effects |
|---|---|---|
| **CysLT1** | LTD4 >> LTC4 > LTE4 | Bronchoconstriction (in airways), vascular permeability increase, mucus secretion, eosinophil recruitment, smooth muscle hypertrophy |
| **CysLT2** | LTC4 = LTD4 >> LTE4 | Vascular permeability, endothelial activation, fibrosis |
| **GPR99** (CysLT3) | LTE4 (selective) | Mucus secretion, mast cell activation, prostaglandin production |

In the nasal mucosa, CysLTs drive:
- **Nasal congestion**: Via vasodilation and increased vascular permeability (sinusoidal engorgement)
- **Mucus hypersecretion**: Direct stimulation of submucosal glands and goblet cells
- **Eosinophil recruitment**: CysLT1 signaling promotes eosinophil chemotaxis and adhesion
- **Edema**: Plasma extravasation through post-capillary venules

**Note on glutathione consumption**: LTC4 synthesis consumes **reduced glutathione (GSH)** -- one molecule of GSH per molecule of LTC4 produced. In the setting of chronic eosinophilic inflammation with continuous CysLT production, this represents a significant drain on local glutathione reserves, contributing to oxidative stress and antioxidant depletion.

### 2.9 Epithelial Barrier Dysfunction

Chronic eosinophilic inflammation in NARES causes progressive disruption of the nasal epithelial barrier, which in turn perpetuates inflammation.

#### 2.9.1 IL-13-Mediated Tight Junction Disruption

IL-13 (produced by ILC2 cells in NARES) directly impairs epithelial barrier function:

**Tight junction protein changes**:
- **Claudin-1**: Downregulated by IL-13 via STAT6 signaling. Claudin-1 is a "sealing" claudin that reduces paracellular permeability.
- **Claudin-2**: Upregulated by IL-13. Claudin-2 is a "pore-forming" claudin that creates paracellular cation channels, increasing permeability.
- **Claudin-4**: Downregulated by IL-13 in some tissues.
- **Occludin**: Redistributed from tight junctions to intracellular compartments by IL-13 signaling. Occludin phosphorylation at specific residues (mediated by MAPK and PKC pathways activated by IL-13) promotes its endocytic internalization.
- **ZO-1** (zonula occludens-1): Displaced from tight junction complexes.

**The signaling pathway**:
IL-13 --> IL-4Ralpha/IL-13Ralpha1 --> **JAK1/TYK2** --> **STAT6** phosphorylation (Tyr641) --> STAT6 dimerization --> nuclear translocation --> transcriptional reprogramming of tight junction genes

**Functional consequence**: The epithelial barrier becomes "leaky," allowing luminal antigens, irritants, and pathogens to penetrate the subepithelial space. This provides:
- More alarmin release (epithelial stress from subepithelial antigen contact)
- More eosinophil activation (direct contact with luminal substances)
- Potential for secondary sensitization (adaptive immune engagement with penetrating antigens -- a possible route from NARES to eventual allergic rhinitis or LAR)

#### 2.9.2 Goblet Cell Metaplasia and Mucus Hypersecretion

IL-13 is the master driver of **goblet cell metaplasia** in airway and nasal epithelium:

**Mechanism**:
- IL-13/STAT6 signaling upregulates the transcription factor **SPDEF** (SAM-pointed domain-containing ETS transcription factor)
- SPDEF cooperates with **FOXA3** to drive differentiation of ciliated epithelial cells and Clara-like cells into mucus-producing goblet cells
- IL-13 simultaneously suppresses **FOXA2**, a transcription factor that maintains the normal ciliated epithelial phenotype and inhibits goblet cell differentiation

**MUC5AC upregulation**:
- IL-13 directly induces **MUC5AC** gene transcription (the gel-forming mucin that constitutes the viscous component of airway/nasal mucus) via STAT6 and downstream activation of the **CLCA1** (calcium-activated chloride channel regulator 1) pathway
- **MUC5B** expression may also be altered, changing the biophysical properties of the mucus layer
- The net result: **thicker, more viscous, more copious nasal mucus** -- contributing to the sensation of nasal obstruction and post-nasal drip

**Mucociliary clearance failure**:
- Goblet cell metaplasia replaces ciliated cells --> reduced ciliary density
- MBP is directly ciliotoxic (disrupts dynein motor proteins in cilia)
- Altered mucus rheology (more viscous) impairs ciliary transport
- The combination of more mucus, fewer cilia, and impaired ciliary function creates **mucociliary stasis** --> mucus retention --> secondary infection risk --> further inflammation

## 3. Relationship to the Aspirin Triad / N-ERD

### 3.1 NARES as a Potential Precursor State

NARES is increasingly recognized as a potential early stage in the progression toward **NSAID-Exacerbated Respiratory Disease (N-ERD)**, historically called **Samter's Triad** or the **Aspirin Triad**:

**Samter's Triad components**:
1. Chronic rhinosinusitis with **nasal polyps** (CRSwNP)
2. **Asthma** (often moderate-to-severe, eosinophilic)
3. **Aspirin/NSAID sensitivity** (respiratory reactions to COX-1 inhibitors)

The typical clinical progression follows a characteristic sequence:
```
NARES (eosinophilic rhinitis, no polyps)
    |
    v  [years]
Nasal polyps develop (CRSwNP)
    |
    v  [years]
Lower airway involvement (eosinophilic asthma)
    |
    v  [variable timing]
NSAID sensitivity emerges
```

Not all NARES patients progress to N-ERD, but NARES represents a **risk state** for this progression. The shared pathological feature is **chronic eosinophilic type 2 inflammation of respiratory mucosa** -- the same ILC2/IL-5/eosinophil axis drives both conditions.

### 3.2 The Arachidonic Acid Shunting Mechanism in N-ERD

The biochemical basis of NSAID sensitivity in N-ERD involves a fundamental dysregulation of arachidonic acid metabolism:

#### 3.2.1 Normal Arachidonic Acid Metabolism

Under normal conditions, arachidonic acid released by cPLA2 is metabolized by two major enzymatic pathways:

```
                    Arachidonic Acid
                   /                \
            COX-1/2                5-LOX/FLAP
           /       \                    |
        PGH2      TXA2               LTA4
       /    \                        /    \
    PGE2   PGD2                  LTB4    LTC4
    PGI2   PGF2a                         |
                                       LTD4
                                         |
                                       LTE4
```

**PGE2** (prostaglandin E2), produced constitutively by COX-1, exerts **anti-inflammatory braking functions** via the **EP2** receptor on mast cells and eosinophils:
- EP2 (coupled to Gs) --> cAMP elevation --> PKA activation --> inhibition of 5-LOX translocation to the nuclear membrane --> **reduced leukotriene synthesis**
- EP2 signaling also inhibits mast cell degranulation and eosinophil activation

#### 3.2.2 The N-ERD Biochemical Defect

In N-ERD patients, even before NSAID exposure, there is a **baseline dysregulation**:

1. **Reduced COX-2 expression** in nasal polyp tissue (paradoxically, given the inflammatory environment)
2. **Reduced PGE2 production** --> loss of the EP2-mediated anti-inflammatory brake
3. **Upregulated 5-LOX and LTC4 synthase expression** in eosinophils and mast cells
4. **Overproduction of CysLTs** at baseline (urinary LTE4 levels are 3-5 fold elevated in N-ERD patients even when not exposed to NSAIDs)

#### 3.2.3 The NSAID Trigger

When a COX-1 inhibitor (aspirin, ibuprofen, naproxen, etc.) is administered:

1. **COX-1 inhibition** --> acute loss of residual PGE2 production
2. **Complete removal of the EP2 brake** --> 5-LOX is disinhibited
3. **Arachidonic acid shunting**: Without COX as a metabolic outlet, available arachidonic acid is preferentially shunted into the **5-LOX pathway**
4. **Massive CysLT surge**: LTC4/LTD4/LTE4 production increases dramatically (up to 10-fold over already-elevated baseline)
5. **Acute clinical reaction**: Nasal congestion, rhinorrhea, bronchospasm, and in severe cases anaphylactoid reactions

The reaction is **not immunologically mediated** (not IgE-dependent) -- it is a **pharmacological/biochemical** phenomenon resulting from the loss of COX-1-derived PGE2 inhibitory tone on an already-overactive 5-LOX pathway.

### 3.3 Why NARES Predisposes to N-ERD

The connection between NARES and eventual N-ERD development involves several biochemical links:

1. **Chronic IL-13 exposure** (from ILC2s) upregulates **LTC4 synthase** expression in eosinophils and mast cells, priming the tissue for CysLT overproduction
2. **Eosinophil-derived PGD2** acting via CRTH2 progressively amplifies the type 2 inflammatory milieu
3. **Epithelial remodeling** driven by chronic eosinophilic inflammation (goblet cell metaplasia, basement membrane thickening, subepithelial fibrosis) creates the substrate for **polyp formation**
4. **Barrier dysfunction** allows increased penetration of environmental agents that may drive further 5-LOX pathway activation
5. **Eosinophil apoptosis** in tissue releases **Charcot-Leyden crystal protein** (galectin-10), which forms extracellular crystals that activate the **NLRP3 inflammasome** and promote further type 2 inflammation and tissue remodeling

The progression NARES --> CRSwNP --> asthma --> NSAID sensitivity typically unfolds over **years to decades**, and not all NARES patients will complete the full progression. However, awareness of this potential trajectory informs both monitoring strategy and treatment urgency.

## 4. Nutrient Depletion: The Hidden Cost of "Negative Allergy Tests"

### 4.1 The Diagnostic Gap

NARES creates a particularly insidious form of nutrient depletion because of the **diagnostic gap**:

1. Patient presents with chronic rhinitis symptoms
2. Allergy testing (SPT, specific IgE) returns **negative**
3. Clinician concludes: "It's not allergy"
4. Patient is reassured and may receive symptomatic treatment (decongestants, antihistamines) but the **underlying eosinophilic inflammation goes unrecognized and untreated**
5. Chronic eosinophilic inflammation silently consumes nutrients, generates oxidative stress, and damages the nasal mucosa for months to years

The patient may not even realize they have significant ongoing inflammation. Their perception: "My allergies are fine -- the tests were normal." The biochemical reality: eosinophils are actively degranulating in their nasal mucosa, consuming antioxidants, generating reactive oxygen/nitrogen species, and depleting cofactors.

### 4.2 Oxidative Stress and Antioxidant Consumption

The same oxidative stress pathways driven by allergic inflammation operate in NARES, but may be **more chronically sustained** because NARES lacks the seasonal fluctuation of some allergic rhinitis:

**EPO/H2O2/Br- system** (Section 2.6.4):
- Continuously generates hypobromous acid (HOBr) and other reactive oxidants
- Consumes **reduced glutathione (GSH)** as it is oxidized to GSSG by HOBr
- GSH regeneration requires **glutathione reductase** (NADPH-dependent, which requires riboflavin/B2 as FAD cofactor) and **glutathione peroxidase** (selenium-dependent)
- Net drain: **selenium**, **riboflavin (B2)**, **niacin (B3, for NADPH via pentose phosphate pathway)**, **cysteine/glycine/glutamate** (GSH precursors)

**Superoxide and hydrogen peroxide production**:
- Activated eosinophils generate superoxide via **NADPH oxidase** (NOX2 complex)
- Superoxide is dismutated to H2O2 by **SOD** (requires zinc and copper for SOD1, manganese for SOD2)
- H2O2 is detoxified by **catalase** (iron-dependent) and **glutathione peroxidase** (selenium-dependent)
- Net drain: **zinc**, **copper**, **manganese**, **iron**, **selenium**

**Lipid peroxidation chain**:
- HOBr and other oxidants initiate lipid peroxidation of membrane polyunsaturated fatty acids
- **Vitamin E (alpha-tocopherol)** is the primary chain-breaking antioxidant, oxidized to tocopheroxyl radical
- **Vitamin C (ascorbate)** regenerates vitamin E from its radical form
- **GSH** regenerates vitamin C from dehydroascorbate
- Net drain: **vitamin E**, **vitamin C**, **GSH** (and all its regeneration cofactors)

### 4.3 Cytokine-Driven Nutrient Redistribution

The type 2 cytokines active in NARES (IL-5, IL-13, IL-33) drive metabolic reprogramming:

- **Hepcidin induction**: Chronic inflammation (even localized) can increase hepatic hepcidin production via IL-6 spillover, reducing iron absorption and sequestering iron in macrophages --> functional iron deficiency
- **Tryptophan consumption**: IDO1 (indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase) is induced by inflammatory signals, shunting tryptophan into the kynurenine pathway --> reduced serotonin synthesis --> potential mood impacts
- **B6 consumption**: Pyridoxal-5-phosphate (PLP, active B6) is consumed as a cofactor in numerous inflammatory enzyme reactions (transamination, decarboxylation, tryptophan metabolism) --> depletion with chronic inflammation
- **Zinc redistribution**: Inflammatory cytokines upregulate metallothionein expression in the liver, sequestering zinc intracellularly --> reduced plasma zinc --> impaired zinc-dependent enzyme function (including SOD1, alkaline phosphatase, and delta-6-desaturase for omega-3/omega-6 metabolism)

### 4.4 Mucosal Damage and Absorption Impairment

While NARES primarily affects nasal mucosa (not gut mucosa directly), there are systemic consequences:

1. **Post-nasal drip of inflammatory mediators**: Swallowed nasal secretions containing eosinophil granule proteins and inflammatory mediators may irritate esophageal and gastric mucosa, potentially contributing to reflux and upper GI inflammation
2. **Chronic mouth breathing**: Nasal obstruction forces oral breathing, which:
   - Reduces nasal nitric oxide (NO) delivery to lungs (nasal sinuses produce NO via constitutive NOS, which normally enters the airways during nasal breathing to promote bronchodilation and antimicrobial defense)
   - Alters oral microbiome (dry mouth promotes dysbiosis)
   - Impairs sleep quality (contributing to stress-related nutrient depletion)
3. **Systemic inflammatory spillover**: While NARES is localized, chronic local inflammation releases mediators into systemic circulation (CysLTs, ECP, EDN, cytokines), contributing to low-grade systemic inflammation that can affect gut barrier function

### 4.5 The Compounding Effect

In NARES, nutrient depletion is particularly insidious because:
- It is **chronic and continuous** (no seasonal reprieve unlike some pollen-driven AR)
- It is **unrecognized** (normal allergy tests provide false reassurance)
- It is **untreated** (without a diagnosis, there is no anti-inflammatory intervention)
- It **self-amplifies**: nutrient depletion impairs antioxidant defenses --> more oxidative damage --> more nutrient consumption (vicious cycle)

## 5. Treatment Approaches

### 5.1 Intranasal Corticosteroids (First-Line Therapy)

**Agents**: Fluticasone propionate, fluticasone furoate, mometasone furoate, budesonide, ciclesonide, beclomethasone dipropionate

**Why they work in NARES (the biochemistry)**:

Intranasal corticosteroids (INCS) are effective in NARES because they target multiple nodes of the eosinophilic inflammatory cascade:

1. **Genomic mechanism (primary)**: The corticosteroid diffuses across cell membranes and binds the **glucocorticoid receptor (GR/NR3C1)** in the cytoplasm. The GR-steroid complex:
   - Dissociates from chaperone proteins (HSP90, HSP70, FKBP51)
   - Translocates to the nucleus
   - Binds **glucocorticoid response elements (GREs)** as homodimers --> **transactivation** of anti-inflammatory genes:
     - **Annexin A1** (lipocortin-1): Inhibits cPLA2, reducing arachidonic acid release
     - **IkappaBalpha**: Increased IkB synthesis sequesters NF-kappaB in cytoplasm
     - **MAPK phosphatase 1 (MKP-1/DUSP1)**: Dephosphorylates and inactivates p38 MAPK and JNK
     - **GILZ** (glucocorticoid-induced leucine zipper): Inhibits NF-kappaB and AP-1
   - Binds **negative GREs (nGREs)** --> **transrepression** of pro-inflammatory genes
   - **Tethering mechanism**: GR monomers physically interact with NF-kappaB (p65/RelA) and AP-1 (c-Jun) transcription factors, preventing them from binding their DNA targets --> suppression of cytokine, chemokine, and adhesion molecule gene transcription

2. **Effects specific to NARES pathways**:
   - **Suppression of alarmin production**: INCS reduce epithelial expression of TSLP, IL-33, and IL-25
   - **ILC2 suppression**: GR activation in ILC2 cells reduces IL-5 and IL-13 production
   - **Eosinophil apoptosis**: Corticosteroids directly induce eosinophil apoptosis by:
     - Suppressing survival factors (IL-5, GM-CSF autocrine loops)
     - Downregulating BCL-2 and MCL-1 (anti-apoptotic proteins)
     - Upregulating pro-apoptotic BIM (BCL2L11)
   - **Reduced eotaxin production**: INCS suppress epithelial and fibroblast eotaxin (CCL11, CCL24, CCL26) synthesis
   - **Reduced CysLT synthesis**: By inhibiting cPLA2 (via annexin A1), INCS reduce arachidonic acid availability for both COX and 5-LOX pathways

3. **Clinical evidence in NARES**: INCS treatment for 1 month with fluticasone propionate significantly decreases nasal Charcot-Leyden crystal (CLC) protein concentrations (a marker of eosinophil activation) and reduces inferior turbinate swelling.

**Key difference from allergic rhinitis treatment**: In AR, INCS can potentially be tapered or used seasonally. In NARES, **ongoing continuous treatment is typically required** because the underlying innate immune dysregulation persists -- there is no allergen to remove.

### 5.2 Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists

**Agent**: Montelukast (Singulair) -- a selective **CysLT1 receptor antagonist**

**Mechanism in NARES**:
- Blocks CysLT1 receptor on nasal mucosal blood vessels, glands, eosinophils, and nerve endings
- Reduces CysLT-mediated vasodilation and vascular permeability --> less congestion
- Reduces CysLT-driven mucus secretion
- Partially blocks CysLT-mediated eosinophil recruitment
- Does NOT block CysLT2 or GPR99 receptors (incomplete leukotriene blockade)

**Rationale**: Given the prominent CysLT production by eosinophils in NARES, leukotriene receptor antagonism is a logical adjunctive therapy. It is particularly useful when NARES is progressing toward N-ERD, where CysLT overproduction is a central pathological feature.

**Limitations**: Montelukast addresses one downstream effector pathway but does not address the upstream ILC2/IL-5/eosinophil recruitment axis. It is adjunctive, not standalone therapy.

### 5.3 Anti-IL-5 Biologics (Severe/Refractory Cases)

**Agents**:
- **Mepolizumab** (Nucala): Humanized IgG1 kappa monoclonal antibody that binds **soluble IL-5**, preventing it from engaging IL-5Ralpha on eosinophils
- **Reslizumab** (Cinqair): Humanized IgG4 kappa monoclonal antibody that also binds soluble IL-5
- **Benralizumab** (Fasenra): Humanized IgG1 kappa monoclonal antibody that binds **IL-5Ralpha** directly (the receptor, not the cytokine), blocking IL-5 signaling AND inducing antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC) of eosinophils via the afucosylated Fc region engaging FcgammaRIIIa on NK cells

**Mechanism**:
- Mepolizumab/reslizumab: Neutralize IL-5 --> reduced eosinophilopoiesis, reduced eosinophil release from bone marrow, reduced eosinophil survival, reduced tissue eosinophilia
- Benralizumab: Blocks IL-5Ralpha + actively depletes eosinophils via ADCC --> near-complete eosinophil depletion in blood and tissues

**Evidence in NARES**: Case reports document successful treatment of refractory NARES with mepolizumab, with resolution of nasal eosinophilia and symptom improvement. These biologics are approved for severe eosinophilic asthma, CRSwNP, and hypereosinophilic syndrome; use in isolated NARES is off-label but pathophysiologically rational.

### 5.4 Anti-TSLP Biologic (Upstream Blockade)

**Agent**: **Tezepelumab** (Tezspire) -- a human IgG2lambda monoclonal antibody that binds TSLP, preventing it from engaging the TSLPR/IL-7Ralpha complex

**Mechanism in NARES context**:
- Blocks the most upstream signal in the epithelial alarmin cascade
- Prevents TSLP-driven ILC2 activation, thereby reducing IL-5 and IL-13 production at the source
- Also blocks TSLP effects on dendritic cells, mast cells, and basophils
- Theoretically addresses the root cause of NARES more directly than anti-IL-5 (which targets one downstream effector)

**Regulatory status**: Tezepelumab is approved for severe asthma (2022) and was approved in the US for CRSwNP (2025). Use in isolated NARES would be off-label.

**Advantage over anti-IL-5**: By blocking TSLP, tezepelumab addresses both the IL-5/eosinophil arm AND the IL-13/epithelial remodeling arm of the disease. Anti-IL-5 agents primarily reduce eosinophils but may not fully address IL-13-driven mucus hypersecretion and barrier dysfunction.

### 5.5 Nasal Saline Irrigation

**Mechanism**: Hypertonic or isotonic saline nasal irrigation (e.g., neti pot, squeeze bottle):
- **Physical mucus clearance**: Mechanically removes accumulated mucus, trapped inflammatory mediators, eosinophil granule proteins, and cellular debris
- **Reduced mediator load**: Washing away ECP, MBP, CysLTs, and PGD2 from the mucosal surface interrupts local amplification loops
- **Ciliary function support**: Hydration of the mucosal surface supports ciliary beat function
- **Osmotic effects** (hypertonic): May draw fluid from edematous submucosal tissue, reducing congestion
- **Biofilm disruption**: Reduces bacterial biofilm burden on inflamed mucosa

While mechanistically simple, saline irrigation provides meaningful adjunctive benefit by reducing the local concentration of inflammatory mediators that drive the positive feedback loops described in Section 2.

### 5.6 Capsaicin Nasal Spray: TRPV1 Desensitization

**Agent**: Intranasal capsaicin (Sinus Buster, compounded preparations; civamide in some formulations)

**The TRPV1 desensitization mechanism**:

1. **Initial phase (seconds-minutes)**: Capsaicin binds and activates **TRPV1** ion channels on C-fiber sensory nerve endings in the nasal mucosa. TRPV1 is a non-selective cation channel permeable to Ca2+ and Na+. Activation causes:
   - Massive Ca2+ influx into the nerve terminal
   - Depolarization and action potential firing
   - **Acute neuropeptide release**: Substance P, CGRP, and Neurokinin A are released from sensory nerve terminals
   - **Initial symptoms**: Burning, stinging, rhinorrhea, sneezing, lacrimation (the "burn")

2. **Desensitization phase (hours-days with repeated application)**:
   - **Neuropeptide depletion**: Repeated capsaicin exposure exhausts the stored neuropeptide content of sensory nerve terminals (substance P synthesis cannot keep pace with release)
   - **TRPV1 receptor desensitization**: Prolonged/repeated TRPV1 activation leads to:
     - Ca2+-dependent dephosphorylation of TRPV1 by **calcineurin** (protein phosphatase 2B)
     - TRPV1 internalization and degradation
     - Reduced TRPV1 expression on the nerve terminal surface
   - **Nerve terminal retraction**: High intracellular Ca2+ activates **calpain** proteases that degrade cytoskeletal elements, causing sensory nerve terminal retraction/degeneration (reversible over weeks-months)

3. **Therapeutic window (weeks-months)**: After desensitization:
   - Sensory C-fibers are functionally silenced (depleted of neuropeptides, reduced TRPV1)
   - Neurogenic inflammation is abolished (no SP/CGRP release)
   - Nasal hyperreactivity is markedly reduced
   - The refractory period lasts several months before nerve terminal regeneration and neuropeptide resynthesis restore sensitivity

**Clinical evidence**: Capsaicin treatment reduces TRPV1 overexpression and nasal hyperreactivity in patients with idiopathic rhinitis. It is particularly effective for the neurogenic component of NARES (rhinorrhea triggered by irritants, temperature changes, strong odors).

**Protocol**: Typically 5 applications of capsaicin spray to each nostril at 1-hour intervals in a single treatment session, or daily application for 1-2 weeks. Effects persist for 3-9 months.

### 5.7 Nutritional Support

Because NARES drives the same oxidative stress and nutrient consumption pathways as allergic eosinophilic inflammation (Section 4), the same nutritional support framework applies -- but with the understanding that it must be **ongoing and sustained** rather than a recovery course:

**Antioxidant defense replenishment**:
- **Vitamin C** (ascorbate): Regenerates vitamin E, scavenges aqueous-phase oxidants, supports epithelial barrier integrity
- **Vitamin E** (alpha-tocopherol): Chain-breaking lipid peroxidation antioxidant
- **Selenium**: GPx cofactor (selenocysteine in active site); critical for H2O2 and lipid hydroperoxide detoxification
- **N-acetylcysteine (NAC)**: Provides cysteine for GSH synthesis; also has direct mucolytic activity (breaks disulfide bonds in mucin glycoproteins), providing dual benefit in NARES

**Anti-inflammatory cofactors**:
- **Omega-3 fatty acids** (EPA/DHA): Compete with arachidonic acid as COX/LOX substrates; generate pro-resolving mediators (resolvins, protectins, maresins); reduce eosinophil activation
- **Vitamin D**: Regulates innate and adaptive immunity; deficiency is associated with increased ILC2 activity and eosinophilic inflammation; VDR activation promotes regulatory T cell function and suppresses type 2 cytokine production
- **Zinc**: Cofactor for SOD1, supports epithelial barrier integrity (tight junction assembly), modulates NF-kappaB signaling
- **Magnesium**: Required for glutathione synthesis, modulates neuronal excitability (may help with neurogenic component)

**B vitamin support**:
- **Riboflavin (B2)**: FAD cofactor for glutathione reductase (GSH regeneration)
- **Niacin (B3)**: NAD+/NADPH precursor for the pentose phosphate pathway (NADPH generation for glutathione reductase and NADPH oxidase)
- **Pyridoxine (B6)**: PLP cofactor consumed in inflammatory enzyme reactions; supports immune function and neurotransmitter synthesis
- **Folate (B9) and Cobalamin (B12)**: Support methylation cycle, homocysteine metabolism, and DNA synthesis required for epithelial repair

The critical distinction: in post-allergic recovery, nutritional support is a **time-limited intervention** during tissue repair. In NARES, it is an **ongoing maintenance requirement** as long as the eosinophilic inflammation persists.

## 6. Distinguishing Post-Allergy Lingering from NARE

### 6.1 The Clinical Question

A common clinical scenario: a patient had confirmed allergic rhinitis (positive SPT, specific IgE), underwent successful avoidance or immunotherapy, but continues to have rhinitis symptoms. Is this:
- **Post-allergic tissue repair** (residual inflammation resolving after allergen removal)?
- **NARES** (a new or concurrent IgE-independent eosinophilic process)?
- **Local allergic rhinitis** (entopy, with ongoing local IgE production)?

### 6.2 Distinguishing Features

| Feature | Post-Allergy Lingering | NARES |
|---|---|---|
| **Timeline** | Resolves within **4-8 weeks** of effective allergen removal/avoidance | **Persists indefinitely**; no improvement with allergen avoidance |
| **Seasonal pattern** | May retain attenuated seasonal fluctuation reflecting prior sensitization pattern | **No seasonal pattern** or **perennial** (year-round, constant) |
| **Trajectory** | **Improving** over weeks; each week slightly better than the last | **Stable or worsening** over time; no spontaneous improvement |
| **Allergy tests** | May remain positive (IgE has a long half-life on mast cells); SPT may be weakly positive | **Negative** SPT and specific IgE |
| **Nasal cytology** | Eosinophils present but **declining** over successive samples | Eosinophils **persistently elevated** (>5-25%) without decline |
| **Anosmia/hyposmia** | Usually mild, improving | Often **prominent and persistent** (a hallmark of NARES) |
| **Trigger pattern** | Known allergen-related triggers | **Non-specific triggers**: temperature changes, irritants, perfumes, humidity, exercise |
| **Response to antihistamines** | May still partially respond (residual histamine effect from mast cells still loaded with IgE) | **Poor response** to antihistamines (histamine is not the primary mediator) |
| **Response to INCS** | Good response; can be **tapered and discontinued** | Good response but **requires continuous treatment**; symptoms return on discontinuation |

### 6.4 Serum Biomarkers

| Biomarker | Utility in NARES |
|---|---|
| **Blood eosinophil count** | Often elevated (>300 cells/uL) but can be normal; correlates poorly with nasal tissue eosinophilia |
| **Serum ECP** | Elevated in active NARES; correlates with eosinophil activation state rather than just count; useful for monitoring treatment response |
| **Total serum IgE** | Usually **normal** in NARES (distinguishes from allergic rhinitis and LAR) |
| **Urinary LTE4** | May be elevated; especially useful if N-ERD progression is suspected |
| **Serum periostin** | Marker of IL-13 activity; may be elevated in NARES reflecting ILC2-derived IL-13 |
| **Fractional exhaled NO (FeNO)** | May be elevated if lower airway eosinophilic inflammation is developing (early asthma screening) |

### 6.5 The Critical Clinical Message

If rhinitis symptoms persist **beyond 8 weeks** after effective allergen removal or after negative allergy testing, and especially if accompanied by **anosmia, non-specific trigger sensitivity, and poor antihistamine response**, NARES should be actively investigated with nasal cytology. The diagnosis is straightforward once the test is ordered -- the challenge is recognizing when to order it.

Untreated NARES carries risks beyond symptom burden:
- Progressive eosinophilic mucosal remodeling
- Potential progression to nasal polyps, asthma, and N-ERD
- Chronic nutrient depletion from ongoing oxidative stress
- Neuroplastic changes in sensory innervation that become increasingly difficult to reverse

Early diagnosis and treatment with intranasal corticosteroids can interrupt these cascades and prevent progression.
