## 1. Pre-Absorptive Phase: Cephalic Phase Responses

### Overview

The cephalic phase represents the earliest stage of the hormonal cascade triggered by food. It begins before any nutrient absorption occurs -- initiated by the sensory anticipation of eating (sight, smell, taste, even thought of food). Pavlov originally coined the term "cephalic phase responses" to describe neurally mediated, anticipatory responses that precede nutrient absorption.

### Triggers

- **Visual cues**: Sight of food activates cortical and limbic regions
- **Olfactory stimuli**: Volatile food compounds stimulate olfactory receptors
- **Gustatory input**: Taste receptor activation on the tongue (sweet via T1R2/T1R3 heterodimers; umami via T1R1/T1R3)
- **Oropharyngeal mechanical stimulation**: Chewing and swallowing amplify the response
- **Conditioned stimuli**: Time of day, environmental cues, sounds associated with feeding (Pavlovian conditioning). Rats fed at consistent times develop circadian-locked anticipatory insulin secretion

### Neural Pathway

1. Sensory inputs from cranial nerves (CN I olfactory, CN VII facial, CN IX glossopharyngeal, CN X vagus) relay food-related information to the brainstem
2. Afferent fibers terminate in the **nucleus of the solitary tract (NTS)** in the medulla oblongata
3. Information is relayed to the **dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus (DMNX)**
4. Efferent vagal cholinergic fibers carry signals to target organs: pancreas, stomach, salivary glands, intestine

### Cephalic Phase Insulin Release (CPIR)

- **Duration**: Approximately the first 10 minutes of eating, before postabsorptive glucose rise
- **Mechanism**: Efferent vagal fibers release **acetylcholine (ACh)**, which binds to **muscarinic M3 receptors** on pancreatic beta-cells. This activates phospholipase C (PLC), generating inositol trisphosphate (IP3) and diacylglycerol (DAG), raising intracellular Ca2+ and priming insulin exocytosis
- **Magnitude**: Small (typically 1-3% of total meal-related insulin), but physiologically significant
- **Function**: Primes hepatic glucose uptake, suppresses hepatic glucose output, and facilitates rapid glucose clearance once nutrients arrive in the blood. Without CPIR, postprandial glucose excursions are substantially larger

### Other Cephalic Phase Responses

- **Gastric acid secretion**: Vagal stimulation of parietal cells via ACh/M3 and histamine (from ECL cells) increases HCl production
- **Pepsinogen release**: Chief cells activated by vagal ACh
- **Salivary amylase secretion**: Parasympathetic stimulation of parotid glands
- **Pancreatic polypeptide (PP) release**: Direct vagal stimulation; PP is the most reliable biomarker of cephalic phase vagal activation
- **Ghrelin modulation**: Initial brief ghrelin rise in anticipation, preparing for nutrient intake

### Dysregulation

- **Type 2 diabetes**: CPIR may be blunted or absent, contributing to impaired first-phase insulin secretion and exaggerated postprandial hyperglycemia
- **Vagotomy**: Abolishes CPIR and other cephalic phase responses, confirming vagal dependence
- **Obesity**: Altered cephalic phase responses may contribute to impaired metabolic readiness and excessive postprandial glycemic excursions
- **Conditioned overeating**: Hyperreactivity of cephalic phase responses to food cues in obesogenic environments can lead to excessive anticipatory insulin release, promoting hypoglycemia-driven hunger

## 2. Digestive Hormones

### 2.1 Ghrelin

**Producing cells**: X/A-like cells (oxyntic/fundic gland cells) in the gastric fundus

**Trigger**: Ghrelin levels peak during fasting and immediately before anticipated meals. Suppression occurs after food intake, particularly in response to nutrients reaching the duodenum. The suppression mechanism involves nutrient-induced gut hormone feedback (GLP-1, PYY, insulin) and possibly direct nutrient sensing by ghrelin-producing cells.

**Biochemical mechanism**:
- Ghrelin is a 28-amino acid peptide requiring octanoylation at Ser3 by the enzyme **ghrelin O-acyltransferase (GOAT)**, a member of the MBOAT family of membrane-bound O-acyltransferases
- Only acyl-ghrelin (AG) is active at the **growth hormone secretagogue receptor 1a (GHS-R1a)**, a G-protein-coupled receptor (GPCR)
- GHS-R1a signals through Gq/11, activating **PLC --> IP3 + DAG --> intracellular Ca2+ release --> activation of CaMKK2 --> AMPK activation** in hypothalamic neurons
- Des-acyl ghrelin (DAG) has antagonist properties: counteracts AG effects on glucose metabolism, lipolysis, and feeding

**Downstream effects**:
- Activates orexigenic **NPY/AgRP neurons** in the arcuate nucleus of the hypothalamus
- Stimulates GH release from anterior pituitary somatotrophs
- Promotes gastric motility (migrating motor complex)
- Enhances hepatic glucose output
- Stimulates appetite and food-seeking behavior via mesolimbic dopamine reward pathways

**Post-meal suppression**: Circulating ghrelin drops to nadir approximately 60-90 minutes postprandially. Carbohydrates suppress ghrelin most potently, followed by protein, then fat.

**Dysregulation**:
- **Prader-Willi syndrome**: Paradoxically elevated ghrelin drives insatiable hunger
- **Obesity**: Ghrelin suppression after meals is blunted, potentially contributing to impaired satiety
- **Anorexia nervosa**: Chronically elevated ghrelin as a compensatory drive to eat
- **Post-bariatric surgery (RYGB)**: Ghrelin levels often decrease, contributing to reduced appetite

### 2.2 Cholecystokinin (CCK)

**Producing cells**: I-cells of the duodenum and proximal jejunum

**Trigger**: Luminal presence of partially digested lipids (monoglycerides, fatty acids with chain length >=12 carbons), amino acids (particularly phenylalanine and tryptophan), and small peptides. Detected by apical microvilli of I-cells via GPR40 (free fatty acid receptor 1) and the calcium-sensing receptor (CaSR) for amino acids.

**Biochemical mechanism**:
- CCK exists in multiple bioactive forms (CCK-8, CCK-33, CCK-58) arising from post-translational processing of prepro-CCK
- Acts on two receptor subtypes:
  - **CCK-A (CCK1) receptor**: Predominantly peripheral (gallbladder, pancreas, vagal afferents) -- mediates satiety and digestive effects
  - **CCK-B (CCK2) receptor**: Predominantly central (brain) and on gastric ECL cells
- CCK-A receptor signals via Gq --> PLC --> IP3/DAG --> Ca2+ mobilization

**Downstream effects**:
- **Gallbladder contraction**: Expels bile into duodenum for lipid emulsification
- **Pancreatic enzyme secretion**: Stimulates acinar cells to release lipase, amylase, trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, and other zymogens
- **Gastric emptying delay**: Pyloric contraction slows gastric emptying, preventing duodenal overload
- **Satiety signaling**: Activates vagal afferents (CCK-A receptors on nodose ganglion neurons) --> NTS --> hypothalamus. This is one of the earliest post-ingestive satiety signals
- **Potentiates insulin secretion**: Synergizes with glucose to enhance beta-cell secretion

**Dysregulation**:
- **CCK resistance**: Chronic high-fat diets can downregulate CCK-A receptor sensitivity, reducing satiety signaling
- **Gallstone disease**: Impaired CCK signaling contributes to gallbladder stasis
- **Zollinger-Ellison syndrome**: Gastrinomas may co-secrete CCK or CCK-like peptides

### 2.3 Glucagon-Like Peptide-1 (GLP-1)

**Producing cells**: Enteroendocrine L-cells, predominantly in the distal ileum and colon, but also present in the duodenum and jejunum

**Trigger**: Arrival of nutrients (glucose, fatty acids, amino acids) in the intestinal lumen. L-cells express nutrient-sensing receptors: SGLT1 (sodium-glucose co-transporter 1) for glucose, GPR40 and GPR120 for fatty acids, CaSR and GPRC6A for amino acids. An early rapid phase (within 15 minutes) is triggered by proximal gut neural signaling and direct nutrient contact; a later sustained phase corresponds to nutrient reaching distal L-cells.

**Biochemical mechanism**:
- GLP-1(7-36)amide and GLP-1(7-37) are the bioactive forms, cleaved from proglucagon by **proprotein convertase 1/3 (PC1/3)** in L-cells
- GLP-1 acts on the **GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R)**, a class B GPCR, coupled to Gs
- Signaling cascade: GLP-1R --> Gs --> **adenylyl cyclase --> cAMP --> PKA and EPAC2 (exchange protein directly activated by cAMP)**
- In beta-cells: cAMP/PKA phosphorylates CREB and potentiates Ca2+-dependent insulin exocytosis. EPAC2 activates Rap1 GTPase, which enhances insulin granule priming
- **Half-life**: Only 1.5-2 minutes due to rapid degradation by **dipeptidyl peptidase-4 (DPP-4)**, which cleaves the N-terminal His-Ala dipeptide

**Downstream effects**:
- **Incretin effect**: Potentiates glucose-stimulated insulin secretion (GSIS) -- accounts for 50-70% of postprandial insulin release together with GIP
- **Glucagon suppression**: Inhibits alpha-cell glucagon secretion (directly and indirectly via somatostatin from delta-cells)
- **Gastric emptying delay**: "Ileal brake" mechanism
- **Beta-cell proliferation and survival**: Via PI3K/Akt and CREB-mediated gene transcription (Pdx1, IRS2)
- **Central satiety**: GLP-1R in hypothalamus and brainstem (NTS, area postrema) -- reduces food intake
- **Cardiovascular protection**: GLP-1R on cardiomyocytes activates pro-survival signaling

**Dysregulation**:
- **Type 2 diabetes**: Incretin effect is substantially reduced. GLP-1 secretion may be modestly impaired, but the beta-cell response to GLP-1 is diminished
- **Pharmacological exploitation**: GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide, tirzepatide) are major therapeutic agents for T2D and obesity
- **DPP-4 overactivity**: Accelerates GLP-1 degradation; DPP-4 inhibitors (sitagliptin, etc.) prolong endogenous GLP-1 action

### 2.4 Glucose-Dependent Insulinotropic Polypeptide (GIP)

**Producing cells**: K-cells of the duodenum and proximal jejunum

**Trigger**: Glucose and fat absorption in the proximal small intestine. K-cells sense glucose via SGLT1 and express lipid-sensing GPCRs.

**Biochemical mechanism**:
- 42-amino acid peptide derived from pro-GIP
- Acts on the **GIP receptor (GIPR)**, a class B GPCR coupled to Gs --> adenylyl cyclase --> cAMP --> PKA/EPAC2
- Also degraded by DPP-4 (half-life ~5-7 minutes)

**Downstream effects**:
- **Potentiates GSIS** in beta-cells (the other major incretin alongside GLP-1)
- **Promotes lipid storage**: Enhances lipoprotein lipase activity in adipose tissue, promotes fatty acid uptake and triglyceride synthesis
- **Bone metabolism**: GIP receptor on osteoblasts stimulates bone formation
- **Unlike GLP-1**: GIP does NOT suppress glucagon in normoglycemia; in hypoglycemia, GIP actually stimulates glucagon release (glucose-dependent bidirectional effect)

**Dysregulation**:
- **Type 2 diabetes**: The insulinotropic effect of GIP is severely blunted, more so than GLP-1
- **Obesity**: GIP may promote fat deposition; GIPR antagonism paradoxically improves metabolic outcomes in some contexts (basis for tirzepatide's dual GIP/GLP-1 agonism)

### 2.5 Peptide YY (PYY)

**Producing cells**: Enteroendocrine L-cells of the distal ileum and colon (co-secreted with GLP-1)

**Trigger**: Intraluminal nutrients (particularly lipids and short-chain fatty acids from microbial fermentation), bile salts, and glucose. Secretion begins within 15 minutes of eating (neurally mediated) and peaks at 1-2 hours.

**Biochemical mechanism**:
- PYY(1-36) is released and cleaved by DPP-4 to **PYY(3-36)**, the predominant circulating form
- PYY(3-36) is a selective agonist of the **Y2 receptor (Y2R)**, a Gi/o-coupled GPCR
- Y2R on vagal afferents and arcuate nucleus neurons: Gi --> inhibition of adenylyl cyclase --> decreased cAMP --> inhibition of NPY/AgRP neuron firing

**Downstream effects**:
- **Satiety**: Reduces food intake by inhibiting orexigenic NPY/AgRP neurons and activating anorexigenic POMC neurons in the arcuate nucleus
- **"Ileal brake"**: Slows gastric emptying and intestinal transit
- **Reduces pancreatic and gastric secretion**
- **Promotes energy expenditure** (modest effect)

**Dysregulation**:
- **Obesity**: Postprandial PYY release is blunted; exogenous PYY(3-36) reduces food intake in obese subjects
- **Post-RYGB surgery**: PYY levels are exaggerated, contributing to early satiety and weight loss

### 2.6 Secretin

**Producing cells**: S-cells of the duodenal mucosa

**Trigger**: Luminal acid (H+ from gastric acid entering duodenum), fatty acids, and bile salts. pH < 4.5 is the primary stimulus.

**Biochemical mechanism**:
- 27-amino acid peptide; acts on the **secretin receptor**, a class B GPCR coupled to Gs --> cAMP/PKA signaling in ductal epithelial cells of the pancreas

**Downstream effects**:
- **Pancreatic bicarbonate secretion**: Activates CFTR chloride channels and Cl-/HCO3- exchangers on ductal cells, producing an alkaline, bicarbonate-rich fluid that neutralizes duodenal acid
- **Augments CCK-stimulated enzyme secretion** from acinar cells
- **Inhibits gastric acid secretion**: Negative feedback to reduce further acid delivery
- **Stimulates bile flow**: Choleretic effect on hepatocytes
- **Stimulates insulin secretion**: Minor incretin-like effect

**Dysregulation**:
- **Cystic fibrosis**: CFTR dysfunction impairs secretin-mediated bicarbonate secretion, leading to acidic pancreatic secretions, enzyme auto-activation, and pancreatitis
- **Zollinger-Ellison syndrome**: Secretin paradoxically stimulates gastrin release from gastrinomas (basis of the secretin stimulation test)

### 2.7 Gastrin

**Producing cells**: G-cells of the gastric antrum and duodenum

**Trigger**: Gastric distension (mechanoreceptors), vagal stimulation (ACh via GRP -- gastrin-releasing peptide from enteric neurons), luminal amino acids and peptides (phenylalanine, tryptophan). Inhibited by somatostatin (from D-cells) when luminal pH drops below 2.

**Biochemical mechanism**:
- Exists as gastrin-17 (G-17, predominant postprandial form) and gastrin-34 (G-34)
- Acts on **CCK-B/gastrin receptors** on parietal cells and ECL cells, coupled to Gq --> PLC --> IP3/DAG --> Ca2+ release

**Downstream effects**:
- **Stimulates ECL cells** to release histamine, which then activates H2 receptors on parietal cells, driving H+/K+-ATPase (proton pump) activity and HCl secretion
- **Direct parietal cell stimulation** (via CCK-B receptors)
- **Trophic effect**: Promotes growth and maintenance of gastric oxyntic mucosa
- **Stimulates chief cell pepsinogen secretion**

**Dysregulation**:
- **Zollinger-Ellison syndrome**: Gastrinoma causes massive hypergastrinemia --> severe peptic ulceration
- **Chronic PPI use**: Achlorhydria removes negative feedback (somatostatin), causing hypergastrinemia and ECL cell hyperplasia
- **Pernicious anemia/atrophic gastritis**: Loss of parietal cells removes acid-mediated feedback, causing secondary hypergastrinemia

## 3. Insulin/Glucagon Axis

### 3.1 Insulin Signaling: Detailed Cascade

#### Trigger

Glucose is the primary secretagogue. Glucose enters beta-cells via **GLUT2** (in rodents) or **GLUT1** (in humans). Intracellular glucose is phosphorylated by **glucokinase** (hexokinase IV, the beta-cell "glucose sensor") to glucose-6-phosphate, enters glycolysis and the TCA cycle, increasing the ATP/ADP ratio. ATP closes **KATP channels** (Kir6.2/SUR1 complex), depolarizing the membrane, opening **voltage-gated L-type Ca2+ channels**, and the resulting Ca2+ influx triggers insulin granule exocytosis.

Amplifying pathways: Incretins (GLP-1, GIP) raise cAMP via Gs-coupled receptors; amino acids (leucine activates glutamate dehydrogenase; arginine directly depolarizes via cationic transport); fatty acids activate GPR40.

#### The Insulin Receptor and Downstream Signaling

**Step 1: Receptor Activation**

- Insulin binds the **insulin receptor (IR)**, a receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK) existing as a disulfide-linked (alpha2-beta2) heterotetramer
- Two IR isoforms: **IR-A** (exon 11 skipped, higher affinity for IGF-II) and **IR-B** (includes exon 11, predominant in metabolic tissues)
- Insulin binding to alpha-subunit ectodomains induces conformational change, activating the intrinsic tyrosine kinase of the beta-subunit
- **Trans-autophosphorylation** occurs at specific tyrosine residues in the activation loop (Y1158, Y1162, Y1163), juxtamembrane domain (Y960), and C-terminal tail (Y1328, Y1334)

**Step 2: IRS Recruitment and Phosphorylation**

- Phospho-Y960 creates a binding site for the **phosphotyrosine-binding (PTB) domain** of insulin receptor substrates (**IRS-1** and **IRS-2**)
- IR phosphorylates IRS proteins on multiple tyrosine residues, creating docking sites for SH2-domain-containing proteins
- **Tissue specificity**: IRS-1 plays the dominant role in skeletal muscle and adipose tissue; IRS-2 is the principal mediator in hepatocytes and pancreatic beta-cells
- **Negative regulation**: Serine/threonine phosphorylation of IRS (by JNK, IKKbeta, mTORC1/S6K1, PKCtheta) attenuates insulin signaling -- this is a major molecular mechanism of insulin resistance

**Step 3: PI3K Activation**

- Phosphotyrosine motifs on IRS (especially pY-M-X-M motifs) recruit the **p85 regulatory subunit** of class IA phosphoinositide 3-kinase (PI3K)
- p85 binding relieves its inhibition of the **p110 catalytic subunit**
- Activated PI3K phosphorylates **phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PIP2)** at the 3' position to generate **phosphatidylinositol 3,4,5-trisphosphate (PIP3)** at the inner leaflet of the plasma membrane
- **Counter-regulation**: The lipid phosphatase **PTEN** dephosphorylates PIP3 back to PIP2, terminating the signal. SHIP2 also degrades PIP3

**Step 4: Akt/PKB Activation**

- PIP3 recruits **Akt/PKB** (three isoforms: Akt1, Akt2, Akt3) to the membrane via its pleckstrin homology (PH) domain
- **PDK1** (phosphoinositide-dependent kinase 1) phosphorylates Akt at **Thr308** (in the activation loop)
- **mTORC2** (mTOR complex 2, containing Rictor) phosphorylates Akt at **Ser473** (in the hydrophobic motif)
- Dual phosphorylation yields fully active Akt
- **Akt2** is the primary isoform mediating metabolic insulin signaling

**Step 5: GLUT4 Translocation (Muscle and Adipose)**

- Akt2 phosphorylates **AS160 (TBC1D4)**, a Rab-GTPase-activating protein (Rab-GAP)
- Phosphorylation of AS160 at Thr642 inactivates its GAP activity, allowing **Rab8a, Rab10, and Rab14** to remain in the GTP-bound active state
- Active Rab GTPases promote the translocation and fusion of **GLUT4 storage vesicles (GSVs)** with the plasma membrane
- GLUT4 insertion increases glucose uptake by 10-20-fold in muscle and adipose tissue
- Additional pathway: Akt-independent signaling through **TC10** (a Rho-family GTPase activated via the CAP/Cbl/CrkII pathway from lipid rafts) also contributes to GLUT4 translocation

**Step 6: Glycogen Synthesis**

- Akt phosphorylates and **inactivates glycogen synthase kinase 3 (GSK3)** at Ser9 (GSK3beta) or Ser21 (GSK3alpha)
- GSK3 normally phosphorylates and inactivates **glycogen synthase (GS)**
- Inhibition of GSK3 relieves GS inhibition, activating it
- Simultaneously, protein phosphatase 1 (PP1), recruited by the glycogen-targeting subunit (GL or GM), dephosphorylates GS, further activating it
- Active GS catalyzes the addition of UDP-glucose to growing glycogen chains (alpha-1,4-glycosidic bonds)
- Branching enzyme creates alpha-1,6 branch points

**Step 7: Lipogenesis (Liver and Adipose)**

- Akt activates **mTORC1** (via phosphorylation and inhibition of TSC2) --> mTORC1 activates **SREBP-1c** (sterol regulatory element-binding protein 1c) processing
- SREBP-1c translocates to the nucleus and transcribes lipogenic genes:
  - **Fatty acid synthase (FASN)**
  - **Acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC)**
  - **Stearoyl-CoA desaturase 1 (SCD1)**
  - **ATP citrate lyase (ACLY)**
- Insulin also activates **ChREBP** (carbohydrate-responsive element-binding protein) in a glucose-dependent manner, synergizing with SREBP-1c
- In adipose tissue, insulin stimulates **lipoprotein lipase (LPL)** activity, promoting triglyceride uptake from circulating lipoproteins, and inhibits **hormone-sensitive lipase (HSL)** via Akt-mediated phosphorylation of PDE3B --> cAMP degradation --> PKA inactivation

**Step 8: Protein Synthesis**

- Akt --> TSC2 phosphorylation (inactivation) --> Rheb-GTP activates **mTORC1**
- mTORC1 phosphorylates **S6K1** (p70 ribosomal S6 kinase) and **4E-BP1** (eIF4E-binding protein 1)
- S6K1 phosphorylates ribosomal protein S6 and eIF4B, enhancing translation
- Phosphorylated 4E-BP1 releases **eIF4E**, enabling cap-dependent translation initiation
- Net effect: increased protein synthesis, cell growth, and proliferation

**Step 9: Gluconeogenesis Suppression (Liver)**

- Akt phosphorylates and excludes **FoxO1** (forkhead box O1) from the nucleus via 14-3-3 protein binding
- FoxO1 normally drives transcription of **PEPCK** (phosphoenolpyruvate carboxykinase) and **G6Pase** (glucose-6-phosphatase), both required for gluconeogenesis
- FoxO1 exclusion suppresses gluconeogenic gene expression

#### Insulin Signaling Dysregulation

- **Type 2 diabetes/Insulin resistance**: Chronic hyperinsulinemia leads to IRS serine phosphorylation (by JNK, IKKbeta, S6K1, PKCtheta), reducing PI3K recruitment. Lipid intermediates (diacylglycerol, ceramides) activate PKCtheta/epsilon, further impairing IRS function. This creates selective insulin resistance: metabolic pathways (glucose uptake, gluconeogenesis suppression) are impaired while mitogenic/lipogenic pathways (via Ras-MAPK and mTORC1-SREBP-1c) remain active -- explaining hyperglycemia coexisting with dyslipidemia
- **PTEN overexpression/loss**: Loss-of-function mutations in PTEN cause PTEN hamartoma tumor syndrome with enhanced insulin sensitivity. PTEN is also a tumor suppressor; its loss amplifies PI3K/Akt pro-survival signaling
- **Akt2 mutations**: Rare loss-of-function mutations cause severe insulin resistance; gain-of-function mutation (p.E17K) causes hypoglycemia

### 3.2 Glucagon Signaling: Detailed Cascade

#### Trigger

Glucagon is secreted by pancreatic alpha-cells in response to:
- **Hypoglycemia**: The primary stimulus. Decreased glucose reduces alpha-cell ATP/ADP ratio, modulating KATP channel activity
- **Amino acids** (especially arginine and alanine): Direct depolarization of alpha-cells
- **Sympathetic nervous system activation**: Epinephrine/norepinephrine via beta2-adrenergic receptors on alpha-cells
- **Suppressed by**: Hyperglycemia, insulin (paracrine inhibition within islets via Akt-mediated GABA release from beta-cells), somatostatin (from delta-cells), GLP-1, zinc co-released with insulin

#### Signaling Cascade

**Step 1: Receptor Activation**

- Glucagon binds the **glucagon receptor (GCGR)**, a class B GPCR, primarily expressed on hepatocytes
- GCGR couples to **Gs** --> activation of **adenylyl cyclase**

**Step 2: cAMP/PKA Pathway**

- Adenylyl cyclase converts ATP to **cyclic AMP (cAMP)**
- cAMP binds the regulatory subunits of **protein kinase A (PKA)**, releasing and activating the catalytic subunits

**Step 3: Glycogenolysis**

- PKA phosphorylates **phosphorylase kinase** at Ser26 on its alpha and beta subunits
- Activated phosphorylase kinase phosphorylates **glycogen phosphorylase b** at Ser14, converting it to the active **phosphorylase a** form
- Phosphorylase a cleaves alpha-1,4-glycosidic bonds in glycogen, releasing **glucose-1-phosphate**
- Glucose-1-phosphate is converted to glucose-6-phosphate by **phosphoglucomutase**
- In liver (but not muscle): **glucose-6-phosphatase (G6Pase)** in the ER membrane hydrolyzes glucose-6-phosphate to free glucose, which is exported via **GLUT2**
- Simultaneously, PKA phosphorylates **glycogen synthase**, inactivating it, preventing futile glycogen synthesis

**Step 4: Gluconeogenesis Activation**

- PKA phosphorylates **CREB** (cAMP response element-binding protein) at Ser133
- Phospho-CREB recruits the coactivator **CBP/p300** and binds CRE elements in the promoter of **PGC-1alpha** (peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma coactivator 1-alpha)
- PGC-1alpha, together with **HNF-4alpha** (hepatocyte nuclear factor 4-alpha) and **FoxO1**, drives transcription of:
  - **PEPCK** (phosphoenolpyruvate carboxykinase): Converts oxaloacetate to phosphoenolpyruvate
  - **G6Pase**: Final step of gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis
  - **Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase (FBPase)**: Converts fructose-1,6-bisphosphate to fructose-6-phosphate
- PKA also activates **CRTC2** (CREB-regulated transcription coactivator 2) by promoting its dephosphorylation, allowing nuclear entry and CREB co-activation

**Step 5: Glycolysis Inhibition**

- PKA phosphorylates the bifunctional enzyme **phosphofructokinase-2/fructose-2,6-bisphosphatase (PFK-2/FBPase-2)** at Ser32
- This activates the FBPase-2 activity and inhibits PFK-2 activity, reducing **fructose-2,6-bisphosphate (F2,6BP)** levels
- Low F2,6BP removes allosteric activation of **PFK-1** (the rate-limiting glycolytic enzyme) and removes allosteric inhibition of **FBPase-1**, shifting the balance toward gluconeogenesis
- PKA phosphorylates **pyruvate kinase (PK)** at Ser12, inactivating it, further inhibiting glycolysis

**Step 6: Fatty Acid Oxidation and Ketogenesis**

- PKA phosphorylates and activates **hormone-sensitive lipase (HSL)** in adipose tissue (via sympathetic co-activation), releasing free fatty acids
- In liver: glucagon-driven reduction in **malonyl-CoA** (due to ACC phosphorylation and inactivation by AMPK, which glucagon also activates) relieves inhibition of **CPT1** (carnitine palmitoyltransferase 1)
- CPT1 transports fatty acyl-CoA into mitochondria for beta-oxidation
- Excess acetyl-CoA is shunted to **ketogenesis**: acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, and acetone via **HMG-CoA synthase** and **HMG-CoA lyase**

#### Glucagon Dysregulation

- **Type 2 diabetes**: Paradoxical hyperglucagonemia despite hyperglycemia, due to alpha-cell insulin resistance and loss of paracrine inhibition. This is a major contributor to fasting and postprandial hyperglycemia
- **Glucagonoma**: Rare alpha-cell tumor causing the "4D syndrome" -- diabetes, dermatitis (necrolytic migratory erythema), DVT, depression
- **Type 1 diabetes**: Loss of intra-islet insulin removes alpha-cell paracrine suppression; impaired counter-regulatory glucagon response to hypoglycemia leads to hypoglycemia unawareness

## 4. Post-Absorptive Nutrient Sensing

### 4.1 mTOR Pathway: Amino Acid Sensing

#### Trigger

Intracellular amino acid accumulation, particularly **leucine**, **arginine**, **glutamine**, and **methionine (via S-adenosylmethionine)**, following protein digestion and absorption.

#### Biochemical Mechanism

**Amino acid sensing at the lysosome (mTORC1 activation)**:

1. Amino acids accumulate in the lysosomal lumen (transported by SLC38A9, PAT1, and other transporters)
2. Intralysosomal amino acids are sensed by the **v-ATPase** (vacuolar H+-ATPase), which undergoes a conformational change
3. The v-ATPase activates the **Ragulator complex** (pentameric complex of LAMTOR1-5/p18, p14, MP1, C7orf59, HBXIP), which is tethered to the lysosomal membrane
4. Ragulator acts as a **guanine nucleotide exchange factor (GEF)** for **RagA/B GTPases**, converting them from GDP-bound (inactive) to GTP-bound (active) state
5. The active Rag heterodimer (**RagA/B-GTP : RagC/D-GDP**) directly binds the **Raptor** subunit of mTORC1, recruiting mTORC1 to the lysosomal surface
6. At the lysosome, mTORC1 encounters **Rheb-GTP** (Ras homolog enriched in brain), which is the direct activator of mTORC1 kinase activity

**Specific amino acid sensors**:
- **Leucine**: Sensed by **Sestrin2** (cytoplasmic; leucine binding to Sestrin2 disrupts its interaction with GATOR2, relieving GATOR1-mediated inhibition of RagA/B) and by the lysosomal sensor **SAR1B**
- **Arginine**: Sensed by **CASTOR1** (cytoplasmic arginine sensor for mTORC1) and by **SLC38A9** (lysosomal arginine sensor/transporter)
- **Methionine**: Via **SAM (S-adenosylmethionine)** sensor **SAMTOR**, which inhibits GATOR1 when SAM levels are high
- **Glutamine**: Activates mTORC1 via **Arf1 GTPase** and v-ATPase, independent of the Rag pathway

**Growth factor convergence (insulin/IGF-1)**:
- Insulin/IGF-1 --> PI3K --> Akt --> phosphorylation and inactivation of **TSC2** (tuberin) in the TSC1-TSC2-TBC1D7 complex
- TSC2 is a GAP for Rheb; inactivation of TSC2 allows Rheb to remain GTP-bound and active
- Thus amino acids (via Rag-mediated lysosomal targeting) and growth factors (via Rheb activation) converge: both are required for full mTORC1 activation

**Downstream effects of mTORC1**:
- Phosphorylates **S6K1** (Thr389) --> enhanced ribosome biogenesis and translation of 5'TOP mRNAs
- Phosphorylates **4E-BP1** (Thr37/46, then Ser65, Thr70) --> releases eIF4E for cap-dependent translation
- Activates **SREBP-1/2** processing --> lipid and cholesterol synthesis
- Phosphorylates **ULK1** (Ser757) --> inhibits autophagy initiation
- Phosphorylates **TFEB** (Ser211) --> cytoplasmic retention via 14-3-3, preventing lysosomal biogenesis gene transcription
- **Negative feedback**: S6K1 phosphorylates IRS-1 at Ser302, Ser307, Ser636/639, promoting IRS-1 degradation -- this creates mTORC1-mediated insulin resistance

#### Dysregulation

- **TSC1/TSC2 loss-of-function**: Tuberous sclerosis complex -- constitutive mTORC1 activation causes hamartomas
- **Cancer**: mTORC1 hyperactivation drives tumor growth via increased translation and metabolic reprogramming (Warburg effect)
- **Sarcopenia/aging**: Reduced mTORC1 activation after meals contributes to anabolic resistance in elderly skeletal muscle
- **Paradox of chronic activation**: Sustained mTORC1 (as in obesity/overnutrition) drives S6K1-mediated IRS-1 degradation, causing insulin resistance

### 4.2 AMPK: Energy Status Sensing

#### Trigger

AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase) is activated when the cellular **AMP:ATP ratio** rises, indicating energy deficit. This occurs during fasting, exercise, and metabolic stress. Conversely, after a nutrient-replete meal, AMPK activity decreases as ATP levels are restored.

#### Biochemical Mechanism

- AMPK is a heterotrimeric kinase: catalytic **alpha** subunit (alpha1 or alpha2), regulatory **beta** subunit (beta1 or beta2, contains glycogen-binding domain), and **gamma** subunit (gamma1, gamma2, or gamma3, contains four CBS domains that bind adenine nucleotides)
- **Activation**: AMP (and to a lesser extent ADP) binds the gamma subunit CBS domains, causing:
  1. **Allosteric activation** (~2-5-fold)
  2. **Promotion of Thr172 phosphorylation** on the alpha subunit by upstream kinases, principally **LKB1** (liver kinase B1, constitutively active tumor suppressor) and **CaMKKbeta** (Ca2+/calmodulin-dependent protein kinase kinase beta, activated by elevated intracellular Ca2+)
  3. **Protection against Thr172 dephosphorylation** by phosphatases (PP2C/PP2A)
- ATP binding to gamma subunit antagonizes all three mechanisms, suppressing AMPK in energy-replete states

**Key downstream targets of AMPK**:
- **ACC1 and ACC2** (acetyl-CoA carboxylase): Phosphorylation at Ser79 (ACC1) and Ser212 (ACC2) inactivates them, reducing malonyl-CoA, disinhibiting CPT1, promoting fatty acid oxidation, and suppressing lipogenesis
- **HMGCR** (HMG-CoA reductase): Phosphorylation inhibits cholesterol synthesis
- **TSC2**: Phosphorylation at Thr1227 and Ser1345 activates TSC2's GAP activity toward Rheb, suppressing mTORC1
- **Raptor**: Direct phosphorylation at Ser722 and Ser792 creates 14-3-3 binding sites, inhibiting mTORC1
- **GLUT4 translocation**: AMPK phosphorylates TBC1D1 (distinct from AS160/TBC1D4), promoting insulin-independent GLUT4 translocation in muscle
- **PGC-1alpha**: Phosphorylation at Thr177 and Ser538 activates mitochondrial biogenesis
- **CRTC2/TORC2**: Phosphorylation sequesters it in the cytoplasm via 14-3-3, inhibiting CREB-mediated gluconeogenic gene expression in liver (note: this opposes glucagon's action of activating CRTC2)
- **ULK1**: Phosphorylation at Ser317 and Ser777 activates autophagy (opposing mTORC1's inhibitory phosphorylation at Ser757)

**Post-meal context**: After eating, rising glucose, amino acids, and insulin all suppress AMPK through ATP restoration and Akt-mediated pathways. This allows mTORC1 to become dominant, shifting the cell from catabolic (fasting: AMPK-ON, mTORC1-OFF) to anabolic (fed: AMPK-OFF, mTORC1-ON) programs.

#### Dysregulation

- **LKB1 mutations (Peutz-Jeghers syndrome)**: Loss of AMPK activation contributes to metabolic dysregulation and tumor susceptibility
- **Metformin mechanism**: Activates AMPK (indirectly, via Complex I inhibition and rising AMP), suppresses hepatic gluconeogenesis -- first-line T2D therapy
- **AMPK gamma2 mutations**: Cause glycogen storage cardiomyopathy and Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome (constitutive AMPK activation in heart)

### 4.3 FGF21 Response

#### Trigger

**FGF21** (fibroblast growth factor 21) is primarily produced by hepatocytes in response to diverse nutritional and metabolic stresses:
- **Fasting/starvation**: PPARalpha activation by free fatty acids drives FGF21 transcription
- **Ketogenic diet**: PPARalpha-dependent
- **High carbohydrate/sugar intake**: ChREBP (carbohydrate-responsive element-binding protein) induces FGF21 in response to fructose and glucose
- **Protein restriction**: The integrated stress response via GCN2 --> eIF2alpha phosphorylation --> **ATF4** activates FGF21 transcription
- **ER stress**: IRE1alpha/XBP1 pathway
- **Mitochondrial dysfunction**: ATF4 and CHOP-mediated induction
- **Cold exposure**: ATF2-mediated induction

#### Biochemical Mechanism

- FGF21 is an atypical FGF lacking a heparan sulfate-binding domain, allowing it to function as an endocrine hormone
- Signals through **FGFR1c** (FGF receptor 1c) with the obligate co-receptor **beta-Klotho (KLB)**
- Beta-Klotho is expressed in specific target tissues: adipose tissue, liver, pancreas, and specific brain regions (suprachiasmatic nucleus, dorsal vagal complex)
- Receptor activation: FGFR1c dimerization --> tyrosine kinase activation --> **FRS2** (FGF receptor substrate 2) phosphorylation --> **Ras-MAPK (ERK1/2)** pathway activation
- In adipose tissue, FGF21 also signals via PI3K/Akt

#### Downstream Effects

- **Adipose tissue**: Enhances glucose uptake (GLUT1 upregulation), promotes adiponectin secretion, activates thermogenesis (UCP1 induction in brown/beige adipose), stimulates lipolysis
- **Liver**: Reduces triglyceride content by suppressing SREBP-1c (via reducing mTORC1 activity), decreases de novo lipogenesis, increases fatty acid oxidation and ketogenesis
- **CNS**: Reduces sugar and alcohol preference, suppresses physical activity, alters circadian behavior (via suprachiasmatic nucleus)
- **Pancreas**: May protect beta-cells from lipotoxicity and glucolipotoxicity

#### Dysregulation

- **Obesity/MASLD (MAFLD)**: Paradoxically elevated FGF21 levels, suggesting "FGF21 resistance" -- likely due to downregulation of beta-Klotho in target tissues. Analogous to leptin resistance
- **FGF21 analogs**: Under clinical development for MASH (metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis) and obesity treatment
- **Aging**: FGF21 resistance increases with age, contributing to metabolic decline

## 5. Satiety Signaling

### 5.1 Leptin

#### Trigger

Leptin (from Greek "leptos" = thin) is produced primarily by **white adipose tissue** in proportion to fat mass. Leptin secretion increases postprandially (particularly in response to insulin, which stimulates leptin gene transcription via PI3K/Akt/mTOR pathway) and is higher in the fed state. It serves as a long-term signal of energy reserves rather than an acute meal-by-meal satiety signal. Secretion follows a diurnal rhythm (peaks at night).

#### Biochemical Mechanism

- 16 kDa protein encoded by the **ob** gene (obese gene)
- Circulates bound to soluble leptin receptor and enters the CNS via a saturable transport system at the blood-brain barrier (BBB), mediated by the short form of the leptin receptor (ObRa)
- Acts primarily on the **long-form leptin receptor (ObRb/LepRb)**, a class I cytokine receptor, highly expressed in the arcuate nucleus, ventromedial hypothalamus, and dorsomedial hypothalamus

**Signaling cascade (JAK-STAT pathway)**:
1. Leptin binding to ObRb induces receptor dimerization and activation of **JAK2** (Janus kinase 2)
2. JAK2 auto-phosphorylates and phosphorylates three key tyrosine residues on ObRb:
   - **Tyr985**: Recruits **SHP-2** --> activates ERK/MAPK pathway (reproductive and developmental effects)
   - **Tyr1077**: Activates **STAT5** (role in energy balance)
   - **Tyr1138**: Recruits **STAT3**, which is phosphorylated by JAK2, dimerizes, and translocates to the nucleus
3. Nuclear pSTAT3 drives transcription of:
   - **POMC** (pro-opiomelanocortin) in POMC neurons: upregulated
   - **SOCS3** (suppressor of cytokine signaling 3): negative feedback, binds Tyr985 and JAK2 to attenuate signaling
   - **AgRP/NPY**: suppressed

**Additional leptin signaling pathways**:
- **PI3K/Akt**: IRS2-dependent, modulates neuronal excitability via KATP channel regulation
- **AMPK inhibition**: Leptin inhibits hypothalamic AMPK (via unknown mechanism), reducing food intake and increasing sympathetic nervous system outflow
- **mTOR activation**: Leptin activates hypothalamic mTOR/S6K1, which contributes to appetite suppression

#### Effects on Hypothalamic Neurons

**POMC neurons (anorexigenic)**:
- Leptin depolarizes and increases firing rate of POMC neurons in the arcuate nucleus
- POMC is cleaved by PC1/3 and PC2 to produce **alpha-MSH** (alpha-melanocyte-stimulating hormone)
- Alpha-MSH acts on **MC4R** (melanocortin-4 receptor) in the paraventricular nucleus (PVN), Gs-coupled --> increased cAMP --> reduced food intake and increased energy expenditure via sympathetic activation
- Also produces **beta-endorphin** (opioid receptor agonist)

**NPY/AgRP neurons (orexigenic)**:
- Leptin hyperpolarizes and inhibits NPY/AgRP neurons via activation of KATP channels and BK channels
- Reduces transcription and release of NPY (neuropeptide Y, acts on Y1R and Y5R to stimulate feeding) and AgRP (agouti-related peptide, inverse agonist at MC3R/MC4R that blocks alpha-MSH anorexigenic signaling)
- AgRP neurons also release **GABA**, directly inhibiting POMC neurons

#### Dysregulation

- **Congenital leptin deficiency (ob/ob)**: Extreme hyperphagia, morbid obesity, infertility, immune deficiency. Leptin replacement normalizes all phenotypes
- **Leptin receptor deficiency (db/db)**: Same phenotype, but unresponsive to exogenous leptin
- **Common obesity**: Hyperleptinemia (elevated leptin proportional to fat mass) with **leptin resistance**. Mechanisms include:
  - Impaired BBB transport (saturation of ObRa-mediated transcytosis)
  - SOCS3 upregulation (JAK2/STAT3 pathway attenuation)
  - PTP1B overexpression (tyrosine phosphatase that dephosphorylates JAK2)
  - ER stress in hypothalamic neurons
  - Hypothalamic inflammation (IKKbeta/NF-kappaB, TLR4 activation by saturated fatty acids)
- **MC4R mutations**: Most common monogenic cause of obesity (3-5% of severe obesity). Loss of MC4R signaling phenocopies leptin resistance downstream

### 5.2 Adiponectin

#### Trigger

Adiponectin is exclusively secreted by adipocytes, paradoxically at **higher levels in lean individuals** and lower levels in obesity (inverse relationship with fat mass -- opposite to leptin). It circulates in three forms: trimers (low molecular weight, LMW), hexamers (medium MW, MMW), and high-molecular-weight multimers (HMW, most biologically active). Insulin, PPARgamma agonists (thiazolidinediones), and caloric restriction increase adiponectin levels. TNF-alpha, IL-6, and oxidative stress suppress it.

#### Biochemical Mechanism

- 30 kDa protein with a collagen-like domain and globular C1q-like domain
- Acts on two receptors:
  - **AdipoR1**: Ubiquitously expressed but enriched in skeletal muscle; higher affinity for globular adiponectin
  - **AdipoR2**: Primarily expressed in liver; higher affinity for full-length adiponectin
- Both receptors are structurally unique -- seven-transmembrane proteins with **intracellular N-terminus and extracellular C-terminus** (topologically inverted compared to classical GPCRs)
- Receptor adaptor: **APPL1** (adaptor protein containing PH domain, PTB domain, and leucine zipper motif 1) binds the intracellular domain of both AdipoRs

**Downstream signaling**:
- **AMPK activation**: AdipoR1 --> APPL1 --> LKB1 recruitment --> AMPK Thr172 phosphorylation. This is the dominant pathway in muscle
- **PPARalpha activation**: AdipoR2 in liver --> increases fatty acid oxidation gene transcription (CPT1, ACOX1, LCAD)
- **p38 MAPK**: Activated by both receptors, contributes to glucose uptake
- **Ceramidase activity**: Both AdipoR1 and AdipoR2 possess intrinsic ceramidase activity, converting ceramide to sphingosine, which is phosphorylated to **sphingosine-1-phosphate (S1P)** -- this reduces ceramide-mediated insulin resistance and apoptosis

**Central (hypothalamic) effects**:
- Adiponectin enters the cerebrospinal fluid and acts on AdipoR1 in the arcuate nucleus
- Effects are glucose-state-dependent:
  - **High glucose (fed state)**: Activates AMPK in POMC neurons --> hyperpolarization --> mild orexigenic effect
  - **Low glucose (fasting state)**: Activates PI3K in POMC neurons --> depolarization --> anorexigenic effect
- Adiponectin and leptin can act synergistically to activate melanocortin (POMC) neurons while inhibiting NPY/AgRP neurons

#### Downstream Metabolic Effects

- **Insulin sensitization**: AMPK activation in muscle increases fatty acid oxidation, reduces intramyocellular lipid accumulation (which causes insulin resistance via DAG/PKCtheta)
- **Hepatoprotection**: Reduces hepatic steatosis via AMPK-mediated suppression of lipogenesis (ACC phosphorylation) and PPARalpha-driven fatty acid oxidation
- **Anti-inflammatory**: Suppresses NF-kappaB signaling, reduces TNF-alpha and IL-6 production in macrophages
- **Anti-atherogenic**: Protects endothelium, reduces monocyte adhesion, inhibits foam cell formation
- **Ceramide reduction**: Direct ceramidase activity improves insulin signaling and reduces lipotoxicity

#### Dysregulation

- **Obesity**: Hypoadiponectinemia -- reduced adiponectin production due to adipocyte hypertrophy, hypoxia, ER stress, and inflammatory cytokines. Contributes to insulin resistance, MASLD, and cardiovascular disease
- **Type 2 diabetes**: Low adiponectin is an independent risk factor and precedes disease development
- **AdipoR1/R2 downregulation**: Occurs in obesity, further reducing adiponectin sensitivity
- **Thiazolidinediones (PPARgamma agonists)**: Therapeutic benefit partly mediated by increasing adiponectin production

## 6. Cortisol Interaction

### HPA Axis: Structure and Food-Related Activation

#### Trigger

The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis governs cortisol production:

1. **Hypothalamic paraventricular nucleus (PVN)** neurons secrete **CRH** (corticotropin-releasing hormone) and **AVP** (arginine vasopressin) into the hypophyseal portal system
2. CRH binds **CRHR1** on anterior pituitary corticotrophs, activating Gs --> cAMP/PKA --> POMC transcription. POMC is cleaved by PC1/3 to produce **ACTH** (adrenocorticotropic hormone)
3. ACTH circulates to the **adrenal cortex (zona fasciculata)**, binds the **MC2R** (melanocortin-2 receptor), and activates Gs --> cAMP/PKA --> StAR (steroidogenic acute regulatory protein) --> cholesterol transport into mitochondria --> cortisol synthesis via CYP11A1 (side-chain cleavage) --> CYP17A1 --> CYP21A2 --> CYP11B1

**Cortisol follows a robust circadian rhythm** (peak at ~08:00, nadir at midnight), driven by the suprachiasmatic nucleus and HPA pulsatility. Food intake itself modulates cortisol:
- Meals, especially those high in protein, can cause a transient cortisol rise
- Hypoglycemia is a potent HPA axis activator (counter-regulatory response)
- Chronic caloric restriction or fasting elevates cortisol
- **Negative feedback**: Cortisol binds **glucocorticoid receptors (GR)** in the hypothalamus and pituitary to suppress CRH and ACTH, completing the feedback loop

### Cortisol's Effects on Nutrient Metabolism

**Cortisol acts via the glucocorticoid receptor (GR/NR3C1)**, a ligand-activated nuclear transcription factor:

1. Cortisol diffuses into cells, binds GR in the cytoplasm (displacing Hsp90 chaperones)
2. GR-cortisol complex translocates to the nucleus
3. GR homodimers bind **glucocorticoid response elements (GREs)** in target gene promoters (transactivation)
4. GR monomers also bind and sequester pro-inflammatory transcription factors (NF-kappaB, AP-1) by tethering (transrepression)

**Hepatic effects (gluconeogenesis stimulation)**:
- GR transactivates **PEPCK** and **G6Pase** genes -- the same targets that glucagon induces via CREB/PGC-1alpha, and that insulin suppresses via FoxO1 exclusion
- Cortisol also induces **tyrosine aminotransferase (TAT)** and **tryptophan oxygenase**, increasing amino acid catabolism and feeding carbon skeletons into gluconeogenesis
- Synergizes with glucagon; opposes insulin

**Skeletal muscle effects (catabolic)**:
- Induces **MuRF1** and **MAFbx/atrogin-1** (E3 ubiquitin ligases of the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway), promoting proteolysis
- Releases amino acids (especially alanine and glutamine) as gluconeogenic substrates
- Impairs insulin-stimulated glucose uptake: reduces GLUT4 translocation, decreases IRS-1 expression, and increases **p85 alpha** (PI3K regulatory subunit) expression, which acts as a dominant-negative inhibitor of PI3K signaling when in excess relative to p110

**Adipose tissue effects**:
- Acute: promotes lipolysis via HSL activation (permissive for catecholamine action)
- Chronic: promotes visceral adipogenesis (GR is more densely expressed in visceral vs. subcutaneous adipose) via induction of **lipoprotein lipase (LPL)** and adipogenesis transcription factors
- Cortisol enhances **11beta-HSD1** (11-beta-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 1) activity in visceral fat, locally converting inactive cortisone to active cortisol, creating a tissue-amplification loop
- The paradox: cortisol both promotes lipolysis (acute, systemic) and lipogenesis (chronic, visceral), explaining the Cushingoid fat redistribution pattern

**Pancreatic effects**:
- Chronic cortisol excess directly impairs beta-cell function: reduces insulin biosynthesis and glucose-stimulated insulin secretion
- May increase beta-cell apoptosis under chronic exposure

### Cortisol and Insulin Resistance: Molecular Mechanisms

1. **IRS-1 serine phosphorylation**: Cortisol upregulates serine kinases (JNK, IKKbeta) that phosphorylate IRS-1 at inhibitory serine sites, blocking PI3K recruitment
2. **GLUT4 suppression**: Reduces GLUT4 mRNA and protein expression in muscle and adipose
3. **Visceral adiposity**: Promotes visceral fat accumulation, which releases free fatty acids directly into the portal circulation, causing hepatic insulin resistance (via DAG/PKCepsilon)
4. **Adipokine dysregulation**: Visceral adipose tissue produces more TNF-alpha, IL-6, resistin (pro-inflammatory, insulin-resistant) and less adiponectin
5. **Hepatic glucose output**: Sustained gluconeogenic drive opposes insulin's hepatic actions

### Dysregulation

- **Cushing's syndrome/disease**: Chronic hypercortisolism causes diabetes (10-20% prevalence), central obesity, muscle wasting, osteoporosis, hypertension
- **Metabolic syndrome**: "Functional hypercortisolism" -- subtle HPA axis hyperactivity with normal circulating cortisol but increased tissue cortisol regeneration via 11beta-HSD1, particularly in visceral fat
- **Addison's disease**: Cortisol deficiency causes hypoglycemia (impaired gluconeogenesis), hypotension, and enhanced insulin sensitivity
- **Chronic psychological stress**: Sustained HPA activation promotes visceral fat accumulation, insulin resistance, and cardiometabolic disease
- **Bidirectional feedback**: Hyperinsulinemia directly stimulates the HPA axis, creating a "functional hypercortisolism" that antagonizes insulin's effects, establishing a vicious cycle

## 7. Thyroid Hormones

### Overview

Thyroid hormones are the principal regulators of the basal metabolic rate (BMR) and play essential roles in modulating the oxidation of all macronutrients.

### Production and Activation

**Trigger**: The hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid (HPT) axis:
1. **TRH** (thyrotropin-releasing hormone) from hypothalamic PVN neurons acts on anterior pituitary thyrotrophs
2. **TSH** (thyroid-stimulating hormone) binds the **TSHR** (TSH receptor, a Gs-coupled GPCR) on thyroid follicular cells
3. TSH activates cAMP/PKA, stimulating:
   - **Sodium-iodide symporter (NIS/SLC5A5)** expression: iodide uptake from blood
   - **Thyroid peroxidase (TPO)**: Oxidizes iodide and catalyzes iodination of tyrosine residues on **thyroglobulin** (mono- and di-iodotyrosine: MIT, DIT)
   - **Coupling**: DIT + DIT = T4 (thyroxine); MIT + DIT = T3 (triiodothyronine)
   - **Proteolysis** of thyroglobulin and T4/T3 release into circulation

**T4 is the predominant circulating form** (90%); T3 is ~4-fold more biologically active:
- **Local activation**: Type 2 deiodinase (D2, encoded by DIO2) converts T4 --> T3 in target tissues (brain, pituitary, brown adipose, skeletal muscle, thyroid)
- **Inactivation**: Type 3 deiodinase (D3, encoded by DIO3) converts T4 --> **reverse T3 (rT3)** (inactive) and T3 --> T2 (diiodothyronine)
- **Hepatic/renal clearance**: Type 1 deiodinase (D1) contributes to both activation and inactivation

### Mechanism of Action

T3 enters cells via specific transporters: **MCT8** (monocarboxylate transporter 8, SLC16A2) and **OATP1C1** in the brain, and **MCT10** in peripheral tissues.

**Genomic (classical) pathway**:
1. T3 binds intracellular **thyroid hormone receptors (TRalpha and TRbeta)**, which are ligand-activated nuclear transcription factors
   - **TRalpha1**: Predominant in heart, bone, brain, skeletal muscle -- mediates heart rate and thermogenesis
   - **TRbeta1**: Predominant in liver, kidney, thyroid -- mediates cholesterol metabolism and TSH feedback
   - **TRbeta2**: Hypothalamus and pituitary -- mediates HPT axis negative feedback
2. TR heterodimerizes with **RXR** (retinoid X receptor) and binds **thyroid hormone response elements (TREs)** in target gene promoters
3. Without T3: TR/RXR recruits corepressors (NCoR, SMRT) and HDACs, silencing transcription
4. With T3: Corepressors are displaced; coactivators (SRC-1, p300/CBP, PGC-1alpha) are recruited, activating transcription

**Non-genomic (rapid) pathway**:
- T4 and T3 bind **integrin alphaVbeta3** on the plasma membrane, activating PI3K/Akt and ERK/MAPK signaling
- T3 can rapidly activate **AMPK** in skeletal muscle
- Direct mitochondrial effects: T3 binds a truncated TRalpha1 (p43) in the mitochondrial matrix, stimulating mitochondrial genome transcription and oxidative phosphorylation

### Effects on Basal Metabolic Rate and Nutrient Oxidation

**Thermogenesis and oxygen consumption**:
- T3 upregulates **Na+/K+-ATPase** expression in nearly all tissues (accounts for 20-40% of BMR)
- Na+/K+-ATPase consumes ATP to maintain ionic gradients; the compensatory increase in mitochondrial ATP production raises oxygen consumption and heat generation
- T3 induces **UCP1** (uncoupling protein 1) in brown and beige adipose tissue (via PGC-1alpha coactivation), uncoupling the electron transport chain from ATP synthesis to generate heat
- T3 also induces **UCP3** in skeletal muscle and **ANT** (adenine nucleotide translocator), increasing mitochondrial proton leak and thermogenesis

**Carbohydrate metabolism**:
- Increases intestinal glucose absorption (upregulates SGLT1 and GLUT2)
- Enhances glucose uptake in muscle (upregulates GLUT4)
- Increases glycogenolysis (hepatic glycogen phosphorylase induction)
- Stimulates gluconeogenesis (PEPCK and G6Pase induction, synergistic with cortisol and glucagon)
- Enhances glycolysis (upregulates hexokinase, PFK-1, pyruvate kinase)
- **Net effect**: Increased glucose turnover without necessarily changing fasting glucose (both production and utilization increase in parallel)

**Lipid metabolism**:
- **Lipolysis**: T3 increases HSL and adipose triglyceride lipase (ATGL) expression, mobilizing free fatty acids
- **Fatty acid oxidation**: Induces CPT1, medium-chain acyl-CoA dehydrogenase (MCAD), and other beta-oxidation enzymes via PPARalpha coactivation
- **Cholesterol clearance**: TRbeta1 in liver drives transcription of **LDL receptor (LDLR)**, increasing LDL cholesterol clearance. Also induces **CYP7A1** (cholesterol 7-alpha-hydroxylase), the rate-limiting enzyme for bile acid synthesis from cholesterol
- **De novo lipogenesis**: T3 induces SREBP-1c, FASN, ACC, and malic enzyme (NADPH generation for fatty acid synthesis)
- **Paradox**: T3 simultaneously promotes lipogenesis and lipolysis/oxidation, but the net balance in hyperthyroidism favors fat loss (oxidation > synthesis)

**Protein metabolism**:
- Physiological T3: Promotes protein synthesis, especially in developing tissues (growth-permissive)
- Supraphysiological T3: Promotes protein catabolism via ubiquitin-proteasome system upregulation, contributing to muscle wasting in thyrotoxicosis

**Mitochondrial biogenesis**:
- T3, via PGC-1alpha and NRF1/NRF2 (nuclear respiratory factors), drives transcription of mitochondrial genes: cytochrome c, Complex I-V subunits, and **TFAM** (mitochondrial transcription factor A)
- This increases the total mitochondrial mass and oxidative capacity of the cell

### Dysregulation

- **Hypothyroidism**: Reduced BMR (may decrease by 30-40%), weight gain, hypercholesterolemia (reduced LDLR and CYP7A1), cold intolerance, fatigue, bradycardia. Impaired glucose disposal but also impaired gluconeogenesis
- **Hyperthyroidism/thyrotoxicosis**: Elevated BMR (may increase by 60-100%), weight loss despite hyperphagia, heat intolerance, tachycardia/atrial fibrillation (TRalpha1-mediated cardiac effects), hyperglycemia (excessive hepatic glucose output can cause "thyrotoxic diabetes"), osteoporosis (TRalpha1-mediated bone resorption), muscle wasting
- **Non-thyroidal illness syndrome ("sick euthyroid")**: During critical illness or starvation, T4-to-T3 conversion decreases (D2 downregulation, D3 upregulation), rT3 rises, and TSH may be low. This is an adaptive response to reduce metabolic rate during metabolic stress, but can impair recovery
- **MCT8 mutations (Allan-Herndon-Dudley syndrome)**: Impaired T3 transport into neurons causes severe intellectual disability with peripheral thyrotoxicosis
- **Resistance to thyroid hormone (RTH)**: TRbeta mutations impair T3 binding, reducing negative feedback (elevated T4/T3 with non-suppressed TSH) and causing variable tissue-specific hyper- or hypothyroid features

## Integrated Summary: The Temporal Cascade

The hormonal response to food intake follows a tightly orchestrated temporal sequence:

| Time | Phase | Key Events |
|------|-------|------------|
| -10 to 0 min | **Cephalic** | Sensory anticipation --> vagal activation --> CPIR, gastric acid, salivary amylase, ghrelin modulation |
| 0-15 min | **Gastric** | Gastric distension --> gastrin release --> HCl, pepsinogen. Early CCK from proximal gut. Ghrelin begins to fall |
| 15-60 min | **Intestinal/Absorptive** | Nutrients reach duodenum: CCK peaks (gallbladder, pancreatic enzymes), GIP rises (incretin), GLP-1 first phase, secretin (bicarbonate). Insulin rises sharply (glucose + incretins). Glucagon suppressed |
| 30-120 min | **Peak Absorption** | Blood glucose peaks and begins to fall. Insulin at maximum. GLUT4 translocation maximal. Glycogen synthesis active. mTORC1 activated by amino acids + insulin. AMPK suppressed. Lipogenesis initiated in liver |
| 1-3 hours | **Late Postprandial** | PYY peaks (distal gut nutrient arrival). GLP-1 second phase. Satiety signals dominate. FGF21 modulated. Leptin begins to rise (insulin-driven transcription). Amino acids activate mTORC1 --> protein synthesis |
| 3-6 hours | **Post-Absorptive Transition** | Glucose normalizes. Insulin falls. Glucagon begins to rise. Glycogenolysis begins. AMPK reactivates. mTORC1 activity wanes. Lipolysis resumes. Counter-regulatory hormones (cortisol, GH, catecholamines) become more prominent |
| 6+ hours | **Fasting** | Glucagon dominant. Hepatic glycogenolysis then gluconeogenesis. Fatty acid oxidation and ketogenesis. AMPK fully active. mTORC1 suppressed. Ghrelin rises. Leptin falls. FGF21 rises (PPARalpha-driven) |

Throughout this entire cascade, cortisol provides a permissive metabolic context (ensuring adequate gluconeogenic capacity and substrate availability), while thyroid hormones set the overall metabolic rate at which all these processes operate.
