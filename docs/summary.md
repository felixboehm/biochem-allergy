## 1. Eating and Hormones

*Full detail: [hormonal-biochemical-cascade-food-intake.md](hormonal-biochemical-cascade-food-intake.md)*

Food intake triggers a precisely timed hormonal cascade that governs digestion, absorption, energy storage, and satiety. Understanding this chain matters because inflammation disrupts it at multiple points.

### The Cascade in Brief

**Before any food is absorbed** (cephalic phase): The sight, smell, and taste of food trigger vagal signals that release a small "priming" dose of insulin (cephalic phase insulin release, CPIR), ramp up gastric acid, and prepare the digestive tract. This priming accounts for only 1-3% of meal insulin but is critical for smooth glucose handling.

**As food enters the gut**, a wave of hormones fires in sequence:

| Hormone | Source | Key Role |
|---------|--------|----------|
| **Ghrelin** | Stomach fundus | Hunger signal; suppressed by eating |
| **CCK** | Duodenal I-cells | Triggers bile release, pancreatic enzymes, slows gastric emptying, early satiety |
| **GLP-1** | Ileal/colonic L-cells | Potentiates insulin secretion (the "incretin effect" = 50-70% of meal insulin), suppresses glucagon, promotes satiety |
| **GIP** | Duodenal K-cells | Second incretin; promotes lipid storage |
| **PYY** | Distal gut L-cells | Late satiety signal; slows gut transit ("ileal brake") |
| **Insulin** | Pancreatic beta-cells | Drives glucose uptake (GLUT4 translocation), glycogen synthesis, lipogenesis, protein synthesis via PI3K/Akt/mTOR |
| **Glucagon** | Pancreatic alpha-cells | Counter-regulatory; mobilizes glucose from liver when blood sugar drops |

**Post-absorptive nutrient sensing** involves mTOR (responds to amino acids, especially leucine), AMPK (the cellular fuel gauge), and FGF21 (signals protein/carbohydrate balance to the brain).

**Cortisol** rises with meals and in stress, driving gluconeogenesis and protein catabolism. Chronic cortisol elevation (as in sustained allergic inflammation) wastes magnesium, B vitamins, and vitamin C, setting the stage for nutrient depletion. **Thyroid hormones** (T3/T4) set the metabolic rate; selenium-dependent deiodinases (DIO1/2/3) convert T4 to active T3.

### Why This Matters for Allergy

Chronic inflammation disrupts this cascade at every level: blunted incretin signaling, cortisol-driven nutrient wasting, impaired gut hormone release from damaged mucosa, and altered thyroid conversion from selenium depletion.

## 2. Nutrients: What the Body Needs and Why

*Full detail: [nutrient-requirements-and-inflammation.md](nutrient-requirements-and-inflammation.md)*

### The Critical Nutrients for Inflammation and Recovery

**Magnesium** -- cofactor for >600 enzymes. All ATP-dependent reactions actually use Mg-ATP as substrate. Critical roles: NMDA receptor voltage-dependent block (prevents excitotoxicity), mast cell membrane stability, glutathione synthesis, HPA axis regulation. Inflammation depletes it through cortisol-driven renal wasting. RDA 310-420 mg/day; recovery needs are higher.

**Zinc** -- cofactor for >300 enzymes and >2,000 transcription factors. Required for thymulin (T cell maturation), Cu/Zn-SOD (antioxidant defense), DAO (histamine degradation), tight junction proteins, and all matrix metalloproteinases (tissue remodeling). During inflammation, IL-6 drives zinc sequestration in the liver via metallothionein/ZIP14, creating peripheral zinc deficiency despite adequate total body stores. RDA 8-11 mg/day.

**Iron** -- essential for hemoglobin, all electron transport chain complexes (Fe-S clusters), catalase, and aconitase. The hepcidin-ferroportin axis is the master regulator: IL-6 drives hepcidin production, which degrades ferroportin, trapping iron in macrophages and blocking dietary absorption. This creates *functional* iron deficiency (low serum iron but normal/high ferritin) -- supplementing iron during active inflammation is ineffective and potentially harmful (Fenton reaction generates more ROS). RDA 8-18 mg/day.

**Selenium** -- incorporated as selenocysteine into 25 selenoproteins. The glutathione peroxidases (GPx1-4) and thioredoxin reductases (TrxR1-3) are selenium-dependent. GPx4 is uniquely critical: it reduces membrane lipid hydroperoxides and prevents ferroptosis. Chronic oxidative stress irreversibly inactivates GPx enzymes, requiring new synthesis and more selenium. RDA 55 mcg/day.

**Vitamin D** -- not just a vitamin but a steroid hormone. Active form 1,25(OH)2D suppresses NF-kB, promotes Treg differentiation (via FoxP3), enhances antimicrobial peptides (cathelicidin), and supports epithelial barrier integrity. During inflammation, immune cells express CYP27B1 and consume circulating 25(OH)D without feedback regulation, creating depletion independent of diet or sun exposure.

**B Vitamins** -- the methylation cycle (folate, B12, B6, B2) and energy production (B1, B2, B3, B5) depend on these. Inflammation increases demand through: DNA repair (PARP consuming NAD+/B3), glutathione regeneration (transsulfuration consuming B6), histamine clearance (HNMT consuming SAM which requires folate+B12), and catecholamine metabolism (COMT consuming SAM). MTHFR C677T polymorphism (affects ~40% of people) impairs folate cycling, making methylated forms essential.

**Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA)** -- precursors for ALL specialized pro-resolving mediators (SPMs): resolvins, protectins, and maresins. Without adequate EPA/DHA, the body physically cannot resolve inflammation. The omega-6:omega-3 ratio in modern diets (~20:1 vs ancestral ~1-4:1) means the inflammatory arachidonic acid pathway dominates.

### How Inflammation Changes Nutrient Requirements

During chronic inflammation, nutrient requirements are not just "higher" -- they change qualitatively:

- **Glutamine** becomes conditionally essential (primary fuel for enterocytes and immune cells)
- **Cysteine** becomes rate-limiting for glutathione synthesis
- **Glycine** becomes conditionally essential (needed for glutathione, heme, collagen, conjugation)
- Vitamin C demand triples (DAO cofactor + direct histamine degradation + adrenal steroid synthesis)
- Iron supplementation becomes *harmful* until inflammation resolves (hepcidin blocks utilization; free iron generates ROS)

## 3. Inflammation: From Acute Response to Chronic Trap

*Full detail: [inflammation-biochemistry-nutrient-cycles.md](inflammation-biochemistry-nutrient-cycles.md)*

### Acute Inflammation: The Normal Program

**NF-kB** is the master inflammatory transcription factor. Pattern recognition receptors (TLRs, NODs) or cytokine receptors activate IKK, which phosphorylates IkBa, marking it for proteasomal degradation and freeing NF-kB to enter the nucleus. There, it activates hundreds of pro-inflammatory genes: TNF-a, IL-1b, IL-6, COX-2, iNOS, adhesion molecules. Critically, NF-kB also induces its own inhibitor IkBa -- a negative feedback loop that normally resolves signaling within 60-90 minutes.

**Arachidonic acid metabolism** branches into two pro-inflammatory paths:
- **COX pathway**: AA -> PGH2 -> PGE2 (pain, fever, vasodilation), PGD2 (allergic inflammation, eosinophil recruitment via DP2/CRTH2), TXA2 (platelet aggregation)
- **5-LOX pathway**: AA -> LTA4 -> LTB4 (neutrophil chemoattractant) or cysteinyl leukotrienes LTC4/LTD4/LTE4 (bronchoconstriction 1000x more potent than histamine, mucus secretion, eosinophil recruitment)

**Resolution is active, not passive.** The same enzymes (5-LOX, 15-LOX) undergo a "lipid mediator class switch" triggered by PGE2 and PGD2 themselves, shifting from prostaglandin/leukotriene production to making pro-resolving mediators:
- **Lipoxins** (from arachidonic acid): stop neutrophil recruitment, promote efferocytosis
- **E-series resolvins** (from EPA): block neutrophil migration, reduce TNF-a/IL-1b
- **D-series resolvins** (from DHA): counter-regulate pro-inflammatory mediators
- **Protectins** (from DHA): neuroprotective, inhibit neutrophil infiltration
- **Maresins** (from DHA): stimulate tissue regeneration, switch macrophages from M1 to M2

**Failure of this lipid mediator class switch = chronic inflammation.** This is the single most important concept in understanding why people get stuck.

### When Inflammation Becomes Chronic

Chronic inflammation represents simultaneous failures:

1. **Insufficient SPM production** -- omega-3 deficiency limits resolvin/protectin/maresin synthesis
2. **Persistent NF-kB activation** -- the oscillatory pattern is replaced by sustained signaling via: continuous DAMP stimulation, A20 (the master NF-kB deubiquitinase) being epigenetically silenced, autocrine TNF-a/IL-1b feedback loops, and epigenetic chromatin remodeling that makes inflammatory gene promoters constitutively accessible
3. **NLRP3 inflammasome chronic activation** -- driven by metabolic danger signals (hyperglycemia, oxidized LDL, damaged mitochondrial DNA) that provide a constant "Signal 2"
4. **The ROS-NF-kB cycle** -- inflammation generates ROS (via iNOS, COX-2, NADPH oxidase), ROS directly activate NF-kB (oxidize IKKb Cys179, inactivate phosphatases), and NF-kB induces more ROS-generating enzymes
5. **Mitochondrial dysfunction** -- TNF-a damages Complex I/III, NO inhibits Complex IV, ROS oxidize cardiolipin -> broken TCA cycle with succinate accumulation -> HIF-1a stabilization -> more IL-1b

## 4. The Vicious Cycles That Lock Inflammation In

*Full detail: [inflammation-biochemistry-nutrient-cycles.md](inflammation-biochemistry-nutrient-cycles.md)*

These are the self-reinforcing loops that make chronic inflammation so difficult to break. Each nutrient deficiency worsens inflammation, which deepens the deficiency.

### The Magnesium Depletion Cycle

```
Inflammation -> cortisol -> renal Mg wasting -> low Mg
    -> NMDA overactivation (loss of voltage-dependent block)
    -> excessive Ca2+ influx -> excitotoxicity, substance P release
    -> mast cell degranulation -> more inflammation
    -> AND: impaired ATP production (Mg-ATP is the true kinase substrate)
    -> AND: impaired glutathione synthesis (GCL requires Mg)
    -> AND: direct NF-kB disinhibition (Mg normally stabilizes IkBa)
    -> all of which worsen inflammation -> more Mg loss
```

### The Zinc Sequestration Trap

```
IL-6 -> STAT3 -> metallothionein + ZIP14 upregulation in liver
    -> zinc sequestered in liver -> peripheral zinc deficiency
    -> impaired SOD1 (antioxidant defense drops)
    -> impaired thymulin (T cell maturation fails, Th2 dominance)
    -> impaired tight junctions (gut permeability increases)
    -> LPS translocation -> NF-kB -> more IL-6 -> more zinc sequestration
```

The paradox: zinc is trapped in metallothionein but cannot be released because MT zinc release requires reducing conditions (GSH, thioredoxin) -- which are themselves depleted by oxidative stress.

### The Tryptophan Steal

```
IFN-g + TNF-a -> IDO1 upregulation -> >95% of tryptophan diverted to kynurenine
    -> serotonin synthesis collapses -> depression, pain sensitivity
    -> melatonin synthesis collapses -> insomnia, loss of circadian antioxidant
    -> quinolinic acid accumulates -> NMDA excitotoxicity -> brain fog
    -> 3-hydroxykynurenine accumulates -> generates ROS -> more inflammation
    -> melatonin loss -> impaired Treg function -> more inflammation -> more IDO1
```

### The Iron Paradox

```
IL-6 -> hepcidin -> ferroportin degradation -> iron trapped in macrophages
    -> serum iron low, ferritin normal/high (misleading)
    -> impaired ETC (Fe-S clusters), impaired catalase, impaired DNA synthesis
    -> fatigue, impaired tissue repair
    -> if you supplement iron: it cannot be distributed (hepcidin blocks it)
       and free iron -> Fenton reaction -> hydroxyl radicals -> MORE inflammation
```

### The Glutathione Collapse

```
Chronic ROS -> GSH consumption -> GSSG accumulates
    -> cysteine depleted (rate-limiting precursor)
    -> glycine depleted (also needed for GSH + heme + collagen)
    -> Mg depleted (GCL and GS require Mg-ATP)
    -> selenium depleted (GPx enzymes irreversibly inactivated by excess ROS)
    -> NAD+ depleted (PARP consuming it for DNA repair)
    -> NADPH insufficient (needed for GSSG -> GSH recycling)
    -> antioxidant system collapses -> uncontrolled ROS -> NF-kB -> more inflammation
```

### The Master Vicious Cycle

All of these cycles interconnect. Gut mucosal damage reduces nutrient absorption surface, impairs brush border enzymes, increases intestinal permeability (LPS translocation), and shifts the microbiome toward pathobionts that produce more inflammatory signals and less beneficial B vitamins and butyrate. Breaking this requires simultaneous multi-targeted intervention -- no single nutrient correction is sufficient because each deficiency perpetuates the others.

## 5. Allergy Biochemistry: The IgE Cascade

*Full detail: [allergy-inflammation-nutrition-medications.md](allergy-inflammation-nutrition-medications.md)*

### Sensitization

1. Dendritic cells capture allergen, present on MHC-II to naive T cells
2. In the presence of IL-4, T cells differentiate to Th2 (GATA-3 master transcription factor)
3. Th2 cells secrete IL-4/IL-13, which drive B cell class-switch recombination to IgE
4. IgE binds FcepsilonRI on mast cells with extraordinary affinity (Ka ~10^10 M-1) -- IgE remains bound for the mast cell's lifetime (months to years)

### The Allergic Reaction

**Immediate phase (seconds-minutes):** Allergen cross-links IgE on mast cells -> FcepsilonRI aggregation -> Lyn/Syk kinase cascade -> PLCg -> IP3 -> Ca2+ release -> degranulation releasing:
- Preformed mediators: histamine, tryptase, chymase, heparin, TNF-a
- Newly synthesized lipids: PGD2 (via COX-2), LTC4 (via 5-LOX)
- Cytokines: IL-4, IL-5, IL-6, IL-13

**Late phase (4-24 hours):** Eosinophil recruitment driven by IL-5 + eotaxins (CCL11/24/26). Eosinophils release granule proteins (MBP, ECP, EDN, EPO) that directly damage epithelium, creating the tissue destruction that sustains symptoms.

### Chronic Allergic Inflammation

Weeks of allergic inflammation create structural changes: subepithelial fibrosis (TGF-b -> fibroblast activation), goblet cell hyperplasia (IL-13 -> STAT6 -> SPDEF), basement membrane thickening, and tight junction disruption (IL-4/IL-13 upregulate pore-forming claudin-2, downregulate sealing claudin-1, displace occludin and ZO-1). These changes take 4-8 weeks of inflammation-free conditions to reverse.

### The Nutrient Cost of Allergy Season

Sustained allergic inflammation creates compounding nutrient debt through all the mechanisms described in Section 4, plus:
- **DAO cofactor depletion**: histamine metabolism consumes B6, vitamin C, and copper; after weeks, DAO activity drops 30-50% from cofactor limitation alone
- **Eosinophil peroxidase** generates hypobromous acid, consuming glutathione, selenium, and vitamin E/C
- **Tryptophan steal via IDO1** depletes serotonin and melatonin
- **Methylation cycle strain**: immune cell proliferation requires methylation of ~28 million CpG sites per cell division; histamine clearance by HNMT consumes SAM

## 6. Medication Effects: What They Fix and What They Cost

*Full detail: [allergy-inflammation-nutrition-medications.md](allergy-inflammation-nutrition-medications.md)*

### Glucocorticoids: The Paradox

Glucocorticoids are the most effective anti-allergy drugs -- they suppress NF-kB (via transrepression, tethering p65/RelA), induce annexin A1 (blocks PLA2), promote eosinophil apoptosis, and suppress Th2 cytokines. But they simultaneously create nutrient depletions that promote the very inflammation they treat:

| Nutrient Depleted | Mechanism | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Calcium | Reduced intestinal absorption (downregulated TRPV6, calbindin), increased renal excretion | Osteoporosis |
| Magnesium | Renal wasting (TRPM6 downregulation) | Impaired ATP, increased NF-kB, muscle cramps |
| Vitamin D | Accelerated catabolism (CYP24A1 induction), reduced VDR expression | Impaired Treg, barrier function, antimicrobial defense |
| Zinc | Increased urinary excretion, hepatic redistribution | Impaired SOD, thymulin, DAO, wound healing |
| B vitamins | Increased demand from gluconeogenesis-driven transamination | Impaired methylation, neurotransmitter synthesis |
| Vitamin C | Adrenal consumption, increased oxidative stress | Impaired DAO, antioxidant collapse |
| Potassium | Mineralocorticoid-effect renal wasting | Muscle weakness, arrhythmias |

The **glucocorticoid metabolic trap**: GCs cause insulin resistance -> hyperglycemia -> AGE formation -> RAGE activation -> NF-kB -> inflammation, while simultaneously causing muscle catabolism, HPA axis suppression, and bone loss. The drug treating inflammation becomes a source of it.

### Antihistamines

**Second-generation H1 blockers** (cetirizine, fexofenadine, loratadine) are well-tolerated with minimal nutrient cost. They block H1R-mediated vasodilation, pruritus, and vascular permeability but do NOT block: mast cell degranulation itself, late-phase eosinophilic inflammation, leukotrienes, prostaglandins, or tissue remodeling.

**First-generation H1 blockers** (diphenhydramine, hydroxyzine) cross the BBB and have anticholinergic effects: reduced GI motility, gastric acid, and pancreatic secretion -> impaired nutrient absorption.

**H2 blockers** (famotidine) reduce gastric acid, impairing absorption of B12 (requires acid to release from food protein), non-heme iron (requires acid for Fe3+ -> Fe2+ conversion), calcium (acid-dependent dissolution), magnesium, folate, and zinc.

### Montelukast

Blocks CysLT1 receptor, addressing leukotriene-driven bronchoconstriction and eosinophil recruitment. No known nutrient-depleting effects. FDA boxed warning for neuropsychiatric effects (mechanism unclear, possibly related to CysLT1/2 receptor blockade in the CNS).

### The Combined Picture

A patient on oral prednisone + cetirizine + famotidine + elimination diet faces convergent nutrient depletion: calcium hit 4 ways, vitamin D hit 3 ways, magnesium hit 3 ways, zinc hit 3 ways. This creates compounding vicious cycles where the treatment regimen deepens the nutrient deficits that drive the disease.

## 7. The Allergy-to-NARE Transition: When the Driver Changes

*Full detail: [allergy-to-nare-transition.md](allergy-to-nare-transition.md)*

The clinical paradox: pollen season ends, but symptoms continue. The underlying driver has changed from IgE/mast cells to eosinophils/ILC2 cells -- but the symptoms look the same.

### Five Mechanisms Drive This Transition

**1. Epithelial Reprogramming (the central mechanism)**

Allergic inflammation damages epithelial cells, which undergo epigenetic reprogramming to constitutively produce three alarmin cytokines -- TSLP, IL-33, and IL-25 -- even after allergen removal. These alarmins activate ILC2 cells (innate immune cells with no antigen receptor) to produce IL-5 and IL-13, driving eosinophil recruitment without any IgE or allergen. The key: histone modifications at the TSLP promoter make its expression constitutive, and ILC2 cells develop "trained immunity" (epigenetic H3K4me1 modifications) that lowers their activation threshold for months.

The self-sustaining loop:
```
Epithelial damage -> TSLP/IL-33/IL-25 -> ILC2 -> IL-5 -> eosinophils
     ^                                                        |
     +---- MBP/EPO tissue damage <----------------------------+
```

**2. Barrier Damage Creates New Triggers**

Tight junction repair takes 4-8 weeks of inflammation-free conditions. During this window, non-allergenic irritants (cold air, pollution, fragrances, smoke) penetrate damaged epithelium and trigger alarmin release -- no IgE required. This is why NARE patients react to temperature changes, odors, and dry air.

**3. Neurogenic Inflammation Lock-In**

Eosinophil granule protein MBP antagonizes M2 muscarinic autoinhibitory receptors on parasympathetic nerves -> unopposed acetylcholine release -> excessive secretion and vasodilation. NGF-driven nerve remodeling increases sensory nerve density. TRPV1/TRPA1 receptor upregulation persists for weeks after the inflammatory stimulus resolves. Substance P released from sensitized nerves triggers mast cell degranulation without IgE.

**4. Eosinophil Self-Sustaining Loops**

Eosinophils maintain their own recruitment through:
- PGD2/DP2 (CRTH2) positive feedback: eosinophils produce PGD2 -> activates CRTH2 on other eosinophils -> chemotaxis + anti-apoptosis -> more PGD2
- Autocrine IL-5 and GM-CSF survival signals (tissue eosinophils survive >14 days vs hours in blood)
- IL-13 -> eotaxin/CCL11 from fibroblasts -> more eosinophil recruitment

**5. Microbiome Shift**

Allergic inflammation shifts the nasal microbiome: loss of protective commensals (Lactobacillus, Corynebacterium), overgrowth of S. aureus (biofilm formation on damaged epithelium, superantigen-driven polyclonal T cell activation). Microbiome restoration requires intact barrier + reduced inflammation + time, creating a chicken-and-egg problem.

## 8. NARE: The IgE-Independent Eosinophilic Disease

*Full detail: [NARE-non-allergic-rhinitis-eosinophilia-syndrome.md](NARE-non-allergic-rhinitis-eosinophilia-syndrome.md)*

### Definition

NARE (Non-Allergic Rhinitis with Eosinophilia Syndrome) = chronic eosinophilic nasal inflammation with negative skin prick tests, negative serum-specific IgE, and normal total IgE. Prevalence: 15-33% of all chronic rhinitis cases, almost certainly underestimated because nasal cytology is rarely performed.

### Why NARE Is Missed

The defining paradox: eosinophils (classically "allergic" cells) flooding the nasal mucosa despite every allergy test being negative. Clinicians reason: "not allergy, so no eosinophilic inflammation" -- but **eosinophilic inflammation does not require IgE**. The ILC2/alarmin pathway produces identical tissue eosinophilia through purely innate mechanisms.

Important distinction from Local Allergic Rhinitis (LAR/entopy): LAR involves local IgE production within the nasal mucosa without systemic sensitization. NARE has no local IgE either. This matters because LAR may respond to immunotherapy; NARE will not.

### Pathophysiology

The driver is the **epithelial alarmin -> ILC2 -> IL-5/IL-13 -> eosinophil** axis, identical to what is described in the transition document but operating as a primary disease rather than a consequence of prior allergy. Eosinophils then cause tissue damage through four granule proteins:

- **MBP** (Major Basic Protein): pI ~10.9, directly cytotoxic to epithelium, antagonizes M2 muscarinic receptors (causing profuse rhinorrhea), triggers IgE-independent mast cell degranulation
- **ECP**: forms membrane pores, neurotoxic
- **EPO**: generates hypobromous acid from H2O2 + Br-, sustained oxidative stress consuming all antioxidant reserves
- **EDN**: potent RNase (antiviral), activates TLR2 on dendritic cells promoting Th2 polarization

### Progression Risk

NARE can progress through a recognized sequence over years:

```
NARE -> Nasal polyps (CRSwNP) -> Eosinophilic asthma -> N-ERD (Samter's triad)
```

The N-ERD connection involves arachidonic acid shunting: chronic IL-13 upregulates LTC4 synthase, and when COX-1 is inhibited by NSAIDs, residual PGE2 anti-inflammatory braking is lost, causing massive cysteinyl leukotriene surges. This is a pharmacological/biochemical reaction, not IgE-mediated.

### NARE-Specific Nutrient Depletion

NARE nutrient depletion is particularly insidious because:
- It is **chronic and continuous** (no seasonal reprieve)
- It is **unrecognized** (negative allergy tests provide false reassurance)
- It is **untreated** (without diagnosis, no anti-inflammatory intervention)
- It **self-amplifies** through the same vicious cycles as allergic inflammation

### NARE Treatment

- **First-line**: Intranasal corticosteroids (suppress alarmins, ILC2, eosinophils). Unlike in allergic rhinitis, NARE requires **continuous** treatment -- there is no allergen to remove.
- **Adjunctive**: Leukotriene receptor antagonists (montelukast), nasal saline irrigation, capsaicin nasal spray (TRPV1 desensitization for neurogenic component)
- **Severe/refractory**: Anti-IL-5 biologics (mepolizumab, benralizumab), anti-TSLP (tezepelumab -- addresses root cause by blocking upstream alarmin)
- **Nutritional support**: Same framework as post-allergy recovery but *ongoing* rather than time-limited

## 9. Distinguishing Post-Allergy Lingering from NARE

*Full detail: [allergy-to-nare-transition.md](allergy-to-nare-transition.md), [NARE-non-allergic-rhinitis-eosinophilia-syndrome.md](NARE-non-allergic-rhinitis-eosinophilia-syndrome.md)*

| Feature | Post-Allergy Residual | NARE |
|---------|----------------------|------|
| **Timeline** | Resolves in 4-8 weeks | Persists >8 weeks or indefinitely |
| **Trend** | Gradually improving | Stable or worsening |
| **Triggers** | None -- just slow healing | Non-allergenic: cold air, odors, smoke, exercise |
| **Antihistamine response** | Partial/declining benefit | Minimal to no benefit |
| **Nasal steroid response** | Helpful, can be tapered | Helpful, symptoms return on cessation |
| **Nasal cytology** | Eosinophils present but declining | >5-20% eosinophils, stable or rising |
| **Serum eosinophils** | Normal or declining | Often >300/uL |
| **Smell disturbance** | Unusual | Common (eosinophilic damage to olfactory epithelium) |
| **Pattern next year** | Same seasonal pattern | Starts earlier, ends later, or persists between seasons |
| **Supplementation need** | Temporary (8-12 weeks) | Ongoing (continuous eosinophilic depletion) |

### Red Flags for NARE

- Symptoms persist >8 weeks after pollen season ends
- New sensitivity to non-allergenic triggers (temperature, odors)
- Anosmia or hyposmia (reduced smell)
- Antihistamines that worked during season no longer help
- Development of nasal polyps
- Worsening with NSAIDs (aspirin, ibuprofen)

### Diagnostic Approach

1. **Nasal cytology** (key test): nasal scraping, May-Grunwald-Giemsa stain, >5% eosinophils with negative allergen-specific IgE
2. Allergy testing to confirm negative for perennial allergens (dust mite, mold, pet dander)
3. Blood work: CBC with differential, serum ECP, total IgE (usually normal in NARE)
4. Nasal endoscopy: assess for polyps, mucosal edema

## 10. Recovery: What the Body Needs to Heal

*Full detail: [post-allergy-recovery-biochemistry.md](post-allergy-recovery-biochemistry.md)*

### Why Symptoms Persist After Allergen Removal

Six overlapping systems sustain inflammation after the trigger is gone:

1. **Tissue-resident memory Th2 cells** maintain subthreshold IL-4/5/13 production without restimulation
2. **IgE bound to mast cell FcepsilonRI** persists for the mast cell's lifetime (months-years); Ka ~10^10 M-1 means dissociation is negligible
3. **Mucosal remodeling** (fibrosis, goblet cell hyperplasia, basement membrane thickening) requires 4-8 weeks minimum to reverse
4. **Neurogenic inflammation** from TRPV1/TRPA1 sensitization and structural nerve remodeling (NGF-driven)
5. **Nasal microbiome dysbiosis** (S. aureus dominance, commensal loss)
6. **Impaired mucociliary clearance** (MBP is directly ciliotoxic; ciliogenesis takes 2-3 weeks)

### The Recovery Checklist

**Resolve inflammation actively** (it does not stop on its own):
- SPM synthesis from omega-3 fatty acids: RvE1 inhibits eosinophil recruitment, RvD1 promotes efferocytosis, PD1 blocks T cell migration, MaR1 stimulates tissue regeneration
- Omega-3 stores must be present *before* resolution can proceed

**Repair the epithelial barrier**:
- Vitamin A (retinoic acid) directly upregulates ZO-1, occludin, claudin-1 via RAR/RXR nuclear receptors
- Zinc required for tight junction protein expression and all MMP-dependent tissue remodeling
- Glutamine: primary fuel for epithelial cells, upregulates tight junction proteins 1.8-6 fold

**Rebalance mucosal immunity**:
- Vitamin D + zinc synergistically enhance Treg differentiation (FoxP3 upregulation)
- Vitamin D promotes IgA class switching (replacing inappropriate IgE with protective IgA)
- Probiotics: displace S. aureus, restore commensal-mediated immune tolerance

**Stabilize mast cells**:
- Quercetin: inhibits PLCg/Ca2+/PKC cascade, NF-kB, PLA2, 5-LOX, COX-2; more effective than cromolyn for mast cell cytokine suppression
- Vitamin C: inverse exponential relationship with histamine; 2g supplementation reduced histamine 38%
- Magnesium: competes with Ca2+ at mast cell membrane channels

**Recalibrate the nervous system**:
- Magnesium: restores NMDA receptor voltage-dependent block
- B6 (P5P): cofactor for GAD (glutamate -> GABA conversion); deficiency creates excitatory dominance
- DHA: ~40% of neuronal membrane phospholipid fatty acids; restores receptor signaling and TRPV1/TRPA1 desensitization kinetics

**Rebuild energy production**:
- Every Krebs cycle step requires specific B vitamin and mineral cofactors (B1, B2, B3, B5 + Mg + iron)
- ETC complexes require Fe-S clusters, copper, CoQ10, and Mg
- CoQ10 supplementation particularly important (depleted by oxidative stress; also the membrane antioxidant form ubiquinol)

**Restore methylation**:
- The methylation cycle requires B12 (methylcobalamin), folate (5-MTHF), B6 (P5P), B2 (FAD for MTHFR), Mg (for MAT), and Zn (for BHMT)
- Any bottleneck -> elevated homocysteine -> endothelial damage, NF-kB activation, impaired histamine clearance

### Recovery Timeline

| Period | What Happens |
|--------|-------------|
| **Days 1-7** | Acute mediators clear (histamine, prostaglandins half-life: minutes-hours). Mast cells restock granules within 72 hours and remain primed. |
| **Weeks 1-2** | Cytokines begin declining. Serum Mg normalizes (does NOT reflect tissue stores). Plasma B vitamins and vitamin C respond. Blood eosinophils start declining. |
| **Weeks 2-4** | Tight junctions reforming. DAO activity improving. Goblet cell hyperplasia resolving. Hepcidin normalizing -> iron mobilization. Microbiome shift beginning. |
| **Weeks 4-8** | Subepithelial fibrosis resolving. Cilia maturing. Mast cell priming waning. Tregs expanding. Glutathione repleted. Omega-3 index reaching steady state. |
| **Weeks 8-12** | TRPV1/TRPA1 desensitizing. Neurogenic inflammation resolving. Tryptophan metabolism normalizing (serotonin/melatonin restored). HPA axis cortisol rhythm recovering. Full Krebs/ETC function restored. |

### What Delays Recovery

- **Ongoing triggers**: perennial allergens, food cross-reactivity, occupational irritants
- **Insufficient supplementation duration**: stopping at 4 weeks when tissue stores need 8-12 weeks
- **Unaddressed genetic variants**: MTHFR C677T requiring methylated B vitamins
- **Chronic stress**: maintains HPA axis activation -> continued nutrient wasting
- **Poor sleep**: melatonin deficit impairs Tregs; sleep deprivation increases IL-6/CRP
- **Gut dysbiosis**: leaky gut -> LPS -> persistent NF-kB; histamine-producing bacteria; insufficient butyrate
- **Medications**: PPIs reduce DAO activity and impair Mg/B12/Fe/Zn absorption; NSAIDs damage gut barrier and impair SPM synthesis; H2 blockers reduce nutrient-dependent acid digestion

## 11. Practical Supplementation Protocol

*Full detail: [post-allergy-recovery-biochemistry.md](post-allergy-recovery-biochemistry.md)*

### Stacking Order (Introduction Schedule)

The order matters -- foundational nutrients enable downstream nutrient function.

**Week 1 -- Foundation:**

| Nutrient | Form | Dose | Timing | Why First |
|----------|------|------|--------|-----------|
| Magnesium | Glycinate | 300-400 mg elemental/day, split 2-3 doses | Dinner and bedtime | Enables vitamin D metabolism, ATP production, >600 enzymes |
| Vitamin C | Sodium/calcium ascorbate or liposomal | 1,000-2,000 mg/day divided | Throughout day | Immediate histamine support, DAO cofactor, antioxidant |
| Vitamin D3 + K2 | Cholecalciferol + MK-7 | D3: 4,000-5,000 IU; K2: 100-200 mcg | With fat-containing meal, AM/midday | Begin Treg induction (takes weeks); requires Mg |

**Week 2 -- Core Repair:**

| Nutrient | Form | Dose | Timing |
|----------|------|------|--------|
| Omega-3 | Triglyceride (rTG) fish oil, high EPA | EPA+DHA 2,000-3,000 mg/day | With fat-containing meals, split AM/PM |
| B-Complex | Methylated forms: 5-MTHF, methylcobalamin, P5P, riboflavin-5-phosphate, benfotiamine, pantethine | See doses below | Morning with breakfast |
| Probiotics | Multi-strain, 50 billion CFU (LGG + Bifidobacterium) | 50 billion CFU/day | Empty stomach, AM or bedtime |

B-Complex doses: Folate (5-MTHF) 400-800 mcg, B12 (methylcobalamin) 1,000-2,000 mcg sublingual, B6 (P5P) 25-50 mg, B2 (riboflavin-5-phosphate) 25-50 mg, B1 (benfotiamine) 150-300 mg, B5 (pantethine) 250-500 mg.

**Week 3 -- Targeted Support:**

| Nutrient | Form | Dose | Timing | Notes |
|----------|------|------|--------|-------|
| Zinc | Picolinate or bisglycinate | 30-50 mg elemental/day | Empty stomach or low-phytate meal | After 4-6 weeks, reduce to 15-25 mg |
| Copper | Bisglycinate | 1-2 mg/day | Separate from zinc | Required at zinc >30 mg/day |
| NAC | Standard or sustained-release | 600 mg twice daily | Empty stomach | Always co-supplement with glycine 2-3g/day |
| Selenium | Selenomethionine | 200 mcg/day | With food | Do not exceed 400 mcg/day |

**Week 4 -- Specialized Support:**

| Nutrient | Form | Dose | Timing |
|----------|------|------|--------|
| Quercetin | Phytosome or standard + bromelain 125-250 mg | 500-1,000 mg twice daily | Between meals |
| Mg L-threonate | Magtein (if neurogenic symptoms persist) | 144 mg elemental Mg (2,000 mg Magtein) | Evening, add to glycinate |
| Extra B6 (P5P) | If anxiety/insomnia prominent | 25 mg additional | With dinner (GAD/GABA support) |

### Stacking Rationale

1. **Mg first**: vitamin D, zinc, B vitamins, and ATP production all require it
2. **Vitamin C first**: supports DAO, glutathione recycling, iron absorption (later), quercetin absorption (later)
3. **Vitamin D early**: Treg induction takes weeks to manifest
4. **Omega-3 before quercetin**: SPM generation is the primary resolution mechanism; quercetin is adjunctive
5. **NAC/selenium after B-complex**: glutathione synthesis and GPx function benefit from adequate B2 and B6
6. **Iron only if confirmed deficient after inflammation resolves**: check ferritin + CRP; if CRP >3, ferritin is unreliable. Use iron bisglycinate 25-50 mg every other day with vitamin C.

### Duration

- **Post-allergy recovery**: 8-12 weeks at therapeutic doses, then maintenance
- **NARE**: Ongoing supplementation as long as eosinophilic inflammation persists (the disease continuously depletes nutrients)

|---------|---------------------------|
| **Fatigue** | Mitochondrial dysfunction (depleted Fe-S clusters, CoQ10, cardiolipin oxidation), Complex I inhibition by NO, NAD+ depletion, anemia of chronic inflammation, B1/B2/B3 deficiency impairing Krebs cycle |
| **Brain fog** | Quinolinic acid excitotoxicity (NMDA agonist from kynurenine pathway), Mg depletion (loss of NMDA block), microglial IL-1b impairing hippocampal LTP, reduced serotonin (impaired prefrontal cortex 5-HT2A signaling) |

| **Muscle cramps** | Mg depletion -> impaired SERCA Ca2+ pump, loss of Ca2+ channel block, excessive ACh release at neuromuscular junction; vitamin D deficiency -> secondary hyperparathyroidism -> Ca2+/Mg2+ imbalance |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Inflammation resolution is active, not passive.** It requires specific substrates (EPA, DHA) to produce resolvins, protectins, and maresins. Without these, the lipid mediator class switch fails and inflammation becomes chronic.

2. **Nutrient deficiencies and inflammation form interlocking vicious cycles.** No single correction is sufficient -- magnesium deficiency worsens NF-kB and depletes glutathione, which worsens oxidative stress, which depletes more magnesium. Multi-targeted intervention is required.

3. **Functional deficiency is not the same as absolute deficiency.** Iron may be adequate in total body stores but trapped by hepcidin. Zinc may be adequate but sequestered in metallothionein. Standard lab tests (serum ferritin, serum zinc) can be misleading during inflammation.

4. **The allergy-to-NARE transition is a mechanistic shift, not just "lingering allergies."** The driver changes from IgE/mast cells to eosinophils/ILC2 cells operating through epithelial alarmins without any allergen. This requires different treatment (intranasal corticosteroids, not just antihistamines).

5. **Medications can deepen the nutrient deficit.** Glucocorticoids deplete Mg, Ca, Zn, vitamin D, B vitamins. H2 blockers impair absorption of B12, iron, calcium, Mg, folate, zinc. Proactive supplementation is not optional for patients on these drugs.

6. **Recovery takes 8-12 weeks minimum** for tissue remodeling, neurogenic desensitization, and deep tissue nutrient repletion. Stopping supplementation at 4 weeks because symptoms improved leaves underlying deficits unresolved.

7. **NARE requires ongoing management.** Unlike post-allergic recovery (time-limited), NARE continuously depletes nutrients and damages tissue. Supplementation and anti-inflammatory treatment must be maintained.
