## 1. Macronutrients

### 1.1 Protein and Amino Acid Profiles

**Acceptable Macronutrient Distribution Range (AMDR):** 10-35% of total energy intake.

**RDA:** 0.8 g/kg body weight/day for sedentary adults (minimum). Optimal intakes for active adults and older populations range from 1.2-2.0 g/kg/day.
- Men: minimum ~56 g/day
- Women: minimum ~46 g/day

#### Essential Amino Acids (EAAs)

Nine amino acids cannot be synthesized by mammals and must be obtained from diet:

| Amino Acid     | RDA (mg/kg/day) | Key Biochemical Role |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Histidine**  | 8-12            | Precursor to histamine; metal ion chelation; carnosine synthesis |
| **Isoleucine** | 19              | BCAA; gluconeogenesis; energy production in muscle |
| **Leucine**    | 42              | BCAA; primary activator of mTOR/protein synthesis; insulin secretion |
| **Lysine**     | 38              | Collagen cross-linking (hydroxylysine); carnitine synthesis; calcium absorption |
| **Methionine** | 19 (+ cysteine) | SAM (S-adenosylmethionine) precursor; methylation donor; glutathione synthesis via transsulfuration |
| **Phenylalanine** | 33 (+ tyrosine) | Precursor to tyrosine, dopamine, norepinephrine, epinephrine; melanin synthesis |
| **Threonine**  | 20              | Mucin glycoprotein synthesis (gut barrier); serine/glycine metabolism |
| **Tryptophan** | 5               | Serotonin and melatonin precursor; NAD+ synthesis via kynurenine pathway |
| **Valine**     | 24              | BCAA; muscle metabolism; nitrogen transport |

#### Conditionally Essential Amino Acids
During inflammation, illness, or stress, the following become conditionally essential:
- **Glutamine**: primary fuel for enterocytes and immune cells (lymphocytes, macrophages); becomes depleted rapidly during sepsis and critical illness
- **Arginine**: substrate for nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) in immune cells; wound healing; T-cell proliferation
- **Cysteine**: rate-limiting precursor for glutathione (GSH) synthesis
- **Glycine**: anti-inflammatory signaling; conjugation reactions; heme synthesis

### 1.2 Carbohydrates

**AMDR:** 45-65% of total energy intake (4 kcal/g).

#### Fiber
- **Recommended intake:** 25-38 g/day (women 25 g, men 38 g)
- **Soluble fiber** (beta-glucan, pectin, psyllium): fermented by gut microbiota to produce short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) -- butyrate, propionate, acetate -- which regulate Treg cell differentiation, maintain intestinal barrier integrity, and suppress NF-kB-mediated inflammation
- **Insoluble fiber** (cellulose, lignin): promotes intestinal motility, increases stool bulk
- **Prebiotic fibers** (inulin, FOS, GOS): selectively feed beneficial Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus species

#### Glucose
- Brain requires ~120 g glucose/day under normal conditions
- Minimum dietary carbohydrate to prevent ketosis: ~50-100 g/day
- Glucose is the primary fuel for immune cells during acute activation (Warburg effect in macrophages and activated T-cells)

### 1.3 Fats

**AMDR:** 20-35% of total energy intake (9 kcal/g).

#### Omega-3/Omega-6 Ratio and Eicosanoid Biochemistry

**Optimal omega-6:omega-3 ratio:** 1:1 to 4:1 (ancestral diet). The modern Western diet provides approximately **20:1**, heavily favoring omega-6.

**Arachidonic Acid (AA, 20:4n-6) -- Pro-inflammatory Pathway:**
- Released from membrane phospholipids by phospholipase A2 (PLA2)
- **COX-1/COX-2 pathway**: AA --> prostaglandins (PGE2, PGD2, PGF2alpha), thromboxanes (TXA2)
  - PGE2: vasodilation, pain sensitization, fever, IL-6 induction
  - TXA2: platelet aggregation, vasoconstriction
- **5-LOX pathway**: AA --> leukotrienes (LTB4, LTC4, LTD4, LTE4)
  - LTB4: potent neutrophil chemoattractant
  - LTC4/D4/E4: bronchoconstriction, vascular permeability (relevant to asthma/allergy)
- **12/15-LOX pathway**: AA --> lipoxins (LXA4, LXB4) -- these are *pro-resolving* (switch signal from inflammation to resolution)

**EPA (20:5n-3) and DHA (22:6n-3) -- Anti-inflammatory Pathway:**
- Compete with AA for COX and LOX enzymes
- EPA --> 3-series prostaglandins (PGE3, much less inflammatory than PGE2) and 5-series leukotrienes (LTB5, 10-100x less potent than LTB4)
- EPA --> **E-series resolvins** (RvE1, RvE2): actively resolve inflammation, enhance macrophage phagocytosis of apoptotic cells (efferocytosis)
- DHA --> **D-series resolvins** (RvD1, RvD2), **protectins** (PD1/neuroprotectin D1), **maresins** (MaR1): potent pro-resolution mediators
  - PD1: neuroprotective; inhibits neutrophil infiltration
  - MaR1: stimulates tissue regeneration and macrophage polarization toward M2

**Recommended Intakes:**
- Total omega-3: 1.1-1.6 g/day (AI)
- EPA + DHA combined: 250-500 mg/day (general health); up to 2-4 g/day for anti-inflammatory therapeutic doses
- **AA:EPA ratio** in blood: optimal <2.5 (values >11 indicate significant pro-inflammatory state)

**Other Important Fats:**
- **GLA (gamma-linolenic acid, 18:3n-6)**: converted to DGLA, which competes with AA and produces anti-inflammatory PGE1
- **Saturated fat**: limit to <10% of calories; excess activates TLR4 signaling and NF-kB
- **Trans fats**: promote inflammation via IL-6, TNF-alpha elevation; eliminate completely

## 2. Key Minerals and Their Biochemical Roles

### 2.1 Magnesium (Mg2+)

**RDA:** Men 400-420 mg/day; Women 310-320 mg/day. UL: 350 mg/day from supplements only (no UL for dietary magnesium).

#### Enzymatic Cofactor Roles
- Cofactor for **>600 enzymes** and activator for an additional ~200 enzymes
- Essential for all reactions involving **ATP** (ATP exists primarily as Mg-ATP complex; Mg2+ stabilizes the negative charges of the phosphate groups)
- Key enzymes requiring Mg2+:
  - **Hexokinase** (glycolysis step 1)
  - **Phosphofructokinase** (glycolysis rate-limiting step)
  - **Pyruvate kinase** (glycolysis)
  - **DNA/RNA polymerases** (replication and transcription)
  - **Adenylate cyclase** (cAMP signaling)
  - **Glutathione synthetase** (GSH production)
  - **Creatine kinase** (phosphocreatine energy buffer)

#### Ion Channel Roles

**NMDA Receptor:**
- Mg2+ provides a voltage-dependent block of the NMDA receptor calcium channel at resting membrane potential
- Depolarization relieves the Mg2+ block, allowing Ca2+ and Na+ influx
- Acts as a "molecular gatekeeper" preventing excitotoxicity from excessive glutamate signaling
- Low Mg2+ lowers the threshold for NMDA activation --> neuronal hyperexcitability, anxiety, migraine, neuropathic pain

**TRPM6 and TRPM7 Channels:**
- TRPM7 is a bifunctional protein: an ion channel fused to a protein kinase (alpha-kinase domain)
- Constitutively active cation channel regulated by intracellular Mg2+ and Mg-ATP
- Essential for whole-body Mg2+ homeostasis; TRPM7 knockout is embryonically lethal in mammals
- TRPM6 (expressed primarily in kidney and intestine) forms heteromers with TRPM7 for transcellular Mg2+ transport

#### Muscle and Nerve Function
- Mg2+ competes with Ca2+ at the neuromuscular junction, modulating acetylcholine release
- Required for Ca2+-ATPase (SERCA pump) function to re-sequester Ca2+ into sarcoplasmic reticulum
- Deficiency leads to hyperexcitability: muscle cramps, tremor, tetany, cardiac arrhythmias
- Regulates PTH secretion (hypomagnesemia causes functional hypoparathyroidism)

### 2.2 Zinc (Zn2+)

**RDA:** Men 11 mg/day; Women 8 mg/day. UL: 40 mg/day.

#### Immune Function
- Required for **thymulin** (a zinc-dependent thymic hormone essential for T-cell maturation)
- Deficiency impairs: NK cell activity, phagocytosis, oxidative burst, lymphocyte proliferation
- Zinc finger proteins regulate gene transcription of >2,000 transcription factors
- Modulates NF-kB signaling: zinc upregulates A20 (TNFAIP3), a zinc finger protein that inhibits NF-kB
- Intracellular zinc released from metallothionein acts as a second messenger in immune signaling

#### Superoxide Dismutase (SOD) Cofactor
- **Cu/Zn-SOD (SOD1)**: cytoplasmic; catalyzes 2O2.- + 2H+ --> H2O2 + O2
- Zinc provides structural stability to SOD1 while copper is the catalytic center
- **Extracellular SOD (SOD3)** also contains Zn and Cu

#### Wound Healing
- Required for cell proliferation (DNA polymerase, thymidine kinase)
- Matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) are zinc-dependent enzymes essential for tissue remodeling
- Collagenase (MMP-1), gelatinases (MMP-2, MMP-9) all require zinc at catalytic site

#### Taste and Smell
- Gustin (carbonic anhydrase VI) is a zinc-dependent salivary protein essential for taste bud maintenance
- Zinc-dependent olfactory receptors; deficiency causes hypogeusia and hyposmia

#### Other Key Zinc Enzymes
- **Alkaline phosphatase** (bone mineralization, phosphate metabolism)
- **Carbonic anhydrase** (CO2 hydration, acid-base balance)
- **Alcohol dehydrogenase** (ethanol metabolism)
- **Delta-aminolevulinic acid dehydratase** (heme synthesis)
- **Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE)**

### 2.3 Iron (Fe2+/Fe3+)

**RDA:** Men 8 mg/day; Premenopausal women 18 mg/day; Postmenopausal women 8 mg/day. UL: 45 mg/day.

#### Heme Synthesis
- Iron is incorporated into protoporphyrin IX by **ferrochelatase** in mitochondria to form heme
- Heme is the prosthetic group of:
  - **Hemoglobin** (oxygen transport: 4 heme groups per molecule, ~70% of body iron)
  - **Myoglobin** (oxygen storage in muscle)
  - **Cytochrome P450 enzymes** (drug metabolism, steroidogenesis)
  - **Catalase** (H2O2 --> H2O + O2)
  - **Myeloperoxidase** (neutrophil antimicrobial defense: generates HOCl)
  - **Cyclooxygenase (COX)** -- heme-containing enzyme

#### Electron Transport Chain (ETC)
- **Complex I** (NADH dehydrogenase): contains 8 iron-sulfur (Fe-S) clusters
- **Complex II** (succinate dehydrogenase): contains 3 Fe-S clusters + heme b
- **Complex III** (cytochrome bc1): contains cytochrome b (2 heme groups), cytochrome c1 (1 heme), Rieske Fe-S protein
- **Complex IV** (cytochrome c oxidase): contains heme a, heme a3, CuA and CuB centers
- **Cytochrome c**: soluble electron carrier with one heme group

#### Ferritin and Transferrin Regulation
- **Ferritin**: intracellular iron storage protein (up to 4,500 Fe3+ atoms per molecule); serum ferritin reflects body iron stores
- **Transferrin**: plasma iron transport glycoprotein (binds 2 Fe3+ ions); delivers iron to cells via transferrin receptor 1 (TfR1)
- **Iron Regulatory Proteins (IRP1/IRP2)**: post-transcriptional regulation
  - Low iron: IRPs bind iron-responsive elements (IREs) on ferritin mRNA (blocks translation) and TfR1 mRNA (stabilizes it) --> decreased storage, increased uptake
  - High iron: IRPs release IREs --> increased ferritin synthesis, decreased TfR1
- **Hepcidin-ferroportin axis**: master systemic regulator (see Section 5)

### 2.4 Selenium (Se)

**RDA:** 55 mcg/day (both sexes). UL: 400 mcg/day.

#### Selenoproteins (25 in human genome)
Selenium is incorporated as the 21st amino acid **selenocysteine (Sec)** at UGA codons via specialized tRNA.

**Glutathione Peroxidases (GPx1-GPx4, GPx6):**
- Catalyze: 2GSH + H2O2 --> GSSG + 2H2O (and lipid hydroperoxides)
- **GPx1** (cytoplasmic): general H2O2 reduction
- **GPx2** (GI epithelium): protects against dietary oxidants
- **GPx3** (plasma/kidney): extracellular antioxidant
- **GPx4** (phospholipid hydroperoxide GPx): uniquely reduces oxidized phospholipids *within membranes*; critical regulator of **ferroptosis** (iron-dependent cell death)

**Iodothyronine Deiodinases (DIO1, DIO2, DIO3):**
- **DIO1**: converts T4 --> T3 (active thyroid hormone) in liver, kidney
- **DIO2**: converts T4 --> T3 in brain, pituitary, brown adipose tissue (local T3 supply)
- **DIO3**: inactivates T4 --> rT3 and T3 --> T2 (prevents excess thyroid hormone activity)

**Other Key Selenoproteins:**
- **Thioredoxin reductases (TrxR1-3)**: regenerate reduced thioredoxin; essential for DNA synthesis, redox signaling, and regeneration of oxidized ascorbate (vitamin C recycling)
- **Selenoprotein P (SELENOP)**: major selenium transport protein in plasma; contains 10 Sec residues; also has antioxidant activity
- **Selenoprotein W (SELENOW)**: muscle antioxidant
- **15-kDa selenoprotein (Sep15/SELENOF)**: ER quality control of glycoprotein folding

### 2.5 Calcium (Ca2+)

**RDA:** 1,000 mg/day (adults 19-50); 1,200 mg/day (women >50, men >70). UL: 2,500 mg/day.

#### Beyond Bones: Signaling and Regulatory Functions

**Intracellular Calcium Signaling:**
- Cytoplasmic Ca2+ is maintained at ~100 nM (10,000-fold lower than extracellular ~1.2 mM)
- This gradient enables Ca2+ to function as a potent second messenger
- **IP3-mediated release**: G-protein coupled receptor activation --> PLC --> IP3 --> IP3R on ER --> Ca2+ release
- **Store-operated calcium entry (SOCE)**: ER depletion sensed by STIM1 --> activates Orai1 (CRAC channels) on plasma membrane
- **NFAT (Nuclear Factor of Activated T-cells)**: calcineurin (Ca2+/calmodulin-dependent phosphatase) dephosphorylates NFAT --> nuclear translocation --> cytokine gene transcription (critical in T-cell activation)

**Calmodulin (CaM):**
- Ubiquitous Ca2+ sensor protein that binds 4 Ca2+ ions
- Activates:
  - **CaM kinases (CaMKI, CaMKII, CaMKIV)**: gene expression, synaptic plasticity, memory
  - **Calcineurin**: T-cell activation (target of immunosuppressants cyclosporine/tacrolimus)
  - **Myosin light chain kinase (MLCK)**: smooth muscle contraction
  - **eNOS (endothelial nitric oxide synthase)**: vasodilation
  - **Phosphodiesterases (PDE1)**: cAMP/cGMP hydrolysis

**Muscle Contraction:**
- Skeletal muscle: Ca2+ binds troponin C --> conformational change in tropomyosin --> exposes myosin binding sites on actin
- Smooth muscle: Ca2+-calmodulin activates MLCK --> phosphorylates myosin regulatory light chain
- Cardiac muscle: Ca2+-induced Ca2+ release (CICR) via ryanodine receptors (RyR2)

### 2.6 Potassium (K+)

**AI (Adequate Intake):** Men 3,400 mg/day; Women 2,600 mg/day. No established UL.

#### Na+/K+-ATPase
- The single most energy-consuming enzyme in the body (~20-25% of basal ATP consumption)
- Pumps 3 Na+ out and 2 K+ in per ATP hydrolyzed
- Maintains the electrochemical gradient essential for:
  - Nerve impulse conduction
  - Muscle contraction
  - Secondary active transport (glucose, amino acid cotransport with Na+)
  - Cell volume regulation

#### Membrane Potential
- Resting membrane potential (~-70 to -90 mV) is primarily determined by K+ permeability through leak channels (Kir channels)
- The Nernst equation for K+ is the dominant factor: E_K = -61.5 log([K+]in/[K+]out)
- Hypokalemia --> membrane hyperpolarization --> muscle weakness, cardiac arrhythmias (prolonged QT, U waves)
- Hyperkalemia --> depolarization --> cardiac arrest risk (peaked T waves, wide QRS)

#### Acid-Base Balance
- K+ and H+ compete for renal tubular secretion
- In acidosis: H+ enters cells, K+ exits --> hyperkalemia
- In alkalosis: H+ exits cells, K+ enters --> hypokalemia
- Kidney proximal tubule: K+ recycling coupled with H+ secretion and HCO3- reabsorption

### 2.7 Copper (Cu+/Cu2+)

**RDA:** 900 mcg/day. UL: 10,000 mcg/day.

#### Ceruloplasmin
- Major copper-carrying protein in plasma (binds 6 Cu atoms, carries ~70% of plasma copper)
- **Ferroxidase activity**: oxidizes Fe2+ to Fe3+ for loading onto transferrin
- Essential link between copper and iron metabolism: copper deficiency causes secondary iron deficiency (iron cannot be mobilized)

#### Cytochrome c Oxidase (Complex IV)
- Contains CuA (binuclear copper center, electron entry) and CuB (coupled with heme a3, O2 reduction site)
- Terminal enzyme of the mitochondrial electron transport chain
- Catalyzes: 4 cytochrome c (reduced) + O2 + 4H+ --> 4 cytochrome c (oxidized) + 2H2O
- Copper deficiency impairs oxidative phosphorylation

#### Superoxide Dismutase
- **Cu/Zn-SOD (SOD1)**: Cu at catalytic site alternates between Cu2+ and Cu+ during dismutation
- **Extracellular SOD (SOD3)**: also Cu/Zn dependent; protects extracellular matrix

#### Other Copper-Dependent Enzymes
- **Lysyl oxidase**: cross-links collagen and elastin (connective tissue integrity)
- **Dopamine beta-hydroxylase**: converts dopamine --> norepinephrine
- **Tyrosinase**: melanin synthesis
- **Peptidylglycine alpha-amidating monooxygenase (PAM)**: neuropeptide processing
- **Amine oxidases (MAO, DAO)**: neurotransmitter and histamine metabolism

## 3. Key Vitamins

### 3.1 Vitamin D (Cholecalciferol / Calcitriol)

**RDA:** 600 IU (15 mcg)/day (ages 1-70); 800 IU (20 mcg)/day (>70 years). UL: 4,000 IU/day.
**Optimal serum 25(OH)D levels:** Many experts advocate 40-60 ng/mL (100-150 nmol/L), considerably above the "sufficient" threshold of 30 ng/mL.

#### Immune Modulation

**Cathelicidin (LL-37) and Defensins:**
- Vitamin D (as 1,25(OH)2D / calcitriol) binds the **Vitamin D Receptor (VDR)**, a nuclear receptor that heterodimerizes with RXR
- VDR-RXR complex binds vitamin D response elements (VDREs) in the promoter of **CAMP gene** (cathelicidin antimicrobial peptide)
- LL-37 has direct antimicrobial activity (disrupts bacterial membranes), promotes autophagy of intracellular pathogens (e.g., M. tuberculosis), and modulates chemotaxis
- VDR activation also induces **beta-defensin 2 (HBD2)** expression -- broad-spectrum antimicrobial peptide in epithelial cells
- This is a *human-specific* pathway (mice lack the VDRE in their CAMP gene)

**Broader Immune Effects via VDR Signaling:**
- **Macrophages/monocytes**: TLR activation (e.g., TLR2/1 by mycobacterial lipopeptides) upregulates VDR and CYP27B1 (1-alpha-hydroxylase), enabling local conversion of 25(OH)D to 1,25(OH)2D
- **T-cells**: suppresses Th1 (IFN-gamma, IL-2) and Th17 (IL-17) responses; promotes Treg differentiation (IL-10, TGF-beta)
- **Dendritic cells**: inhibits maturation and antigen presentation; promotes tolerogenic phenotype
- **B-cells**: inhibits plasma cell differentiation and immunoglobulin secretion

#### Calcium Homeostasis
- Calcitriol increases intestinal calcium absorption (induces TRPV6, calbindin-D9k, PMCA1b)
- Stimulates osteoclast differentiation (via RANKL on osteoblasts) for bone resorption when Ca2+ is low
- Enhances renal calcium reabsorption in distal tubule

### 3.2 B Vitamins

#### Methylation Cycle (B12, Folate/B9)

**One-Carbon Metabolism -- The Hub:**
1. **Folate (B9)** enters cells and is reduced to **tetrahydrofolate (THF)** by dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR)
2. THF acquires a one-carbon unit from serine (via serine hydroxymethyltransferase, SHMT; requires B6) --> **5,10-methylene-THF**
3. 5,10-methylene-THF is either:
   - Used by **thymidylate synthase** for dTMP synthesis (DNA synthesis)
   - Reduced by **MTHFR** (methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase) to **5-methyl-THF** (the circulating form of folate)
4. **Methionine synthase** (requires **B12** as cofactor, in form of methylcobalamin) transfers the methyl group from 5-methyl-THF to homocysteine --> **methionine** + THF (regenerated)
5. Methionine is activated by ATP to form **S-adenosylmethionine (SAM)** -- the universal methyl donor
6. SAM donates its methyl group to >200 methyltransferases (DNA methyltransferases, histone methyltransferases, COMT, PEMT, etc.) --> **S-adenosylhomocysteine (SAH)** --> homocysteine

**B12 (Cobalamin) -- Dual Cofactor:**
- **Methylcobalamin**: cofactor for methionine synthase (cytoplasmic)
- **Adenosylcobalamin**: cofactor for **methylmalonyl-CoA mutase** (mitochondrial) -- converts methylmalonyl-CoA to succinyl-CoA (propionate metabolism, odd-chain fatty acid catabolism, branched-chain amino acid catabolism --> TCA cycle entry)

**RDA:** B12: 2.4 mcg/day; Folate: 400 mcg DFE/day

#### Energy Metabolism (B1, B2, B3)

**Vitamin B1 (Thiamine) -- RDA: 1.1-1.2 mg/day:**
- **Thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP)** is cofactor for:
  - **Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex** (pyruvate --> acetyl-CoA; glycolysis-to-TCA link)
  - **Alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase** (TCA cycle)
  - **Branched-chain alpha-keto acid dehydrogenase** (BCAA catabolism)
  - **Transketolase** (pentose phosphate pathway -- NADPH and ribose-5-phosphate production)

**Vitamin B2 (Riboflavin) -- RDA: 1.1-1.3 mg/day:**
- Precursor to **FAD** (flavin adenine dinucleotide) and **FMN** (flavin mononucleotide)
- FAD is cofactor for:
  - **Succinate dehydrogenase** (Complex II / TCA cycle)
  - **NADH dehydrogenase** (Complex I) -- contains FMN
  - **Fatty acyl-CoA dehydrogenase** (beta-oxidation)
  - **MTHFR** (links riboflavin to methylation cycle)
  - **Glutathione reductase** (GSSG --> 2 GSH; FAD-dependent)
  - **Xanthine oxidase** (purine catabolism)
  - **Monoamine oxidase** (neurotransmitter catabolism)

**Vitamin B3 (Niacin) -- RDA: 14-16 mg NE/day:**
- Precursor to **NAD+** and **NADP+**
- NAD+ is required for:
  - Glycolysis (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase)
  - TCA cycle (isocitrate DH, alpha-ketoglutarate DH, malate DH)
  - Beta-oxidation
  - **Sirtuins (SIRT1-7)**: NAD+-dependent deacetylases regulating metabolism, inflammation (SIRT1 deacetylates NF-kB p65), aging, DNA repair
  - **PARP enzymes**: NAD+ consumers for DNA repair (high NAD+ demand during inflammation/DNA damage)
  - **CD38**: NAD+ glycohydrolase upregulated during inflammation -- major NAD+ consumer
- NADPH (from NADP+):
  - **NADPH oxidase (NOX2)**: respiratory burst in neutrophils/macrophages
  - **Glutathione reductase**: GSSG recycling
  - **Thioredoxin reductase**: thioredoxin recycling
  - **Fatty acid synthesis** and **cholesterol synthesis**
  - **CYP450 enzyme system**

#### Neurotransmitter Synthesis (B6)

**Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine/PLP) -- RDA: 1.3-1.7 mg/day:**
- Active form: **pyridoxal 5'-phosphate (PLP)** -- cofactor for >140 enzymes
- **Neurotransmitter synthesis:**
  - **Aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase (AADC)**: 5-HTP --> serotonin; L-DOPA --> dopamine
  - **Glutamic acid decarboxylase (GAD)**: glutamate --> GABA (primary inhibitory neurotransmitter)
  - **Histidine decarboxylase**: histidine --> histamine
  - **Cysteine sulfinic acid decarboxylase**: cysteine --> taurine
- **Transsulfuration pathway**: B6 is cofactor for cystathionine beta-synthase (CBS) and cystathionine gamma-lyase (CSE): homocysteine --> cystathionine --> cysteine (for glutathione synthesis)
- **Aminotransferases** (ALT, AST): all require PLP
- **Serine hydroxymethyltransferase (SHMT)**: connects to one-carbon metabolism
- **ALA synthase**: first step of heme synthesis (succinyl-CoA + glycine --> delta-aminolevulinic acid)
- **Glycogen phosphorylase**: glycogenolysis (PLP is a permanent cofactor)
- **Sphingolipid synthesis**: serine palmitoyltransferase (PLP-dependent)

### 3.3 Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid)

**RDA:** Men 90 mg/day; Women 75 mg/day. Smokers: +35 mg/day. UL: 2,000 mg/day.
**Optimal range (debated):** Many researchers advocate 200-500 mg/day for maximal tissue saturation.

#### Collagen Synthesis
- Essential cofactor for **prolyl 4-hydroxylase** and **prolyl 3-hydroxylase**: hydroxylate proline residues in procollagen to hydroxyproline (stabilizes collagen triple helix via hydrogen bonds)
- Cofactor for **lysyl hydroxylase**: hydroxylates lysine residues for subsequent glycosylation and cross-linking
- These are Fe2+/2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenases; vitamin C maintains iron in the reduced (Fe2+) state at the active site
- Deficiency --> scurvy: collagen destabilization, hemorrhaging, tooth loss, wound failure

#### Antioxidant Recycling
- Directly scavenges superoxide (O2.-), hydroxyl radical (OH.), hypochlorous acid (HOCl), and peroxynitrite
- **Regenerates alpha-tocopherol (vitamin E)**: donates electron to tocopheroxyl radical at the membrane-water interface, restoring vitamin E's lipid peroxidation-breaking activity
- Ascorbate itself is recycled by:
  - Glutathione (non-enzymatic)
  - **Thioredoxin reductase** (selenium-dependent -- links Se and vitamin C)
  - **Dehydroascorbate reductase** activity of glutaredoxin

#### Immune Cell Function
- Accumulates in neutrophils and lymphocytes at 50-100x plasma concentration
- Enhances neutrophil chemotaxis, phagocytosis, and oxidative burst (ROS generation)
- Promotes neutrophil apoptosis (after microbial killing) and macrophage clearance of spent neutrophils (efferocytosis) -- essential for inflammation resolution
- Supports lymphocyte proliferation and differentiation
- Cofactor for enzymes in epigenetic regulation: **TET (ten-eleven translocation)** DNA demethylases and **Jumonji-C domain histone demethylases** -- Fe2+/2-oxoglutarate-dependent (similar chemistry to collagen hydroxylases)

### 3.4 Vitamin A (Retinoids)

**RDA:** Men 900 mcg RAE/day; Women 700 mcg RAE/day. UL: 3,000 mcg/day (preformed retinol).

#### Mucosal Immunity
- **Retinoic acid (RA)** -- the active metabolite -- is critical for:
  - Differentiation and maintenance of mucosal epithelial cells (barrier integrity)
  - Goblet cell differentiation and mucin production
  - **IgA class switching**: retinoic acid in gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) induces B-cell IgA production via activation of RA receptors (RARs)
  - Dendritic cells in GALT produce RA (via RALDH2) to imprint gut-homing receptors (alpha4beta7, CCR9) on activated T and B cells
  - Treg induction in the gut: synergizes with TGF-beta to promote FoxP3+ regulatory T-cells
  - Th17/Treg balance: RA favors Treg over Th17 differentiation

#### Retinoid Signaling
- **RAR (retinoic acid receptor)** and **RXR (retinoid X receptor)**: nuclear receptors forming heterodimers that bind RA response elements (RAREs) on DNA
- RXR is the obligate heterodimerization partner for multiple nuclear receptors: VDR, PPAR, LXR, FXR, thyroid receptor
- Regulates >500 genes involved in cell differentiation, proliferation, apoptosis, and immune function

#### Other Roles
- **Rhodopsin (visual cycle)**: 11-cis-retinal is chromophore
- **Growth and differentiation**: essential for embryonic development, hematopoiesis

### 3.5 Vitamin E (Tocopherols and Tocotrienols)

**RDA:** 15 mg/day (22.4 IU) of alpha-tocopherol. UL: 1,000 mg/day.

#### Lipid Peroxidation Protection
- **Alpha-tocopherol** is the primary chain-breaking antioxidant in cell membranes and lipoproteins (especially LDL)
- Mechanism: donates a hydrogen atom from its chromanol hydroxyl group to lipid peroxyl radical (LOO.) --> terminates the propagation chain reaction of lipid peroxidation
- Resulting tocopheroxyl radical is relatively stable and is regenerated by **vitamin C** (and partially by ubiquinol/CoQ10 and glutathione)
- Protects polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs), including DHA and AA, in membrane phospholipids
- **Gamma-tocopherol**: better at scavenging reactive nitrogen species (peroxynitrite, NO2) via its unsubstituted 5-position on the chromanol ring
- Modulates signal transduction: inhibits **protein kinase C (PKC)**, **5-LOX** (leukotriene synthesis), **COX-2** expression
- Enhances immune function: T-cell proliferation, IL-2 production, NK cell activity (particularly in elderly)

## 5. How Requirements Change During Inflammation

### 5.1 The Acute Phase Response (APR) and Nutrient Redistribution

When the immune system is activated by infection, injury, or chronic inflammatory conditions, a coordinated systemic response occurs driven primarily by **IL-1beta, IL-6, and TNF-alpha**:

**Hepatic Acute Phase Protein Changes:**
- **Positive** acute phase proteins (increase): CRP (up to 1000x), ferritin (up to 50x), hepcidin, ceruloplasmin, fibrinogen, complement C3, alpha-1-antitrypsin, SAA
- **Negative** acute phase proteins (decrease): albumin, transferrin, transthyretin (prealbumin), retinol-binding protein (RBP)

This has profound effects on circulating micronutrient levels because key carrier proteins decline:
- **Albumin** decrease --> loss of zinc transport capacity (albumin carries ~70% of circulating zinc)
- **Transferrin** decrease --> reduced iron transport capacity
- **Transthyretin** decrease --> reduced vitamin A (retinol-binding protein binds transthyretin) and thyroxine transport

### 5.2 Quantitative Nutrient Depletion During Major Inflammation

In major inflammation (CRP >80 mg/L), research documents:
- **Zinc**: plasma levels fall ~40%
- **Selenium**: falls ~40%
- **Vitamin A**: falls ~40%
- **Vitamin B6**: falls ~40%
- **Vitamin C**: falls ~70-80% (the most dramatically depleted)
- **Carotenoids**: fall ~80%
- **Iron**: falls due to hepcidin-mediated sequestration (see below)

### 5.3 Nutrient-Specific Mechanisms of Depletion

#### Iron: Hepcidin-Mediated Sequestration
- IL-6 activates the **JAK2-STAT3** pathway in hepatocytes
- STAT3 binds the hepcidin (HAMP) gene promoter --> massive hepcidin upregulation
- Hepcidin binds **ferroportin** (the sole cellular iron exporter) on enterocytes, macrophages, and hepatocytes
- Ferroportin is ubiquitinated, internalized, and degraded
- Result: **hypoferremia of inflammation** -- iron is trapped inside macrophages and hepatocytes, plasma iron drops ~34% within 2 hours of IL-6 infusion
- Biological rationale: iron withholding from extracellular pathogens ("nutritional immunity")
- Consequence: functional iron deficiency despite adequate stores; impaired erythropoiesis --> anemia of chronic disease

#### Zinc: Redistribution and Increased Demand
- IL-6 induces hepatic **ZIP14** (zinc importer) --> zinc sequestration in liver
- Metallothionein synthesis is upregulated (MT-1, MT-2) in response to IL-1, IL-6, glucocorticoids --> binds intracellular zinc
- Zinc is consumed by rapidly proliferating immune cells
- T-cell activation requires substantial zinc (zinc finger transcription factors, thymulin)
- NF-kB activation consumes zinc (A20/TNFAIP3 is zinc-dependent)

#### Vitamin C: Oxidative Consumption
- Activated neutrophils and macrophages generate massive ROS via NADPH oxidase (respiratory burst)
- Vitamin C is consumed directly in scavenging superoxide, HOCl, peroxynitrite
- Plasma ascorbate can drop to **undetectable levels** during sepsis
- Leukocyte ascorbate concentration depletes rapidly as immune cells use it during phagocytosis
- Adrenal glands release stored vitamin C during stress (cortisol synthesis also requires ascorbate-dependent hydroxylases)
- Increased demand for collagen synthesis in wound repair further draws on stores

#### Vitamin D: Increased Catabolism and Consumption
- Immune cell activation consumes 25(OH)D locally: monocytes/macrophages upregulate **CYP27B1** (1-alpha-hydroxylase) to produce local calcitriol
- Calcitriol is rapidly catabolized by **CYP24A1** (24-hydroxylase, also upregulated)
- FGF23 is elevated in inflammation, which reduces 1,25(OH)2D production
- Inflammatory cytokines suppress hepatic 25-hydroxylase (CYP2R1) activity
- Glutathione depletion during inflammation further impairs vitamin D metabolism (GSH is needed to maintain VDR function)

#### Glutathione (GSH): The Master Antioxidant Under Siege
- TNF-alpha, IL-1beta increase ROS production --> massive GSH oxidation (GSH --> GSSG)
- GSH is consumed by GPx enzymes neutralizing H2O2 and lipid hydroperoxides
- **Gamma-glutamylcysteine synthetase** (rate-limiting enzyme for GSH synthesis) cannot keep up with demand
- Cysteine (the rate-limiting substrate) becomes depleted
- Result: oxidative stress spiral -- depleted GSH --> more oxidative damage --> more inflammation --> more GSH consumption

#### B Vitamins: Increased Metabolic Demand
- **B6 (PLP)**: consumed in transamination reactions upregulated during immune activation; catabolized faster with elevated alkaline phosphatase (positive APR enzyme)
- **B12/Folate**: increased demand for nucleotide synthesis in rapidly proliferating lymphocytes
- **B3 (NAD+)**: **CD38** (NAD+ glycohydrolase) is massively upregulated on activated immune cells, consuming NAD+; PARP activation for DNA repair further depletes NAD+ stores
- **B1 (Thiamine)**: hypermetabolic state increases flux through PDH and TCA cycle; fever increases basal metabolic rate ~13% per degree C

#### Protein and Amino Acids: Catabolic Storm
- TNF-alpha and IL-1 activate muscle proteolysis via the **ubiquitin-proteasome pathway** (MuRF1, atrogin-1/MAFbx)
- Released amino acids are redirected to:
  - Hepatic acute phase protein synthesis
  - Gluconeogenesis (cortisol-driven)
  - Immune cell proliferation
- **Glutamine** demand increases 2-5x: fuel for lymphocytes, neutrophils, and enterocytes; substrate for nucleotide biosynthesis; precursor for glutathione
- **Arginine** is consumed by iNOS (producing NO for pathogen killing) and by arginase (produces ornithine for polyamine synthesis/cell proliferation)
- Protein requirements may increase from 0.8 g/kg to **1.5-2.5 g/kg/day** during acute illness

#### Selenium: Selenoprotein Demand
- GPx enzymes work overtime to neutralize the oxidative burst
- TrxR enzymes needed for thioredoxin cycling (essential for lymphocyte proliferation and DNA synthesis)
- Selenoprotein P drops as a negative acute phase reactant
- Selenium supplementation during sepsis has been studied with doses up to 1,000 mcg/day (IV)

### 5.4 Summary: Inflammation Nutrient Multiplier

| Nutrient | Healthy RDA | Estimated Need During Inflammation | Mechanism |
|----------|-------------|-------------------------------------|-----------|
| Vitamin C | 75-90 mg | 1-3 g (or more in acute illness) | ROS scavenging, immune cell consumption |
| Zinc | 8-11 mg | 25-45 mg (short-term) | Hepatic sequestration, immune cell demand |
| Vitamin D | 600-800 IU | 2,000-5,000 IU | Increased CYP27B1 catabolism, FGF23 elevation |
| Selenium | 55 mcg | 200-400 mcg | GPx/TrxR demand for ROS neutralization |
| Iron | 8-18 mg | Paradoxical: do NOT supplement in acute infection | Hepcidin blocks absorption; free iron feeds pathogens |
| Protein | 0.8 g/kg | 1.5-2.5 g/kg | Muscle catabolism, APR synthesis, immune proliferation |
| Glutamine | -- | 20-30 g/day (in critical illness) | Immune cell fuel, gut barrier, GSH synthesis |
| B vitamins | 1x RDA | 2-5x RDA | Hypermetabolism, NAD+ depletion (CD38), nucleotide demand |
| Magnesium | 400-420 mg | 500-700 mg | ATP turnover increased; Mg2+ lost in stress response |
| Vitamin A | 700-900 mcg | 1,500-3,000 mcg (short-term) | Mucosal barrier repair, RBP decline |

**Critical note on iron during infection:** Unlike other nutrients, iron should generally NOT be supplemented during acute infection. Hepcidin-mediated hypoferremia is an evolved defense mechanism ("nutritional immunity") to deprive extracellular pathogens of iron. Supplementing iron during active infection can worsen outcomes (demonstrated in malaria, tuberculosis, and sepsis studies). Address iron only after inflammation resolves.

## 6. Nutrient Interactions: Synergies and Antagonisms

### 6.1 Synergistic Interactions

| Interaction | Mechanism | Practical Implication |
|-------------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Vitamin C + Iron** | Ascorbate reduces Fe3+ to Fe2+ in the gut lumen, keeping it soluble for DMT1 absorption; chelates iron preventing phytate/tannin binding. Increases non-heme iron absorption 2-6x. | Take iron supplements with vitamin C-rich foods or 200 mg ascorbic acid |
| **Vitamin D + Calcium** | 1,25(OH)2D induces TRPV6 calcium channel, calbindin-D9k (intracellular transport), and PMCA1b (basolateral export) in enterocytes | Ensure adequate vitamin D status for calcium absorption; without vitamin D, only ~10-15% of dietary calcium is absorbed vs. 30-40% with |
| **Vitamin D + Magnesium** | Mg2+ is cofactor for CYP2R1 (25-hydroxylase), CYP27B1 (1-alpha-hydroxylase), and vitamin D binding protein. Mg deficiency impairs vitamin D activation and transport | Magnesium repletion is prerequisite for effective vitamin D supplementation |
| **Vitamin C + Vitamin E** | Ascorbate regenerates alpha-tocopherol from its radical form at membrane-water interface | Combined supplementation more effective than either alone for antioxidant protection |
| **Selenium + Vitamin E** | GPx4 (selenium-dependent) and vitamin E both protect membrane lipids from peroxidation via complementary mechanisms; deficiency of both causes synergistic damage (e.g., Keshan disease exacerbated by low Se + low E) | Address both in oxidative stress conditions |
| **Copper + Iron** | Ceruloplasmin (Cu-dependent ferroxidase) oxidizes Fe2+ to Fe3+ for transferrin loading; hephaestin (Cu-dependent) needed for intestinal iron export | Copper deficiency causes secondary iron deficiency anemia despite adequate iron stores |
| **Vitamin B2 + Folate** | MTHFR (converts 5,10-methylene-THF to 5-methyl-THF) is FAD-dependent; riboflavin deficiency impairs methylation cycle | Riboflavin status affects homocysteine levels and methylation capacity |
| **Vitamin B6 + B12 + Folate** | All three required for homocysteine metabolism: B6 for transsulfuration (CBS), B12 for remethylation (methionine synthase), folate provides methyl group | Deficiency of any one causes hyperhomocysteinemia |
| **Vitamin A + Zinc** | Zinc is required for synthesis of retinol-binding protein (RBP) in liver and for retinol dehydrogenase (converts retinol to retinal) | Zinc deficiency causes secondary vitamin A deficiency even with adequate vitamin A intake |
| **Vitamin K2 + Vitamin D + Calcium** | Vitamin D increases calcium absorption; vitamin K2 activates osteocalcin (bone mineralization) and matrix Gla protein (prevents vascular calcification) | Prevents soft tissue calcification when supplementing calcium + D |

### 6.2 Antagonistic Interactions

| Interaction | Mechanism | Practical Implication |
|-------------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Calcium vs. Iron** | Ca2+ inhibits both heme and non-heme iron absorption, likely at the basolateral transfer step (ferroportin/hephaestin). Can reduce iron absorption by up to 60% at doses >300 mg | Separate calcium and iron supplements by 2-4 hours |
| **Calcium vs. Zinc** | Compete for similar absorption pathways; high calcium reduces zinc absorption | Avoid high-dose calcium with zinc-rich meals |
| **Calcium vs. Magnesium** | Compete for intestinal absorption (share TRPM6/7 channels) and renal reabsorption | Maintain ~2:1 Ca:Mg ratio; avoid very high-dose calcium without magnesium |
| **Zinc vs. Copper** | Zinc induces intestinal metallothionein, which preferentially binds copper and traps it in enterocytes (lost when cells slough off). Chronic zinc >50 mg/day causes copper deficiency | Supplement zinc:copper at ~10-15:1 ratio (e.g., 30 mg Zn with 2 mg Cu) |
| **Iron vs. Zinc** | Compete for DMT1 (divalent metal transporter 1) in intestine when taken together as supplements in ionic form | Separate high-dose iron and zinc supplements; food-based forms have less competition |
| **Zinc vs. Folate** | Some evidence that zinc inhibits folate absorption; folate may also impair zinc absorption via formation of insoluble complexes | Generally minor at dietary levels; relevant at high supplemental doses |
| **High-dose Vitamin C vs. Copper** | Ascorbate reduces Cu2+ to Cu+, which can generate hydroxyl radicals (Fenton-like chemistry) and may impair ceruloplasmin activity at very high doses | Unlikely at doses <2 g/day; theoretical concern at mega-doses |
| **Iron vs. Vitamin E** | Iron (as Fe2+) can oxidize vitamin E and generate free radicals; inorganic iron supplements can impair vitamin E absorption | Separate iron and vitamin E supplements |
| **Calcium vs. Thyroid hormone (levothyroxine)** | Calcium forms insoluble complexes with T4, reducing absorption by 20-25% | Separate by 4 hours |

### 6.3 Key Interaction Ratios to Monitor

| Ratio | Optimal Range | Significance |
|-------|---------------|--------------|
| **Ca:Mg** | 1.5:1 to 2.5:1 | Excess calcium without magnesium increases cardiovascular risk, muscle cramping, and impairs magnesium-dependent enzymes |
| **Zn:Cu** | 10:1 to 15:1 | Higher ratios deplete copper; lower ratios may indicate copper excess (associated with inflammation, oxidative stress) |
| **Omega-6:Omega-3** | 1:1 to 4:1 | Higher ratios promote pro-inflammatory eicosanoid production |
| **AA:EPA** (blood) | <2.5 | Values >11 indicate a highly pro-inflammatory state |
| **Fe:Cu** (serum) | Context-dependent | Adequate copper required for iron utilization; imbalance causes anemia |
| **Na:K** (dietary) | <1:1 | Modern diets are ~3:1 (too much sodium); adequate potassium counters sodium's hypertensive effects via Na/K-ATPase regulation |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Inflammation fundamentally reshapes nutrient biology** -- it is not simply "more of the same." The acute phase response actively redistributes minerals (iron sequestration, zinc hepatic uptake), depletes antioxidants (vitamin C, glutathione), and catabolizes proteins for immune function.

2. **Antioxidant nutrients face the greatest depletion** during inflammation: vitamin C (up to 80% reduction), carotenoids, vitamin E, and glutathione are consumed by the oxidative burst of activated immune cells.

3. **Iron is the exception** to the "supplement more during inflammation" rule. Hepcidin-mediated iron restriction is a deliberate immune defense. Supplementing iron during active infection can be harmful.

4. **Nutrient interactions are not optional considerations** -- they are biochemically mandated. Vitamin D cannot be properly activated without magnesium. Iron cannot be mobilized without copper. The methylation cycle requires the coordinated action of B6, B12, and folate. Vitamin C regenerates vitamin E. Selenium-dependent enzymes recycle vitamin C.

5. **The omega-6/omega-3 ratio** is arguably the most modifiable dietary factor for baseline inflammatory tone. Reducing this ratio from the typical 20:1 to 4:1 or lower fundamentally shifts eicosanoid production from pro-inflammatory prostaglandins and leukotrienes toward anti-inflammatory resolvins, protectins, and maresins.
