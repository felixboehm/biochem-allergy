## 1. Overview

NARE (Non-Allergic Rhinitis with Eosinophilia Syndrome) does not technically "follow" allergy season as a consequence. Rather, allergic rhinitis can **trigger, unmask, or transition into** NARE through several converging biochemical mechanisms. The core event is a **mechanistic shift**: inflammation that was driven by IgE/mast cells becomes sustained by eosinophils and ILC2 cells independently of any allergen.

This creates a clinical paradox: the patient had clear seasonal allergic rhinitis, pollen disappears, but symptoms continue for weeks or months. The underlying driver has changed, but the symptoms look the same.

**The five mechanisms driving this transition:**

```
Allergy Season Active                    Pollen Gone — But:
─────────────────────                    ──────────────────
Allergen → IgE → Mast cells             1. Epithelium reprogrammed → alarmins → ILC2 → IL-5
         → Th2 → IL-5                   2. Barrier damaged → irritants penetrate → alarmin release
         → Eosinophils                  3. Nerves sensitized → neurogenic inflammation
         → Epithelial damage            4. Eosinophils sustain own recruitment (PGD2/DP2 loop)
         → Nerve sensitization          5. Microbiome shifted → loss of anti-inflammatory commensals
                  ↓
         All five persist WITHOUT IgE = NARE
```

## 2. Epithelial Reprogramming and the Alarmin–ILC2 Axis

This is the central mechanism of the allergy-to-NARE transition.

### During Allergy Season

Allergic inflammation damages the nasal epithelium through:
- Eosinophil granule proteins (MBP, ECP) directly toxic to epithelial cells
- Th2 cytokines (IL-4, IL-13) disrupting tight junctions
- Mast cell tryptase activating PAR-2 receptors on epithelial cells
- Oxidative stress from eosinophil peroxidase (EPO)

### The Reprogramming Event

Damaged and stressed epithelial cells undergo a phenotypic shift. They begin constitutively producing three key **alarmin** cytokines:

#### TSLP (Thymic Stromal Lymphopoietin)
- **Trigger**: Epithelial damage, oxidative stress, proteases, mechanical stress — none of which require allergen
- **Mechanism**: NFκB and AP-1 activation in basal epithelial cells → TSLP transcription
- **Persistence**: Epigenetic modifications (histone acetylation at the TSLP promoter) during prolonged allergic inflammation make TSLP expression **constitutive** — it continues after the allergic trigger is removed
- **Downstream**: TSLP binds TSLPR/IL-7Rα on ILC2 cells → STAT5 activation → IL-5/IL-13 production

#### IL-33
- **Storage**: Constitutively expressed and stored in epithelial cell nuclei as a chromatin-associated protein
- **Release trigger**: Cell damage or necrosis releases IL-33 (an "alarmin" — danger signal released by dying cells)
- **Persistence**: During allergy season, repeated epithelial damage causes chronic IL-33 release. Even after allergen removal, ongoing epithelial repair/turnover and residual damage continues releasing IL-33
- **Receptor**: ST2 (IL1RL1) on ILC2 cells, Th2 cells, mast cells → MyD88 → NFκB and MAPK → cytokine production
- **Key point**: IL-33 is the most potent ILC2 activator — even small amounts from residual tissue damage maintain ILC2 activity

#### IL-25 (IL-17E)
- **Source**: Tuft cells (chemosensory epithelial cells) and damaged epithelial cells
- **Receptor**: IL-17RB on ILC2 cells
- **Persistence**: Tuft cell hyperplasia occurs during allergic inflammation and persists for weeks after allergen removal

### The ILC2 Takeover

**ILC2 cells (Group 2 Innate Lymphoid Cells)** are the critical link. They are tissue-resident innate immune cells that produce Th2-type cytokines **without any antigen-specific activation**:

```
                      No allergen needed
                      No IgE needed
                      No T cell needed
                            ↓
Epithelial damage → TSLP/IL-33/IL-25 → ILC2 activation
                                              ↓
                                    IL-5 → Eosinophil survival, recruitment
                                    IL-13 → Goblet cell metaplasia, mucus
                                    IL-9 → Mast cell recruitment (minor)
                                    Amphiregulin → Tissue remodeling
```

**Why this is self-sustaining**: Eosinophils recruited by ILC2-derived IL-5 damage the epithelium → more alarmin release → more ILC2 activation → more eosinophils. The loop runs independently of any external allergen.

### Epigenetic Memory

Recent research shows that ILC2 cells develop **trained immunity** — epigenetic modifications (H3K4me1 at cytokine loci) after initial activation that lower their activation threshold. ILC2 cells that were activated during allergy season respond more vigorously to subsequent alarmin signals, even at lower concentrations. This "memory" can persist for months.

## 3. Barrier Damage Creates New Trigger Sources

### Tight Junction Disruption

During allergy season, IL-4 and IL-13 from Th2 cells and ILC2 cells cause:
- **Claudin-2 upregulation** (pore-forming claudin → increased paracellular permeability)
- **Occludin downregulation** (barrier-forming protein lost)
- **ZO-1 redistribution** from tight junctions to cytoplasm
- **E-cadherin cleavage** by mast cell tryptase and MMP-7

### Post-Allergy Consequence

Tight junction repair takes **4–8 weeks** of inflammation-free conditions. During this window:

- **Non-allergenic irritants** now penetrate: pollution particles (PM2.5, diesel exhaust), cold/dry air, fragrances, cleaning chemicals, cigarette smoke
- These irritants directly contact subepithelial sensory nerves and immune cells
- Epithelial cells respond to physical/chemical irritation by releasing alarmins (TSLP, IL-33) — no IgE required
- **The disease mechanism shifts**: what was allergen-driven becomes irritant-driven

### Practical Implication

This is why NARE patients report symptoms triggered by:
- Temperature changes (cold air)
- Strong odors (perfume, cleaning products)
- Dry air / air conditioning
- Exercise (increased airflow over damaged mucosa)
- Smoke, pollution

None of these are allergens. They are physical/chemical irritants that wouldn't cause symptoms through intact epithelium.

## 4. Neurogenic Inflammation Lock-In

### Nerve Sensitization During Allergy

Eosinophil granule products, particularly **Major Basic Protein (MBP)**, have direct neurotoxic and neuromodulatory effects:

- **MBP antagonizes M2 muscarinic receptors** on parasympathetic nerve endings → loss of autoinhibitory feedback → unopposed ACh release → parasympathetic hyperreactivity (excessive secretion, vasodilation)
- **Nerve Growth Factor (NGF)** is upregulated by eosinophilic inflammation → increases density and sensitivity of sensory nerve fibers in nasal mucosa (neural remodeling)
- **TRPV1 upregulation**: Inflammatory mediators (PGE2, bradykinin, NGF) increase TRPV1 expression on C-fiber nociceptors → lowered activation threshold for temperature and chemical stimuli
- **TRPA1 sensitization**: IL-13 and oxidative stress products (4-HNE) sensitize TRPA1 channels → increased responsiveness to environmental irritants

### The Locked-In State

After allergen removal, the neural changes persist because:

1. **Structural remodeling**: New nerve fiber growth (NGF-driven) takes months to regress
2. **TRPV1/TRPA1 expression**: Receptor upregulation persists for weeks beyond the inflammatory stimulus
3. **Neuropeptide axis activation**: Sensitized nerves release:
   - **Substance P** → NK1 receptor on blood vessels → vasodilation, plasma extravasation (nasal congestion)
   - **CGRP** (calcitonin gene-related peptide) → vasodilation, mast cell degranulation
   - **NKA** (neurokinin A) → glandular secretion
4. **Axon reflex**: Antidromic nerve impulses spread inflammation to adjacent tissue without any central processing

### Clinical Manifestation

This explains the persistent **stuffed nose** even when:
- No allergen is present
- Antihistamines don't help (histamine isn't the primary driver)
- Decongestants provide only temporary relief
- Symptoms worsen with cold air, exercise, or strong odors

## 5. Eosinophil Self-Sustaining Loops

Even without ILC2 activation, eosinophils can maintain their own recruitment through autocrine/paracrine signaling:

### PGD2–DP2/CRTH2 Positive Feedback

```
Eosinophil → PGD2 (via H-PGDS)
                ↓
         DP2/CRTH2 receptor on eosinophils
                ↓
         Chemotaxis of more eosinophils + reduced apoptosis
                ↓
         More PGD2 production
                ↓
         Self-amplifying loop
```

- **DP2/CRTH2** (chemoattractant receptor-homologous molecule expressed on Th2 cells) is highly expressed on eosinophils
- PGD2 binding to DP2 activates Gi → inhibits cAMP → promotes eosinophil survival (blocks apoptosis)
- PGD2 is also a potent eosinophil chemoattractant — directs migration into tissue

### IL-5 Autocrine Survival

- Tissue eosinophils produce their own IL-5 (autocrine survival signal)
- They also produce GM-CSF which further extends their lifespan from hours to weeks
- In tissue, eosinophils can survive **>14 days** (vs. hours in circulation)

### Eotaxin Amplification

- Eosinophil-derived IL-13 stimulates local fibroblasts and epithelial cells to produce **eotaxin/CCL11**
- CCL11 binds CCR3 on eosinophils → chemotaxis → more eosinophils arrive → more IL-13 → more CCL11

## 6. Microbiome Shift Perpetuates Inflammation

### Allergy-Driven Dysbiosis

During allergic rhinitis, the nasal microbiome shifts:
- **Decrease** in protective commensals: *Lactobacillus*, *Corynebacterium*, *Dolosigranulum*
- **Increase** in pathobionts: *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Moraxella catarrhalis*, *Haemophilus influenzae*
- Eosinophil antimicrobial products (ECP, EDN) non-selectively damage bacterial communities

### Post-Allergy Persistence

The dysbiotic microbiome doesn't snap back when pollen disappears:
- **S. aureus biofilms** form on damaged epithelium → resistant to immune clearance
- S. aureus superantigens (SEA, SEB) activate T cells polyclonally → sustained local inflammation
- S. aureus staphylococcal protein A binds VH3+ B cells → can drive local IgE production even without allergen
- **Loss of commensals** means loss of:
  - Short-chain fatty acid production (propionate, butyrate) → reduced Treg induction
  - Competitive exclusion of pathogens
  - Tonic immunoregulatory signaling

### Recovery Requirement

Microbiome restoration requires:
- Intact epithelial barrier (for commensal adherence)
- Reduced inflammation (for commensal survival)
- Time (weeks to months for recolonization)
- This creates a chicken-and-egg problem: barrier repair needs reduced inflammation, which needs microbiome restoration, which needs barrier repair

## 7. The Diagnostic Blind Spot

### Why This Transition Gets Missed

The allergy-to-NARE transition falls into a clinical gap:

1. **Patient narrative**: "I have allergies, the season ended, but my symptoms haven't gone away" → both patient and doctor frame it as "lingering allergy"
2. **Antihistamine persistence**: Doctor may say "keep taking your antihistamine, it'll clear up" → antihistamines don't treat eosinophilic inflammation
3. **Negative re-testing**: If allergy tests are repeated out-of-season, they're still positive (the IgE sensitization is real) → reinforces the "allergy" diagnosis
4. **Nasal cytology not routine**: The definitive test (nasal smear showing eosinophils without allergen exposure) is rarely performed in primary care
5. **Symptom overlap**: Congestion, rhinorrhea, post-nasal drip, fatigue — identical whether IgE-driven or eosinophil-driven

### The Key Insight

**The same nose can have two diseases simultaneously or sequentially.** Allergic rhinitis (IgE-mediated) can coexist with or transition to NARE (eosinophil/ILC2-mediated). The symptoms overlap, but the treatment differs:

- Antihistamines → treat IgE/mast cell component only
- Intranasal corticosteroids → treat eosinophilic component (effective for both AR and NARE)
- If antihistamines alone aren't working post-season → suspect eosinophilic (NARE) component

## 8. Distinguishing Post-Allergy Lingering from NARE

| Feature | Post-Allergy Residual | NARE (Unmasked) |
|---------|----------------------|-----------------|
| **Timeline** | Resolves in 4–8 weeks | Persists >8 weeks or indefinitely |
| **Trend** | Symptoms gradually improving | Symptoms stable or worsening |
| **Triggers** | None — just slow healing | Irritants (cold air, odors, smoke) |
| **Antihistamine response** | Partial/declining benefit | Minimal to no benefit |
| **Nasal steroid response** | Helpful, can be tapered | Helpful, symptoms return on cessation |
| **Nasal cytology** | Eosinophils present but declining | >5–20% eosinophils, stable/rising |
| **Serum eosinophils** | Normal or declining | Often elevated (>300/μL) |
| **Serum ECP** | Declining | Elevated |
| **Pattern next year** | Same seasonal pattern | May start earlier, end later, or persist between seasons |
| **Smell disturbance** | Unusual | Common (eosinophilic damage to olfactory epithelium) |

### Red Flags Suggesting NARE Rather Than Lingering

- Symptoms persist **>8 weeks** after confirmed end of relevant pollen season
- New sensitivity to **non-allergenic triggers** (temperature, odors)
- **Anosmia or hyposmia** (reduced smell — eosinophilic damage to olfactory mucosa)
- Antihistamines that worked during season **no longer help**
- Development of **nasal polyps** (NARE is a precursor to CRSwNP)
- Worsening with **NSAIDs** (aspirin, ibuprofen) — suggests arachidonic acid shunting

## 9. Diagnostic Workup

### Step 1: Nasal Cytology (Key Test)

The single most informative test for distinguishing post-allergy from NARE:

- **Method**: Nasal scraping or blow sample, stained with May-Grünwald-Giemsa or Wright stain
- **NARE diagnosis**: >5% eosinophils (some criteria use >10% or >20%) in nasal cytology with negative allergen-specific IgE for currently relevant allergens
- **What to look for**:
  - Eosinophil percentage and absolute count
  - Mast cells (if present → mixed phenotype, possible local allergic rhinitis)
  - Neutrophils (if dominant → infectious or NARNE — non-allergic rhinitis with neutrophilia)
  - Columnar cell integrity (ciliated cell damage indicates ongoing mucosal injury)

### Step 2: Allergy Testing (Confirm Negative for Current Exposures)

- Skin prick testing or serum-specific IgE for **perennial** allergens (dust mite, mold, pet dander)
- Purpose: rule out that symptoms are from a perennial allergen rather than NARE
- Positive seasonal + negative perennial = supports NARE diagnosis for off-season symptoms

### Step 3: Blood Work

- **Complete blood count with differential**: eosinophil count
- **Serum ECP** (eosinophil cationic protein): marker of eosinophil activation
- **Serum total IgE**: may be normal in NARE (vs. elevated in allergic rhinitis)
- **Serum tryptase**: if elevated, consider mastocytosis or mast cell activation syndrome

### Step 4: Nasal Endoscopy

- Assess for nasal polyps (NARE progression)
- Evaluate mucosal edema, secretion character
- Rule out anatomical contributors (septal deviation, turbinate hypertrophy)

### Step 5: Consider Nasal Allergen Provocation (Research Settings)

- For suspected **local allergic rhinitis** (LAR) — local IgE production without systemic sensitization
- Nasal provocation with specific allergen + measurement of local IgE, tryptase, ECP in nasal lavage

## 10. Clinical Implications

### Treatment Differences

**Post-allergy lingering** (self-resolving):
- Nasal saline irrigation (physical clearance of residual mediators)
- Short course of intranasal corticosteroids (2–4 weeks, then taper)
- Nutritional support for recovery (see: [post-allergy recovery document](post-allergy-recovery-nutrition.md))
- No long-term treatment needed

**NARE (requires ongoing management)**:
- Intranasal corticosteroids as maintenance (fluticasone, mometasone) — first-line
- Leukotriene receptor antagonists (montelukast) — particularly if NSAID sensitivity present
- Nasal saline irrigation — ongoing
- Capsaicin nasal spray — TRPV1 desensitization for neurogenic component
- Nutritional support — ongoing (same nutrient depletion mechanisms as allergic inflammation)
- Monitoring for progression to nasal polyps or aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease

### The Supplementation Paradox

Both conditions benefit from the same nutritional recovery framework, but:
- **Post-allergy**: Supplementation is **temporary** (8–12 weeks to replenish)
- **NARE**: Supplementation is **ongoing** because the eosinophilic inflammation continuously depletes nutrients

### Progression Awareness

NARE can progress through a recognized sequence:

```
NARE → Chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps (CRSwNP)
     → Asthma (eosinophilic)
     → NSAID-exacerbated respiratory disease (N-ERD / Samter's triad)
```

Early identification and treatment of NARE may prevent this progression. This is why distinguishing "my allergies are just lingering" from NARE matters clinically — untreated NARE has a progressive natural history.
